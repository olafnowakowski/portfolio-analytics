# Qualitative vs Quantitative Data Tools

## Overview

There are two main types of data:

- **Qualitative data**
- **Quantitative data**

Each type helps answer different kinds of questions and uses different data collection tools.

---

## Qualitative Data Tools

Qualitative data helps explain **why** something happens. It focuses on descriptions, opinions, experiences, and reasons.

### Common qualitative data tools

- **Focus groups**
- **Social media text analysis**
- **In-person interviews**

### What qualitative data is useful for

Qualitative data helps analysts understand:

- customer opinions
- reasons behind behavior
- preferences
- experiences
- motivations

---

## Quantitative Data Tools

Quantitative data focuses on **numbers** and measurable facts. It helps answer questions like **what**, **how many**, and **how often**.

### Common quantitative data tools

- **Structured interviews**
- **Surveys**
- **Polls**

### What quantitative data is useful for

Quantitative data helps analysts measure:

- attendance
- sales
- profit
- ratings
- frequency
- trends over time

---

# Movie Theater Scenario

In this example, you are a data analyst for a chain of movie theaters. Your manager wants you to track trends in:

- movie attendance over time
- profitability of the concession stand
- evening audience preferences

The theater already has **quantitative data** for all three areas.

---

## 1. Movie Attendance Over Time

The first step is to review historical attendance data from the theater’s loyalty and rewards program.

### What the analysis found

- looking at only the last 3 months was not enough
- a **full year** of data gave a better picture
- average attendance was **550 per month**
- during holiday months, attendance rose to **1,600 per month**

### Conclusion

The analyst decides to use the full-year data because it gives more complete insight. The analyst also plans to continue the analysis later after the theater raises evening ticket prices.

### Data type used

- **Quantitative data**

---

## 2. Profitability of the Concession Stand

Profit is measured by subtracting cost from sales revenue.

### What the analysis found

- the concession stand was profitable
- profit margins were very small, at **less than 5%**
- average purchases were **$20 or less**

### Next step

The analyst decides to keep monitoring concession performance and also recommends an **online survey** so customers can comment on the food.

### Why this matters

The survey can provide additional data to help improve the menu and increase profits.

### Data types used

- existing sales and profit data = **quantitative**
- customer comments from the survey = **qualitative**

---

## 3. Evening Audience Preferences

The historical data shows which evening showtimes are most popular.

### What the analysis found

- **7:30 PM** had the highest attendance
- **7:15 PM** was next
- **9:00 PM** followed
- **8:00 PM** had lower attendance

### Possible change

The analyst thinks replacing the **8:00 PM** showtime with **8:30 PM** may attract more movie-goers, but more data is needed before making that recommendation.

### Next step

The analyst includes a question in the online survey to understand audience preferences.

### Data types used

- showtime attendance = **quantitative**
- customer preference explanations = **qualitative**

---

## Qualitative Survey Questions

Since the theater also plans to raise ticket prices for evening shows, the analyst includes questions to better understand customer opinions and price sensitivity.

### Example survey questions

- **What went into your decision to see a movie in our theater today?**  
  *(movie attendance)*

- **What do you think about the quality and value of your purchases at the concession stand?**  
  *(concession stand profitability)*

- **Which showtime do you prefer, 8:00 PM or 8:30 PM, and why do you prefer that time?**  
  *(evening audience preferences)*

- **Under what circumstances would you choose a matinee over a nighttime showing?**  
  *(ticket price increase)*

These questions produce **qualitative data** because they explain customer reasons, opinions, and preferences.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Data analysts usually work with **both quantitative and qualitative data**.
- **Quantitative data** gives the **what**:
  - how many people attended
  - how much profit was made
  - which showtime had the most customers

- **Qualitative data** gives the **why**:
  - why customers chose that theater
  - why they liked or disliked concession items
  - why they prefer one showtime over another
  - why ticket price changes may affect their choices

Using both types of data gives a fuller understanding of customer behavior and helps businesses make better decisions.

---

## Why Both Types Are Important

If the analyst only looked at quantitative data, they would know:

- attendance numbers
- profit margins
- popular showtimes

But they would **not know why** customers made those choices.

With qualitative data, the theater could learn things like:

- customers like the reclining chairs
- the theater offers a drink or snack they prefer
- a later showtime works better after dinner
- families choose matinees to save money

This extra context helps managers make smarter business decisions.

## Quick Comparison Table

| Data Type | Main Purpose | Example Tools | Example in Theater Scenario |
|---|---|---|---|
| Qualitative | Explains why something happens | Focus groups, social media text analysis, in-person interviews | Why customers prefer 8:30 PM, why they choose matinees |
| Quantitative | Measures what, how many, or how often | Structured interviews, surveys, polls | Attendance numbers, concession profit, popular showtimes |

---

## Short Summary

Quantitative data measures trends like attendance, profit, and showtime popularity. Qualitative data explains the reasons behind those numbers, such as customer preferences and opinions. In business analysis, both types of data are important because together they provide a more complete picture.
