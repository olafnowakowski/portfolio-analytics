# Data and Decision-Making Notes

## What We Already Know About Data

- **Data** is a collection of facts.
- **Data analysis** helps reveal patterns and insights.
- **Data analysis** supports better, more informed decisions.

---

## Data in Decision-Making

People and organizations use data to make decisions every day.

### Simple example
When you search **"restaurants near me"** and sort by rating, you are using data to help make a decision.

---

## Two Ways Businesses Use Data

### 1. Data-driven decision-making
This means making decisions based mainly on data analysis and evidence.

### 2. Data-inspired decision-making
This means exploring different data sources, looking for what they have in common, and using those insights to guide decisions.

---

## Real-World Examples

### Google data centers
Google used years of data and artificial intelligence to improve cooling systems in data centers. This helped reduce the energy used for cooling by **more than 40%**.

### Hiring and onboarding at Google
Google’s People Operations team used data from:

- job applications
- interviews
- onboarding processes

This helped them:

- identify strong applicants who might have been missed
- decide the ideal number of interviews
- improve onboarding agendas for new employees

---

## What Is an Algorithm?

An **algorithm** is a process or set of rules followed to complete a specific task.

In the hiring example, an algorithm helped review applicants more effectively.

---

## Why Data Alone Is Not Enough

Having a lot of data is not enough. Data only becomes useful when it is interpreted and given meaning.

### Important idea
- **Data** = raw facts
- **Information** = data that has been organized and interpreted
- **Knowledge** = information that has been understood and applied

---

## From Data to Information to Knowledge

### Data
Michael Phelps finished a race in **1 minute, 54 seconds**.

By itself, this is just a fact.

### Information
When we compare his time to other swimmers in the same race, we learn that he came in **first place** and won the **gold medal**.

Now the data has context and meaning.

### Knowledge
When we understand and apply that information, we can say:

**Michael Phelps is a very fast swimmer.**

This is knowledge because the information has been interpreted and understood.

---

## Why Context Matters

Context gives data meaning.

In the swimming example, we also needed to know that the race was an **Olympic final**. Without that context, the time alone would not tell us that it was a gold-medal performance.

---

## Limitations of Data Analytics

Data analytics is powerful, but it also has limits.

Some common challenges include:

- not having access to all the data needed
- data being measured differently across systems or programs
- difficulty finding complete or consistent examples

These limits can make analysis harder and affect decision-making.

---

## Key Takeaway

Data is a powerful tool for decision-making, but raw data alone is not enough. Analysts must organize, compare, and interpret data to turn it into information and knowledge. This is what allows businesses to solve problems, improve processes, and make better decisions.

---

## Quick Summary

- Data is a collection of facts.
- Data analysis reveals patterns and insights.
- Businesses use data to make decisions.
- Decisions can be **data-driven** or **data-inspired**.
- Data becomes useful when it is turned into **information** and then **knowledge**.
- Context is necessary to understand what data means.
- Data analytics has limitations, such as missing or inconsistent data.
