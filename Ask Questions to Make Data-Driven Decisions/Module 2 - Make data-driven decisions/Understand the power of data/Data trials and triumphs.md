# Data Trials and Triumphs

## Introduction

A data analytics professional helps provide the data needed to support important decisions. They also present their analysis in a way that helps business leaders make the best possible choices.

Both **data-driven** and **data-inspired** approaches are based on the idea that data is valuable in decision-making. Data does not make decisions on its own, but it can improve the quality of decisions.

---

## Data-Driven Decisions

**Data-driven decision-making** means using facts and available data to guide business strategy.

### Main idea
The decision is based mainly on the data collected and analyzed.

### Benefits
- can improve decision-making when the data is strong
- helps reduce guesswork
- supports evidence-based choices

### Limitations
This approach depends on the **quality** and **quantity** of available data.

### Risks of relying only on data-driven decisions
- overreliance on historical data
- ignoring qualitative insights
- bias in data collection or analysis
- incomplete data leading to poor decisions

### Example: A/B testing
A website that sells widgets wants to know if a new layout will increase sales.

- half the visitors see the old website
- half see the new website
- the analyst compares visitor data and widget sales after two weeks

If the new version leads to more sales, the business can confidently adopt the new design.

---

## Data-Inspired Decisions

**Data-inspired decision-making** uses data, but also includes broader insights such as:

- comparisons to related situations
- people’s experiences
- feelings and opinions
- qualitative information that may be hard to measure

### Main idea
Data is still important, but it is not the only factor used in the decision.

### Benefit
This approach can avoid some of the weaknesses of relying only on numerical data.

### Example: Customer support center
A support center collects:

- customer satisfaction scores from 1 to 10
- written descriptions of customer experiences

The manager also interviews support staff to understand what is happening behind the scores. Then the manager uses both the numbers and the feedback to decide how to improve customer experience.

---

## Example of a Data Analysis Triumph

### PepsiCo
PepsiCo improved its use of data by:

- hiring analytical talent
- building cross-functional workflows
- creating processes for data-based decisions
- investing in technology platforms
- bringing data into a central cloud-based hub

### Why this worked
When PepsiCo combined internal and external data sources, it gained a more complete understanding of customers and their behavior.

### Key lesson
This is an example of a **data-inspired decision**, because the company used multiple sources of data and broader insights to improve the customer experience.

---

## Data Analysis Failures

Good data is important, but it is not enough by itself. Analysts must also interpret the data correctly.

### Important note
It is possible to have good data and still make the wrong decision if the data is misunderstood or incomplete.

---

## Failure Example 1: New Coke

In 1985, Coca-Cola launched **New Coke** after taste tests with 200,000 people suggested that people preferred it over Pepsi.

### What happened
- Coca-Cola removed the original formula
- New Coke was unpopular
- the company lost tens of millions of dollars

### What went wrong
The data was not necessarily wrong, but it was **incomplete**. The company did not consider how emotionally attached customers were to the original Coke.

### Key lesson
A decision based only on incomplete data can fail, even if the data seems strong at first.

---

## Failure Example 2: Mars Climate Orbiter

In 1999, NASA lost the **Mars Climate Orbiter**, worth $125 million.

### What happened
Two teams used different measurement systems:

- one team used **newtons**
- the other team used **pounds**

The software treated the data as if both systems were the same, which caused the spacecraft to fail.

### What went wrong
- poor collaboration
- poor communication
- incorrect assumptions in data interpretation
- no proper unit conversion during data preparation

### Key lesson
Even when the data itself is accurate, wrong assumptions and poor preparation can lead to major failure.

---

## Small Data vs Incomplete Data

There is a difference between:

- **small amounts of accurate data**
- **incomplete data**

### Important distinction
- a **small test** can still lead to a good decision if the data is accurate and relevant
- **incomplete data** is dangerous because it leaves out important information

---

## Key Takeaways

- Data helps improve decisions, but it does not make decisions by itself.
- **Data-driven decisions** rely mostly on facts and measurable evidence.
- **Data-inspired decisions** combine data with qualitative insights, experiences, and broader context.
- Strong analysis requires **complete, correct, and relevant data**.
- Even accurate data can lead to poor results if it is interpreted incorrectly.
- Communication, collaboration, and context are essential in data analysis.
- As a data analyst, it is important to use both **logic** and **creativity** when interpreting data.

## Final Reminder

As a data analyst, you may not need to choose between being fully data-driven or data-inspired in every project. What matters most is using data carefully, asking good questions, exploring different possibilities, and interpreting results accurately.
