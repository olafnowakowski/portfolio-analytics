# Data vs Metrics Notes

## Introduction

Data helps organizations make better decisions, but raw data by itself is not always useful. To make data meaningful, analysts often organize it into **metrics**.

Metrics help turn raw data into information that can be measured, compared, and used in decision-making.

---

## What Is Data?

**Data** is a collection of raw facts.

By itself, data may include many details, but it can be difficult to understand without structure or purpose.

### Example of raw data
A sales dataset might include:

- salesperson names
- number of sales
- product prices
- order dates
- revenue amounts

This is useful information, but it needs to be organized to answer a specific question.

---

## What Is a Metric?

A **metric** is a single, quantifiable type of data that is used for measurement.

### Key idea
A metric focuses on one measurable aspect of the data.

### Example
If a company wants to compare sales performance, one useful metric might be:

- **revenue by salesperson**

This helps the company clearly see which salesperson generated the most revenue.

---

## Difference Between Data and Metrics

### Data
- raw facts
- unorganized details
- may not be meaningful on their own

### Metrics
- organized, measurable values
- focused on one type of information
- help answer specific business questions

### Simple way to think about it
- **Data** = all the raw details
- **Metrics** = the specific measurements created from that data

---

## How Metrics Make Data Useful

Metrics help analysts:

- organize raw data
- focus on important measurements
- compare results
- evaluate progress toward goals
- support better decision-making

Without metrics, data can be too broad or confusing.

---

## Metrics Often Use Simple Math

Many metrics are based on formulas.

### Example: Revenue
**Revenue = number of sales × sales price**

This metric helps measure how much money sales generated.

---

## Why Choosing the Right Metric Matters

Choosing the right metric is important because different questions need different measurements.

### Example
If the goal is to compare employee performance in sales, a useful metric could be:

- revenue by salesperson

But if the goal is to measure customer loyalty, a better metric might be:

- customer retention rate

The right metric depends on the business problem being solved.

---

## Example: ROI

One common business metric is **ROI**, which stands for **Return on Investment**.

### What ROI measures
ROI helps a business understand how well an investment is performing.

### ROI uses two metrics
- **net profit**
- **cost of investment**

By comparing these two values, a company can evaluate whether an investment was worth it.

### Why ROI matters
ROI helps businesses decide:

- which investments are successful
- which investments to prioritize in the future

---

## Example: Customer Retention Rate

Another common metric is **customer retention rate**.

### What it measures
Customer retention rate shows how well a company keeps its customers over time.

### How it works
The company compares:

- the number of customers at the beginning of a period
- the number of customers at the end of that period

### Why it matters
This helps a business understand whether its marketing strategies are effective and whether it needs to improve customer loyalty efforts.

---

## Metric Goals

A **metric goal** is a measurable goal set by a company and evaluated using metrics.

### Examples of metric goals
- reaching a target number of monthly sales
- achieving a specific percentage of repeat customers
- improving profit margins
- increasing return on investment

### Why metric goals matter
Metric goals help businesses track performance and measure success in a clear way.

---

## Why Metrics Matter in Business

Different industries use different metrics, but they all use them for the same reason:

- to measure progress
- to understand performance
- to support decisions
- to work toward specific goals

Metrics help reveal the story hidden inside the data.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Data** is a collection of raw facts.
- A **metric** is a single, measurable type of data used for analysis.
- Metrics help turn raw data into useful information.
- Many metrics use formulas, such as:
  - **Revenue = sales × sales price**
  - **ROI = net profit compared to cost of investment**
- Choosing the right metric is important because it determines what insight you can gain.
- **Metric goals** are measurable business goals evaluated through metrics.

---

## Short Summary

Data is the raw information a business collects, while metrics are the specific measurements created from that data. Metrics make it easier to analyze performance, compare results, and track progress toward goals. By choosing the right metrics, businesses can turn data into useful information and make better decisions.
