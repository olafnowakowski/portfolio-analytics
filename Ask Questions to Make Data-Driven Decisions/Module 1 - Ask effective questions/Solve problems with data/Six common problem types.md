# Six Common Problem Types in Data Analytics

Data analytics is not just about putting data into tools and getting results. It is mainly about **solving problems**. To solve a problem well, the first step is always to **understand the problem clearly**. Once you understand it, you can decide:

- what data you need
- how to organize or clean the data
- how to analyze it
- how the results will be used

Data analysts often work with **six common types of problems**:

1. **Making predictions**
2. **Categorizing things**
3. **Spotting something unusual**
4. **Identifying themes**
5. **Discovering connections**
6. **Finding patterns**

## 1. Making Predictions

This means using data to make an informed guess about what may happen in the future.

**Example:**  
A company wants to know which type of advertising will bring in the most new customers. By looking at past data such as ad location, media type, and customer response, analysts can predict which advertising method may work best.

**Important note:**  
Predictions do not guarantee future results, but they help businesses make smarter decisions.

## 2. Categorizing Things

This means sorting data into groups based on shared features.

**Example:**  
A company wants to improve customer satisfaction. Analysts can group customer service calls by keywords, issue type, or satisfaction scores. This can help identify which service methods or employees perform best.

**Important note:**  
Categorizing makes data easier to understand and compare.

## 3. Spotting Something Unusual

This means finding data that is different from the normal trend.

**Example:**  
A smart watch company wants its product to detect unusual health signs. Analysts study health data to help create algorithms that trigger alerts when something does not look normal.

**Important note:**  
This is often used for detecting risks, errors, or sudden changes.

## 4. Identifying Themes

This means finding broader ideas or main messages in data.

**Example:**  
UX designers may ask analysts to study user feedback or interaction data. Analysts might identify themes such as user needs, frustrations, or preferences to help improve product features.

**Important note:**  
Themes help researchers understand the bigger picture.

### Difference Between Categorizing Things and Identifying Themes

- **Categorizing things** = putting items into specific groups
- **Identifying themes** = combining those groups into broader ideas

**Example:**

- **Category:** login issues, slow loading, confusing menu
- **Theme:** poor user experience

## 5. Discovering Connections

This means finding relationships between different factors or events.

**Example:**  
A logistics company wants to improve on-time delivery. By studying wait times at shipping hubs, analysts may discover that delays at one location are connected to late deliveries in other places.

**Important note:**  
Discovering connections helps find the real cause of a problem.

## 6. Finding Patterns

This means looking at past data to identify trends that happen again and again.

**Example:**  
A company wants to reduce machine failures. Analysts review maintenance records and notice that machines often fail when maintenance is delayed by more than 15 days.

**Important note:**  
Patterns help organizations prevent problems and plan ahead.

## Key Takeaway

As a data analyst, you need to recognize what kind of problem you are solving before you begin your analysis. Understanding the problem type helps you choose the right data, the right method, and the right solution. This leads to better decisions and better outcomes for stakeholders.
