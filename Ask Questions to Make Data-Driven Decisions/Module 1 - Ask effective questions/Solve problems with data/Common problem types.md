# Question
The finding patterns problem type could involve which of the following actions?
- Using historical data to create a report that shows when batteries have been replaced on critical equipment 
> The finding patterns problem type could involve using historical data to create a report that shows when batteries on critical equipment have been replaced. 
# 1. Making Predictions
Meaning:
- Using current and historical data to make an informed guess about what may happen in the future.

Example:
- A hospital uses patient health data, age, and risk factors to predict future health problems for chronically ill patients.

Good note:
- Prediction does not guarantee the future.
- It helps organizations prepare early and make better decisions.
- Common in healthcare, sales forecasting, weather, and finance.

# 2. Categorizing Things
Meaning:
- Grouping data into categories or clusters based on shared features.

Example:
A manufacturer groups employees by strengths, such as:
- best at engineering
- best at repair and maintenance
- best at assembly

Good note:
- Categorizing helps organize large amounts of information.
- It makes comparisons easier.
- Often used in customer segmentation, employee evaluation, and product classification.

# 3. Spotting Something Unusual
Meaning:
- Finding data that is different from the normal pattern or trend.

Example:
- A school notices a sudden 30% increase in student enrollment. A data analyst investigates and finds that new apartment complexes were built nearby.

Good note:
- This is also called detecting an outlier or anomaly.
- Unusual data can point to a problem, an opportunity, or a major change.
- Useful in fraud detection, system monitoring, and public services.
# 4. Identifying Themes

Meaning:
- Looking at categories and combining them into broader ideas or concepts.

Example:
- A manufacturer first groups workers by tasks, then combines those groups into bigger themes such as:
- high productivity
- low productivity

Good note:
- Themes help analysts move from details to the bigger picture.
- This is useful for understanding overall performance or behavior.
- Common in survey analysis, interview analysis, and performance reviews.

# 5. Discovering Connections
Meaning:
- Finding relationships between different people, departments, companies, or events.

Example:
- A scooter company has wheel supply problems. After sharing data, they discover the wheel supplier has rubber shortages because the rubber supplier also has material issues.

Good note:
- Connections help reveal the root cause of a problem.
- This is very useful in supply chains, business operations, and stakeholder analysis.
- It often requires collaboration and data sharing.

# 6. Finding Patterns
Meaning:
- Using historical data to identify repeated behaviors or trends.

Example:
An ecommerce company finds that:
- people buy more canned goods before hurricanes
- people buy fewer winter accessories during warm months

Good note:
- Patterns help businesses plan ahead.
- They can improve inventory, staffing, marketing, and customer service.
- Historical trends often guide future strategy.

# Extra notes to remember
- These are common data analysis problem types, not the six phases of data analysis.
- The six phases of data analysis are different:
  - Ask, Prepare, Process, Analyze, Share, Act
- A data analyst must first understand what kind of problem they are solving before choosing tools or methods.
- One real-world problem may include more than one type.
  - For example, a business may find patterns, make predictions, and spot unusual changes all in the same project.
- Strong problem-solving in data analysis means:
  - asking clear questions
  - using the right data
  - looking for meaning, not just numbers
  - giving useful recommendations
