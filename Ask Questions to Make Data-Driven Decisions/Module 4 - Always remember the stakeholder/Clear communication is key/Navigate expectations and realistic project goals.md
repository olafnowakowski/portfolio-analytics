# Setting Realistic Expectations with Stakeholders in Data Analysis

## Main Idea
Data analysis projects often have limits.

Sometimes:

- you do not have access to all the data you need
- your data sources do not match well
- your data is messy or unclean

These issues do not only affect the analysis itself. They also affect how you communicate with stakeholders.

Because of this, a data analyst needs to balance:

- what stakeholders want
- what is actually possible
- the time, data, and resources available

That is why it is important to set **realistic, objective goals** and communicate clearly when problems come up.

---

## Why This Matters
A lot can depend on your analysis.

For example:

- your team may need your report before they can make a decision
- your early analysis may determine how and where more data will be collected later

This means stakeholders need accurate updates so they can plan properly.

---

## Communication and Stakeholder Expectations
You have already learned that it is important to keep stakeholders informed.

For example, you should tell your project manager:

- whether the project is on schedule
- whether you are having problems
- whether something may affect the timeline

This lesson builds on that idea by showing how to communicate when a project faces limitations or unexpected issues.

---

# Example Project: Insurance Company and Minor Car Accidents

## Situation
Imagine you are working on a project for an **insurance company**.

The company wants to:

- identify common causes of **minor car accidents**
- create educational materials
- encourage safer driving

---

## Early Questions the Team Must Answer
At the beginning of the project, you and your team need to answer several important questions:

- What driving habits will be included in the dataset?
- How will the data be collected?
- How long will it take to collect and clean the data before it can be analyzed?

These questions are important because they help the team build a realistic project plan.

---

## Communicate Clearly from the Start
Right away, you should communicate clearly with stakeholders about these early questions.

This helps your team and stakeholders:

- understand the scope of the project
- agree on what the project includes
- create a reasonable timeline

---

## Do Not Overpromise
It can be tempting to tell stakeholders that the work will be done quickly and without problems.

But that creates risk.

If you promise too much too early, you may:

- overwork yourself
- miss deadlines
- disappoint stakeholders
- damage trust

### Important lesson
Setting realistic expectations early is better than making promises you cannot keep.

---

## Why Realistic Timelines Help
When you set a realistic timeline:

- stakeholders know what to expect
- they can plan their own work better
- you can work more productively
- the team avoids confusion and surprise delays

The speaker says that setting expectations early helps them use their time more productively.

---

# Start with a High-Level Schedule
At the beginning of a project, it is a good idea to share a **high-level schedule**.

This schedule should include:

- the major phases of the project
- approximate start dates
- the expected overall timeline

## Example
In this project, you and your team estimate that you will need:

- **three weeks** to complete the analysis
- and provide recommendations

You then communicate that timeline to stakeholders so they can plan accordingly.

---

# Problems Can Appear Later in the Project

Even with a strong plan, issues may come up during the project.

That is why communication must continue throughout the process.

---

## Example Problem: Inconsistent Phone Usage Data
Later in the insurance project, suppose drivers have agreed to share data about **phone usage while driving**.

But then you discover a problem:

- some data sources count **GPS usage**
- some data sources do **not** count GPS usage

This means the data is inconsistent.

## Why this is a problem
This inconsistency may:

- make the data harder to process
- require more cleaning
- delay project milestones

---

## What You Should Do
If this happens, you should:

- let your **project manager** know
- explain the issue clearly
- possibly work out a **new timeline**
- prepare to share that updated timeline with stakeholders

### Important lesson
The earlier you flag a problem, the better.

This gives stakeholders more time to:

- adjust plans
- reset expectations
- make needed changes

---

# Stakeholder Requests Can Also Change the Project

Sometimes the challenge does not come from messy data.  
Sometimes it comes from stakeholders asking for changes.

## Example request
Stakeholders may ask to add new variables such as:

- **car model**
- **car age**

These may seem like useful additions, but they can affect the project.

---

## What You Need to Communicate About a Change Request
If stakeholders want to add new variables, you need to explain:

- how the request changes the model you have built
- whether the change can realistically be added before the deadline
- what obstacles or risks the change creates
- whether the change is worth making at this stage

This allows stakeholders to make an informed decision.

---

## How You Can Help Stakeholders Decide
To support them, you might prepare a report that explains:

- how the request affects the project timeline
- how it changes the model
- the **pros and cons** of making the change

### Why this matters
Your role is not only to do analysis.  
It is also to help stakeholders understand the tradeoffs of their decisions.

---

# The Balance a Data Analyst Must Manage
As a data analyst, you must balance several things at once:

- the needs of stakeholders
- the needs of your team
- what is actually possible
- project limitations
- available resources
- time constraints

You want to help stakeholders achieve their goals, but you also need to be honest about what can and cannot be done.

---

# Why Realism and Objectivity Are Important
Being **realistic** and **objective** helps you:

- set fair timelines
- avoid overpromising
- communicate project limitations clearly
- help stakeholders make better decisions
- build trust in your work

When stakeholders understand the timeline and the challenges, they are more likely to have confidence in your ability to meet project goals.

---

# Key Takeaways
- Data projects often have limitations, such as missing data, messy data, or inconsistent data sources.
- These limitations affect both the analysis and communication with stakeholders.
- A data analyst must balance stakeholder expectations with what is actually possible.
- Realistic, objective goals are essential for project success.
- Early project questions help define the scope, timeline, and data needs.
- It is better to set realistic expectations early than to overpromise and miss deadlines.
- Sharing a high-level schedule helps stakeholders plan.
- Problems should be communicated as early as possible.
- Project managers are important contacts when issues affect timelines or progress.
- Stakeholder requests for project changes may affect the model, deadlines, and resources.
- Analysts should explain the pros, cons, and timeline impact of requested changes.
- Clear communication helps stakeholders understand limits and trust the process.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- Good stakeholder communication is not only about giving updates when things go well. It is especially important when things go wrong.
- Being honest about data limitations is part of being a strong and trustworthy analyst.
- A realistic timeline protects both the project and the people doing the work.
- Stakeholders may ask for changes that sound simple, but those changes can affect the entire project. It is the analyst’s job to explain that clearly.
- The goal is not just to say yes to every request. The goal is to help stakeholders make smart decisions based on what is realistic.

---

# Final Summary
Data analysis projects often face limitations such as missing, inconsistent, or unclean data. Because of this, data analysts must balance stakeholder expectations with what is realistically possible. Clear communication is essential from the beginning of the project through every stage of the work. Analysts should set realistic timelines, avoid overpromising, share high-level schedules, report problems early, and explain how new stakeholder requests may affect deadlines and models. By being objective, realistic, and communicative, a data analyst helps stakeholders plan better and builds trust in the project.
