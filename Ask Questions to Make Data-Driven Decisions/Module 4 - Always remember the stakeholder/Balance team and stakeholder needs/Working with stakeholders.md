# Project Stakeholders in Data Analysis

## Main Idea
A data analysis project should do two things:

1. answer the **business task**
2. create opportunities for **data-driven decision-making**

That is why stakeholders are so important. As a data analyst, you are responsible for understanding and managing stakeholder expectations while keeping the project goals as the main focus.

---

## What Are Stakeholders?
Stakeholders are people who have invested:

- time
- interest
- resources

into the project you are working on.

This group can be broad, and it may change depending on the project.

---

## Three Common Stakeholder Groups
You will often work with these three groups:

- **Executive team**
- **Customer-facing team**
- **Data science team**

Each group has different goals, different needs, and may require different communication styles.

---

# 1. Executive Team

## Who they are
The executive team provides the company’s **strategic and operational leadership**.

They:

- set goals
- develop strategy
- make sure the strategy is carried out effectively

Examples of executive stakeholders include:

- vice presidents
- chief marketing officer (CMO)
- other senior-level professionals

## What they care about
Executive stakeholders think at a **high level**.

They usually want:

- the main findings first
- quick answers to their questions
- information that helps them make decisions

They are usually **less interested in detailed analysis** at the beginning.

## How to communicate with them
Because their time is limited:

- lead with the **main answer**
- present the **headline news first**
- keep detailed information in:
  - an **appendix**
  - or **project documentation**

This way, they can review more detail later if they want.

## Example
You might work with:

- a **VP of HR** on employee absence rates
- a **marketing director** on competitive analysis

Part of your job is balancing:

- the information they need to make good decisions
- with the fact that they have busy schedules

## Role of the Project Manager
You usually will not communicate with executives most often.

Instead, you will often give more regular updates to the **project manager**.

The project manager:

- oversees progress across the team
- helps keep the project moving
- can help get approvals from the executive team
- helps you understand what executive stakeholders need

### Important takeaway
Working closely with your project manager can help you better support executive stakeholders.  
You should not be afraid to ask the project manager for guidance.

---

# 2. Customer-Facing Team

## Who they are
The customer-facing team includes people in the company who interact with:

- customers
- potential customers

They often:

- collect information from customers
- set expectations
- communicate customer feedback to the rest of the organization

## What they care about
These stakeholders usually have their own goals and may come to you with **specific requests**.

But as a data analyst, you must be careful.

## Important rule
You must let **the data tell the story**.

Do **not** let stakeholder opinions or requests push you into trying to prove patterns that are not actually in the data.

## Example
Imagine the customer-facing team is helping build a new version of a company’s most popular product.

Your role might be to collect and share data about:

- customer buying behavior
- preferences that could shape product features

In this case, your analysis should focus on:

- what the data actually shows
- not what stakeholders hope it shows

### Important takeaway
Stay objective.  
Your job is to report what is true in the data, not to confirm someone’s assumptions.

---

# 3. Data Science Team

## Who they are
Data work inside a company is a team effort.

You may work with:

- other data analysts
- data scientists
- data engineers

## How teamwork works
Each person may focus on a different part of the data.

Example:

- you analyze **employee productivity**
- another analyst studies **hiring data**
- a data scientist uses those findings to **predict** how new processes could improve productivity and engagement

When all team members share their results, the team can uncover the **bigger story**.

## Why this matters
A big part of your job is collaborating with other data team members to:

- explore new angles in the data
- combine findings
- uncover broader insights

---

# Working Effectively with Stakeholders

When working with any stakeholder group, you often need to go **beyond the data**.  
You need strong communication, trust, and clear delivery of findings.

Below are the main tips from the lesson.

---

## 1. Discuss Goals
Stakeholder requests are usually connected to a bigger business goal.

When a stakeholder asks for something:

- ask questions
- learn more about their real goal
- understand the result they want

A quick conversation about goals can help:

- set expectations
- clarify the request
- plan the next steps

### Why this matters
Sometimes what a stakeholder asks for is not exactly what they actually need.

---

## 2. Feel Empowered to Say “No”
Sometimes stakeholders make urgent or unrealistic requests.

### Example
A marketing director may say they have a **high-priority** project and want you to produce analysis and charts by tomorrow morning to support their hypothesis.

But you may realize:

- their hypothesis is weak or incomplete
- there is a better way to do the analysis
- the work will take more time than they expect

In situations like this, do not be afraid to push back.

## Why saying no can be necessary
Stakeholders may not understand:

- how much time data collection takes
- how much effort analysis requires
- what they really need

Your job is to help them figure that out.

## How to say no well
If you cannot do what they ask:

- say no respectfully
- explain why
- give context
- suggest a better option if one exists
- clarify what can be done and by when

If other projects must come first:

- explain your priorities
- discuss a realistic timeline

### Important takeaway
You should feel empowered to say no, but always explain your reasoning clearly so expectations can be reset properly.

---

## 3. Plan for the Unexpected
Before starting a project:

- make a list of possible roadblocks
- think about what might slow the work down

When discussing timelines and expectations with stakeholders:

- add extra time for solving problems at each stage of the process

### Why this matters
Unexpected issues are normal in data projects, so planning for them helps avoid missed deadlines and frustration.

---

## 4. Know Your Project
Keep track of project discussions through:

- email
- reports
- other documentation

Be ready to answer questions about:

- why certain parts of the project matter
- how the work connects to the organization

You should understand:

- why the analysis is being done
- how it supports company goals
- how it connects to other work in the business

### Why this matters
A strong understanding of the project helps you:

- provide better insights
- connect your analysis to bigger goals
- solve larger business problems more effectively

---

## 5. Start with Words and Visuals
Data analysts and stakeholders often think they understand each other when they actually do not.

This is called the **illusion of agreement**.

## Illusion of agreement
This happens when both sides assume they are on the same page, but they are actually imagining different things.

This can cause projects to go back and forth many times before the final direction becomes clear.

## How to avoid it
Start with:

- a short written description
- a quick visual

This helps make your message clearer early in the process.

Stakeholders may prefer:

- words
- pictures
- or both

Then you can work together to improve the idea from there.

## Why this matters
The faster everyone agrees on the direction:

- the faster the first analysis can begin
- the faster the team can test whether the project is useful
- the faster feedback can be measured
- the faster improvements can be made

---

## 6. Communicate Often
Stakeholders usually want regular updates.

Share updates about:

- project milestones
- setbacks
- changes
- overall progress

Then use your notes to create a report others can share and review.

## Change-log
A **change-log** is a file that contains a **chronologically ordered list of modifications** made to a project.

Depending on how it is set up, stakeholders may be able to check it whenever they want.

### Why this matters
Frequent communication helps everyone stay aligned and reduces confusion.

---

# Key Takeaways
- A data analysis project should answer the business task and support data-driven decisions.
- Stakeholders are people who invest time, interest, and resources in a project.
- The three common stakeholder groups are:
  - executive team
  - customer-facing team
  - data science team
- Executive stakeholders want high-level insights and quick answers.
- Customer-facing stakeholders may have specific requests, but your analysis must stay objective.
- Data science teams rely on collaboration across multiple technical roles.
- Strong stakeholder communication requires more than technical analysis.
- You should discuss goals, communicate clearly, plan for problems, document progress, and stay aligned with business needs.
- It is okay to say no when a request is unrealistic, as long as you explain why.
- Starting with words and visuals can prevent misunderstandings.
- Frequent updates and change-logs help build trust and keep projects moving.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- A data analyst is not just someone who analyzes numbers. They also help manage communication, expectations, and decision-making.
- Different stakeholders need different kinds of information, so you should adjust how you present your work.
- Being objective is very important, especially when stakeholders want data to support a belief they already have.
- Good stakeholder management helps turn analysis into action.

---

# Final Summary
In data analytics, stakeholder management is a major part of the job.  
You need to understand who your stakeholders are, what they need, how they make decisions, and how to communicate with them effectively. Executive teams want quick, high-level insights, customer-facing teams need accurate information grounded in data, and data science teams depend on collaboration. By discussing goals, setting boundaries, planning ahead, documenting your work, using words and visuals, and communicating often, you can build trust and help the whole project succeed.
