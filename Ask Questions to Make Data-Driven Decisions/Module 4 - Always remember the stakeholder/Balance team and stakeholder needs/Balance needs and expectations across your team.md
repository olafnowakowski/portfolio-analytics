# Stakeholder Expectations in Data Analytics

## Main Idea
As a data analyst, one of your most important responsibilities is understanding and managing **stakeholder expectations**.  
Your work needs to match what stakeholders need, because they depend on your analysis to do their own jobs and help the project succeed.

---

## What is a Stakeholder?
A **stakeholder** is a person who has invested **time, interest, or resources** into a project.

### Simple meaning:
They have something at stake in the project, so they care about the results and will often use your work to support their own decisions.

---

## Why Stakeholder Expectations Matter
Focusing on stakeholder expectations is important because it helps you:

- understand the **goal of the project**
- make sure your work matches the **needs of the team**
- communicate clearly with everyone involved
- build **trust and confidence** in your work
- help the team move the project forward successfully

---

## What Stakeholders Want to Discuss
Stakeholders will usually want to talk about:

- the **project objective**
- what is needed to reach that goal
- any **challenges, concerns, or problems**
- progress updates
- how your findings should be shared

These conversations are a good thing because they help create trust and improve teamwork.

---

## Example Project: HR and Employee Turnover

### Situation
Imagine you are a data analyst working with a company's **Human Resources (HR)** department.

The company has noticed that its **turnover rate** has increased.

### Turnover rate:
The **turnover rate** is the rate at which employees leave a company.

The HR department wants to know:

- **why employees are leaving**
- what patterns exist
- what possible solutions could reduce turnover

---

## Stakeholders in This Example

### 1. Vice President (VP) of HR
The VP of HR wants to:

- identify shared patterns among employees who quit
- see whether there is a connection between quitting, **employee productivity**, and **engagement**
- use the results to make decisions

### 2. Project Manager
The project manager is usually the person you update more often.

#### Their role:
- plan and execute the project
- keep the project on track
- monitor the progress of the team

#### What you need to do:
- give regular updates
- explain what you need to succeed
- tell them about any problems or blockers

### 3. HR Administrators
HR administrators need to know:

- which **metrics** you are using

This helps them:

- design better ways to collect employee data effectively

### 4. Other Data Analysts
You may also work with other analysts who focus on different parts of the data.

This means collaboration is important, because each analyst may discover information that supports the full picture.

---

## Why Knowing the Stakeholders is Important
It is important to know:

- who the stakeholders are
- what they need from you
- how often they need updates
- how your work helps them do their jobs

This allows you to communicate effectively and give each person the information they need to move forward in their role.

---

## Example of Data Analysis Findings

After analyzing company data, you discover:

- employee **engagement** and **performance** drop after the first **13 months**
- this may suggest employees start to feel:
  - demotivated
  - disconnected from their work
- many of them then quit a few months later

At the same time, another data analyst who studies hiring data finds that:

- the company had a large increase in hiring around **18 months ago**

---

## Team Communication
You share your findings with:

- team members
- stakeholders

Then they give feedback about:

- how the information should be presented
- how to communicate it to the VP of HR

This shows that data analysis is not only about finding insights.  
It is also about **sharing them clearly with the right people**.

---

## Outcome of the Project
Based on the analysis, the VP decides to:

- create an **in-depth manager check-in**
- do this with employees who are close to their **12-month mark** at the company
- use the check-in to identify **career growth opportunities**

### Result:
This action helps reduce employee turnover starting around the **13-month mark**.

---

## Big Lesson from This Example
This example shows how a data analyst must balance the needs and expectations of different people on a team.

People on the project may include:

- senior leaders like the VP of HR
- project managers
- HR staff
- other data analysts

All of them need strong communication and useful information from you.

---

## Key Takeaways
- Stakeholders are people who have an interest or investment in a project.
- Your work as a data analyst should align with stakeholder needs.
- Good communication helps build trust and keeps projects moving.
- Different stakeholders need different things from you.
- Project managers often need regular updates.
- Stakeholders may help decide how your results should be presented.
- Data analysis is not just about finding answers — it is also about helping others take action based on those answers.

---

## Extra Important Notes
- A data analyst must focus on both **technical analysis** and **people communication**.
- Understanding stakeholder expectations helps prevent confusion and keeps the analysis relevant.
- A strong project outcome often depends on how well the analyst communicates insights across the team.
- Stakeholder communication is a major part of turning data into real business solutions.

---

## Final Summary
Stakeholder expectations are a key part of data analytics work.  
Stakeholders depend on your analysis to make decisions, solve problems, and achieve project goals.  
By understanding who they are, what they need, and how to communicate with them, you can do more effective analysis, support your team better, and help create successful solutions.
