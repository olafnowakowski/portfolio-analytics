# Contextualizing Data and Recognizing Data Bias

## Main Idea
Data is not useful by itself. To understand what data really means, you need **context**.  
Context is the situation or conditions in which something exists or happens.

The video explains that as a data analyst, your job is not only to collect data, but also to:
- understand the context around it
- interpret it fairly
- stay objective
- recognize bias before making conclusions

---

## What “context” means
Context changes the meaning of information or actions.

### Example from the video:
Yelling **“move!”** can mean different things depending on the situation:
- it may be rude if your friend is blocking the TV
- it may be appropriate if your friend is about to be hit by a kid on a tricycle

The point is that the same word or action can mean different things depending on the situation.  
The same is true for data.

### Important idea:
**Numbers alone do not mean much without context.**

---

## Why data becomes more valuable with better analysis
The speaker explains that as more data becomes available, we can use it in more advanced ways and get stronger insights from it.

### Different levels of data use:

#### 1. Descriptive
Descriptive data answers basic questions about what happened.

**Example:**
- How much did we spend on travel last month?

#### 2. Diagnostic
Diagnostic insights help explain **why** something happened.

**Example:**
- Why did travel spending increase last month?

#### 3. Predictive
Predictive insights help estimate what may happen next.

#### 4. Prescriptive
Prescriptive insights are the most valuable because they help guide action.

**Example:**
- How can we use data to encourage more efficient travel?

### Key point:
Data becomes more valuable when it moves from simply describing events to helping people make better decisions.

---

## A data analyst’s role
A major part of being a data analyst is **putting data into context**.

This means:
- understanding what the data is really about
- looking at all sides before drawing conclusions
- staying objective
- avoiding personal assumptions

The video emphasizes that figuring out what data means is just as important as collecting it.

---

## Why people can interpret the same data differently
Even if two people work with the same dataset and follow the same instructions, they may still come to different conclusions.

### Why?
Because there is **no universal set of contextual interpretations**.

People interpret data based on:
- personal experience
- culture
- society
- market norms
- conscious bias
- subconscious bias

So even when data collection is done correctly, the analysis can still be misinterpreted.

---

## What bias is and why it matters
Bias can affect the way people understand and explain data.

### Example from the video:
If you ask someone from Boston which baseball team is the best, they will probably say the **Boston Red Sox**.

This shows how personal background and preferences can influence conclusions.

### Important point:
A major limitation of data analytics is that **if analysis is not objective, the conclusions can be misleading**.

That means bias can make results unfair, incomplete, or inaccurate.

---

## Questions analysts should ask to understand data
To interpret data correctly, the video says analysts should think through:

- **Who**
- **What**
- **Where**
- **When**
- **How**
- **Why**

These questions help analysts understand the full meaning of the data.

---

## The “Who” question
Ask:
- **Who collected the data?**
- **Who is represented in the data?**

This matters because the source of the data can affect how trustworthy, complete, or biased it may be.

---

## The “What” question
Ask:
- **What is the data about?**
- **What does the data represent in the real world?**
- **How does it connect to other data?**

This helps you understand what the numbers actually mean instead of just reading them at face value.

---

## The “When” question
Ask:
- **When was the data collected?**

Older data may not fully match current conditions.

### Example from the video:
If phone numbers had been collected over the past century, eventually **mobile phones** would have been introduced.  
That would mean the dataset might need an extra phone number field.

### Key point:
Data collected a long time ago can have limitations because the world changes.

---

## The “Where” question
Ask:
- **Where was the data collected?**

This matters because conditions can vary across:
- cities
- states
- countries

So data from one location may not apply the same way somewhere else.

---

## The “How” question
Ask:
- **How was the data collected?**

The collection method affects quality.

### Example from the video:
A **survey** may not be as effective as an **in-person interview**.

### Key point:
Different collection methods may produce different types or levels of accuracy in the data.

---

## The “Why” question
Ask:
- **Why was the data collected?**

This is especially important because the reason behind data collection can be strongly connected to bias.

### Important warning:
Sometimes data is collected—or even made up—to support an agenda.

This means analysts must be careful and question the purpose behind the data.

---

## How to make data fair and accurate
The video says the best way to improve fairness and accuracy is to:

- start with an accurate representation of the population
- collect data in the most appropriate way
- collect it as objectively as possible

Doing this helps analysts work with reliable facts and pass accurate information to their team.

---

## Final takeaway
To understand data correctly, you need more than numbers.  
You need context, fairness, and objectivity.

### The biggest lessons from this video:
- Data does not exist in a vacuum.
- Numbers are hard to understand without context.
- Analysts must interpret data carefully, not just collect it.
- Bias can affect conclusions even if the data collection process was correct.
- Asking **who, what, where, when, how, and why** helps reveal the full meaning of the data.
- Fair and objective data leads to more accurate decisions.

---

## Extra note for your notes
A very important idea in this lesson is that **good analysis is not just about technical skill**.  
It is also about judgment.

A strong data analyst must:
- question the source of the data
- understand the situation around it
- notice possible bias
