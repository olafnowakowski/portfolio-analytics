# Scope of Work (SOW) — Notes

## Activity Overview
In this activity, you learn how a **Scope of Work (SOW)** helps data analysts plan projects clearly and avoid mistakes.  
You use a template to create a **mock SOW** for an imaginary project and practice thinking like a real analyst.

The goal is to understand how analysts:
- define the work they will do
- clarify expectations
- set boundaries
- communicate progress clearly

---

## Why an SOW is Important
A **Scope of Work** is one of the most important documents in a data project.

It helps make sure that:
- analysts
- engineers
- managers
- sponsors
- stakeholders

all understand the **same project goals**, **requirements**, and **plan**.

### Main reason for using an SOW
Data analysis projects are meant to solve **business problems** or support **business tasks** that matter to stakeholders.  
An SOW helps everyone agree on:
- what the business goal is
- what work will be done
- what will be delivered
- what is *not* part of the project

---

## Connection to the Data Analysis Process
The SOW is strongly connected to the **first phase of the Data Analysis Process: Ask questions**.

Before creating an SOW, analysts ask questions to understand:
- requirements
- goals
- stakeholders
- data sources
- project limits
- expectations

The word **“ask”** has two meanings here:
1. asking questions to collect project information  
2. understanding exactly what you are being asked to do

This is important because if you do not know what is **inside** or **outside** the project, then you cannot clearly define success.

---

## What Makes a Good SOW
There is **no single standard format** for a Scope of Work.  
Different organizations and projects may use different layouts.

But strong SOWs usually include the same core content.

### 1. Deliverables
Deliverables explain:
- what work is being done
- what is being created
- what stakeholders will receive at the end

You must be **specific**.

#### Important idea:
Avoid vague statements.

### Bad example:
> “Fixing traffic problems”

This is too broad because it could mean many different things.

### Better example:
> “Identify top 10 issues with traffic patterns within the city limits, and identify the top 3 solutions that are most cost-effective for reducing traffic congestion.”

This is better because it is:
- specific
- measurable
- limited in scope

Deliverables can also include details like:
- whether data will be collected
- how much data will be collected
- how long data collection will last

---

### 2. Milestones
Milestones are the **major checkpoints** of the project.

They help define when an important part of the work is considered complete.

Milestones may be set by:
- the analyst
- stakeholders
- a project manager
- other team members

### Examples of milestones:
- collect and process 50% of required data (100 survey responses)
- complete initial data analysis report
- deliver finished dashboards and reports to stakeholders

Milestones are important because they help track progress.

---

### 3. Timeline
The timeline explains **how long each part of the project should take**.

It should help everyone know whether the project is on schedule.

A good timeline answers questions like:
- when will deliverables be completed?
- how long will the whole project take?
- how long will each step take?
- when should each milestone be reached?

The timeline and milestones are closely connected.

---

### 4. Reports
Reports explain **how progress will be communicated** to stakeholders and sponsors.

A good SOW should say:
- how updates will be shared
- how often updates will happen
- what information will be included in the updates

### Example reporting frequency:
- weekly
- monthly
- after major milestones

This helps set expectations for communication during the project.

---

## What an SOW Must Always Be
At minimum, a good SOW should be:
- **specific**
- **relevant**
- **accurate**

Also, if the project changes, the SOW should be **updated** to reflect those changes.

This is very important.

---

## What Is “In Scope” and “Out of Scope”?
An SOW must clearly explain what work **is part of the project** and what work **is not part of the project**.

The **scope** is everything you are expected to complete or accomplish.

It should be detailed enough that there is **no confusion** about whether something belongs to the project.

### Example:
In the traffic example, the study only covers areas **within the city limits**.

That makes the scope clear because anyone can check a map and know whether a road or intersection is included.

---

## Why Defining Scope Can Be Difficult
Defining scope sounds simple, but it can be tricky.

That is why analysts should be:
- very specific
- as clear as possible
- quantitative whenever possible

### Climate change coastline example
If you are studying how climate change affects a city coastline, you need to clearly define:
- which parts of the coastline you are responsible for
- which parts are outside your responsibility

This could be done using:
- GPS coordinates
- landmarks
- exact boundaries

The idea is to reduce confusion and make expectations clear.

---

## Key Takeaways
- An SOW keeps everyone aligned on goals, expectations, and responsibilities.
- It is created by asking clear questions at the beginning of a project.
- A good SOW defines both what the project includes and what it does not include.
- Strong SOWs are specific and measurable.
- The four main foundational parts are:
  - deliverables
  - milestones
  - timeline
  - reports
- The SOW should be updated if project details change.

## Extra Important Note
A weak SOW causes confusion.  
A strong SOW makes success easier because everyone knows:
- what the goal is
- what work will be done
- when it should be done
- how progress will be shared
- where the project boundaries are

That is why this document is considered an **industry-standard tool** for analysts.
