# Context in Data Analytics

## What context means
In data analytics, **context** is the background information that helps explain what the data means.  
It includes the conditions and circumstances around the data.

### Simple idea:
Data by itself is often just numbers.  
**Context gives those numbers meaning.**

---

## Why context is important
Context is important because it helps turn **raw or disorganized data** into information that people can understand and use.

Without context:
- data can be confusing
- people may guess the wrong meaning
- analysis may be misunderstood

### Key point:
**Data has little value if it is not paired with context.**

---

## Example: data without context
Imagine you see this table:

| 2010 | 28000 |
|------|------:|
| 2005 | 18000 |
| 2000 | 23000 |
| 1995 | 10000 |

You might guess what it means, but you cannot be sure.

Maybe:
- the first column is years
- the second column is money, people, sales, or something else

There is not enough information to understand it clearly.

---

## Example: data with context
Now imagine the table is labeled like this:

| Years (collected every 5 years) | Respondents |
|---------------------------------|------------:|
| 2010                            | 28000       |
| 2005                            | 18000       |
| 2000                            | 23000       |
| 1995                            | 10000       |

Now the data makes much more sense.

You can understand that:
- the first column shows the **years of a survey**
- the second column shows the **number of people who responded**
- the survey was done **every 5 years**

This extra information is the **context**.

---

## What it means to contextualize data
To **contextualize data** means to give the data meaning by explaining its background and purpose.

A data analyst should identify these 6 important parts of context:

### 1. Who
Who created, collected, or funded the data?

This helps you understand:
- where the data came from
- who was responsible for it
- whether the source may affect the data

---

### 2. What
What things in the real world can this data affect?

This helps explain:
- what the data is about
- what it relates to
- why it matters

---

### 3. Where
Where did the data come from?

This matters because data from one place may not mean the same thing somewhere else.

---

### 4. When
When was the data created or collected?

This is important because:
- data can become outdated
- timing can affect the results
- trends may change over time

---

### 5. Why
Why was the data created or collected?

This helps explain:
- the purpose of the data
- the reason it exists
- whether there may be an agenda or bias behind it

---

### 6. How
How was the data created or collected?

This matters because the method can affect:
- quality
- accuracy
- reliability

For example, data collected from a survey may be different from data collected through interviews or direct observation.

---

## Why context matters in every step of analysis
Context is important during the **entire data analysis process**, not just at the start.

### During data collection
You should ask questions about the context to better understand:
- the business
- the business process
- why the data is being collected

This helps make sure the data is useful and relevant.

---

### During data organization
Context helps you decide:
- how to name files, columns, and variables
- how to show relationships between variables
- what information should be kept
- what information can be left out

So context helps you organize data in a logical way.

---

### During data presentation
When you present your analysis, context helps stakeholders understand:
- what the data means
- what the findings are based on
- why the results matter

Without context, people may not understand your analysis correctly.

---

## Why this is important for a data analyst
A data analyst should not just collect data and show numbers.  
They also need to explain what the data means in the real world.

That is why contextualizing data is an important skill early in a data analyst’s career.

---

## Key takeaways
- **Context gives data meaning.**
- Data alone is often unclear or incomplete.
- Labels, background, timing, source, and method all help explain data.
- Analysts should always think about:
  - **Who**
  - **What**
  - **Where**
  - **When**
  - **Why**
  - **How**
- Context is important when collecting, organizing, and presenting data.
- Good analysis is not just about numbers — it is also about helping people understand what those numbers mean.

---

## Easy summary for notes
Context in data analytics is the background information that helps explain data. It makes raw data easier to understand and more useful. Analysts should always ask who, what, where, when, why, and how, because context is important in every step of the analysis process.
