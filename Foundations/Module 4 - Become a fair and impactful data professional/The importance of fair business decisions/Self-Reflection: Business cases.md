# Case Study #1 
To improve the effectiveness of its teaching staff, the administration of a high school offered the opportunity for all teachers to participate in a workshop. They were not required to attend; instead, the administration encouraged teachers to sign up. Of the 43 teachers on staff, 19 chose to take the workshop.
At the end of the academic year, the administration collected data on teacher performance for all teachers on staff. The data was collected via student survey. In the survey, students were asked to rank each teacher's effectiveness on a scale of 1 (very poor) to 6 (very good). 
The administration compared data on teachers who attended the workshop to data on teachers who did not. The comparison revealed that teachers who attended the workshop had an average score of 4.95, while teachers who did not attend had an average score of 4.22. The administration concluded that the workshop was a success.

**Reflection**
- What are the examples of fair or unfair practices?
  - This is an example of an unfair practice because the school assumed the workshop caused higher teacher ratings, even though the workshop was voluntary and not random. Since teachers chose to attend, the group that went to the workshop may already have been more motivated or better teachers. Because of this, the higher ratings cannot be used as proof that the workshop was successful. Another unfair practice is using student survey responses as evidence when those responses were not directly connected to workshop attendance.
- How could a data analyst correct the unfair practices?
  - A data analyst could correct this by having teachers selected randomly to attend the workshop. This would make the groups more equal and fair to compare. The analyst should also collect data that is directly related to the workshop, such as whether teachers used the strategies they learned and whether those strategies improved classroom results. This would make the conclusion more accurate and fair.
 
# Case Study #2
An automotive company tests the driving capabilities of its self-driving car prototype. They carry out the tests on various types of roadways—specifically, a race track, trail track, and dirt road.
The researchers only test the prototype during the daytime. They collect two types of data: sensor data from the car during the drives and video data of the drives from cameras on the car.
They review the data after the initial tests. The results illustrate that the new self-driving car meets the performance standards across each of the roadways. As a result, the car can progress to the next phase of testing, which will include driving in various weather conditions. 

**Reflection**
- What are the examples of fair or unfair practices?
  - his is an example of an unfair practice because the researchers tested the prototype on three different tracks but only during the day. The conditions on each track may be very different during the day and at night, so only testing in daytime does not give a complete or fair picture of how the prototype performs. This could affect the results and make the conclusions less accurate.
- How could a data analyst correct the unfair practices?
  - A data analyst could correct this unfair practice by asking the test team to include nighttime testing as well. Testing during both the day and the night would give a fuller perspective of the prototype’s performance under different conditions. This would make the data more complete, fair, and reliable.

# Case Study #3
An amusement park plans to add new rides to their property. First, they need to determine what kinds of new rides visitors want the park to build. In order to understand their visitors’ interests, the park develops a survey. 
They decide to distribute the survey near the roller coasters because the lines are long enough that visitors will have time to answer all of the questions. After collecting this survey data, they find that most of the respondents want more roller coasters at the park. They conclude that they should add more roller coasters, as most of their visitors prefer them.

**Reflection**
- What are the examples of fair or unfair practices?
  - This is an example of an unfair practice because the survey was only given to people waiting in line for the roller coasters. This creates sampling bias because the people answering the survey may already prefer roller coasters, so their opinions may not represent all visitors at the amusement park. As a result, the survey findings could be unfairly biased and not reflect the park’s general audience.
- How could a data analyst correct the unfair practices?
  - A data analyst could correct this by giving the survey in places where many different visitors have time to respond, such as at the entrance and exit of the park. This would help include a wider range of people instead of mostly roller coaster fans. As a result, the data would be more balanced, fair, and accurate.
