# Data Collection Considerations

## Main Idea

As a Data Analyst, you will often need to decide what kind of data to collect and use for a project.

There is a huge amount of data available, so your job is to choose the data that actually helps answer the business question or solve the problem.

---

# Why Choosing the Right Data Matters

Not all data is useful for every project.

Good data should:

- match the business question
- help answer the problem
- come from a trustworthy source
- be accurate
- be approved for use
- be relevant to the analysis

### Important Lesson

A Data Analyst should not get distracted by unrelated data. The data you choose should directly support the question you are trying to answer.

---

# Example: Rush Hour Traffic

## Business Question

What is causing increased rush hour traffic in your city?

To answer this question, you need to think about:

- how the data will be collected
- where the data comes from
- how much data is needed
- what type of data is needed
- what time period the data should cover

---

# 1. How Will the Data Be Collected?

The first thing to consider is how the data will be collected.

For the traffic example, you could collect data by observing traffic patterns.

You might count:

- how many cars are on the road
- which streets are busiest
- what time traffic builds up
- where cars are getting backed up

### Example

You observe that cars are getting backed up on one specific street during rush hour.

This observation becomes useful traffic data.

---

# 2. Data Sources

A **data source** is where your data comes from.

There are three main types of data sources:

1. **First-party data**
2. **Second-party data**
3. **Third-party data**

---

# First-Party Data

## Definition

**First-party data** is data collected by an individual or group using their own resources.

## Example

If you personally observe traffic patterns and count cars on city streets, that is first-party data.

## Why It Is Preferred

First-party data is usually preferred because you know exactly:

- where it came from
- how it was collected
- who collected it
- why it was collected

### Important Lesson

First-party data is often more trustworthy because you have direct knowledge of its source.

---

# Second-Party Data

## Definition

**Second-party data** is data collected by a group directly from its audience and then sold or shared with another organization.

## Example

If you cannot collect your own traffic data, you might buy traffic study data from an organization that has already studied traffic patterns in your city.

## Why It Can Be Reliable

Second-party data can still be reliable if it comes from a trusted organization with experience in that subject.

### Example

A traffic research organization may be a reliable second-party source because it has experience collecting and analyzing traffic data.

---

# Third-Party Data

## Definition

**Third-party data** is data collected from outside sources that did not collect it directly.

This data may have passed through several sources before reaching you.

## Why It Can Be Risky

Third-party data may be less reliable because:

- the original source may be unclear
- the collection method may be unknown
- the data may be combined from many places
- it may contain bias
- it may be outdated or inaccurate

## Is Third-Party Data Useless?

No.

Third-party data can still be useful, but it must be checked carefully.

You should inspect it for:

- accuracy
- bias
- credibility
- trustworthiness

### Important Lesson

All data should be checked for quality, but third-party data needs extra caution.

---

# Data Must Be Approved for Use

The data you choose must be approved for use.

This means you need to make sure:

- you are allowed to access it
- you are allowed to analyze it
- you are allowed to share it if needed
- it follows privacy and ethical rules

### Important Lesson

Just because data exists does not mean you are allowed to use it.

---

# 3. Choosing Relevant Data

A Data Analyst must choose data that helps answer the question.

## Traffic Example

Useful data might include:

- traffic volume by time of day
- traffic volume by street
- historical traffic patterns
- road construction data
- accident reports
- public transportation schedules

Less useful data might include:

- unrelated financial data
- restaurant sales data
- data that has nothing to do with traffic patterns

### Important Lesson

Relevant data helps solve the problem. Irrelevant data distracts from the analysis.

---

# 4. How Much Data Should You Collect?

Another important decision is how much data to collect.

To understand this, you need to know the difference between:

- population
- sample

---

# Population

## Definition

In data analytics, a **population** refers to all possible data values in a dataset.

## Traffic Example

If you are analyzing car traffic in a city, the population would be:

**all the cars in that area**

---

# Sample

## Definition

A **sample** is a smaller part of a population that represents the population.

## Why Samples Are Useful

Collecting data from an entire population can be difficult, expensive, or impossible.

A sample allows you to study part of the population and use it to understand the larger group.

## Traffic Example

Instead of tracking every car in the city, you might collect data from:

- one busy intersection
- one specific street
- a random sample of traffic records
- traffic during selected rush hour periods

---

# Choosing the Right Sample

How you choose your sample depends on the project.

A good sample should be representative of the larger population.

### Important Lesson

A sample should reflect the population well enough that your analysis can produce useful conclusions.

---

# 5. Choosing the Right Data Type

You also need to select the right type of data.

The data type should match the question you are asking.

## Traffic Example

If you want to understand when traffic is busiest, useful data types might include:

- dates
- times
- number of cars
- street names
- traffic speed
- accident counts

## Date Format Example

Traffic records stored in a date format could help you figure out which days of the week are likely to have high traffic volume.

### Important Lesson

The right data type helps make the analysis accurate and useful.

---

# 6. Choosing the Time Frame

You need to decide what time period your data should cover.

The time frame depends on the question and how quickly you need an answer.

---

## Historical Data

**Historical data** is data that already exists.

## When to Use Historical Data

You might use historical data if:

- you need an answer quickly
- data has already been collected
- you want to compare past patterns
- you want to identify trends over time

## Traffic Example

If you need an immediate answer about rush hour traffic, you may need to use traffic data that already exists.

---

# Long-Term Data Collection

Sometimes you need to collect data over a longer period of time.

For example, if you want to study traffic patterns over months or years, you may need to collect new data continuously.

This can affect:

- what data you collect
- how you collect it
- how much data you collect
- what tools you use
- how you organize the data

---

# Main Data Collection Factors

When collecting data, consider:

1. **How the data will be collected**
2. **Where the data comes from**
3. **Whether the data is first-party, second-party, or third-party**
4. **Whether the data is accurate and trustworthy**
5. **Whether the data is approved for use**
6. **How much data is needed**
7. **Whether to use a population or sample**
8. **What data type is needed**
9. **What time frame the data should cover**
10. **Whether historical data is enough or new data must be collected**

---

# Big Lesson

Choosing the right data is one of the most important parts of the Prepare step in data analysis.

The right data helps you answer the question clearly.

The wrong data can lead to confusion, wasted time, or inaccurate conclusions.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Data Analysts must decide what data to collect and use for each project.
- The business task or problem often helps narrow down the data needed.
- Data collection begins by deciding how the data will be collected.
- First-party data is data you collect yourself using your own resources.
- First-party data is usually preferred because you know where it came from.
- Second-party data is collected directly by another group from its audience and then sold or shared.
- Third-party data comes from outside sources that did not collect it directly.
- Third-party data can be useful, but it must be checked carefully.
- All data should be inspected for accuracy and trustworthiness.
- Data must be approved for use.
- A population includes all possible data values in a dataset.
- A sample is a smaller part of a population that represents the whole.
- Samples are useful when collecting data from the full population is difficult.
- Choosing the correct data type is important for accurate analysis.
- The time frame for data collection depends on the business question.
- Historical data is data that already exists.
- Relevant data helps solve the problem; unrelated data distracts from it.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Do not collect data just because it is available.
- Always connect the data back to the business question.
- First-party data is not automatically perfect; it still needs to be checked.
- Second-party and third-party data can be useful, but source credibility matters.
- The sample must be chosen carefully to avoid misleading results.
- Time frame matters because old data may not answer a current question.
- The data collection method affects the quality of the analysis.

This lesson explains the main factors Data Analysts should consider when collecting data. Because there is so much data available, analysts must choose data that is relevant to the business question. In the traffic example, observations of rush hour traffic could be first-party data, while purchased traffic studies could be second-party data.
