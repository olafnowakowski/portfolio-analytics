# Data Collection Considerations

## Main Idea

Before starting an analysis, a Data Analyst needs to think carefully about **what data to collect**, **where it comes from**, **how much is needed**, and **whether it fits the business problem**.

Choosing the wrong data can lead to inaccurate or useless analysis.

---

# Key Data Collection Considerations

When collecting data, keep these main questions in mind:

1. **How will the data be collected?**
2. **Where will the data come from?**
3. **Will the data help solve the business problem?**
4. **How much data should be collected?**
5. **What time frame is needed?**

---

# 1. How the Data Will Be Collected

## Main Question

Will you collect the data yourself, or will you get it from another source?

If you collect the data using your own resources, it is called **first-party data**.

---

## First-Party Data

**First-party data** is data collected directly by you or your organization.

Examples:

- customer surveys your company sends
- website data collected by your company
- interviews your team conducts
- observations your team records
- sales data from your company’s system

### Why First-Party Data Is Useful

First-party data is usually valuable because you know:

- where it came from
- how it was collected
- why it was collected
- whether it matches your analysis needs

### Important Lesson

First-party data is often preferred because the source and collection process are clearer.

---

# 2. Data Sources

If you do not collect the data yourself, you may get data from another provider.

These sources may be:

- second-party data providers
- third-party data providers

---

## Second-Party Data

**Second-party data** is data collected directly by another group and then sold or shared.

Example:

A traffic research company collects traffic data from drivers and then sells that data to a city planning department.

### Important Note

Second-party data can be useful if the source is trustworthy and collected the data directly.

---

## Third-Party Data

**Third-party data** is data sold by a provider that did not collect the data themselves.

Third-party data may come from many different sources before it reaches you.

### Why Third-Party Data Needs Caution

Third-party data can be less reliable because:

- the original source may be unclear
- the collection method may be unknown
- the data may be combined from many places
- it may contain bias
- it may not be fully accurate
- it may not perfectly fit your business problem

### Important Lesson

Third-party data can still be useful, but it must be checked carefully for accuracy, bias, and credibility.

---

# 3. Solving the Business Problem

## Main Question

Does the data actually help answer your business question?

Datasets can contain a lot of interesting information, but not all interesting data is useful for your analysis.

You should choose data that directly supports the problem you are trying to solve.

---

## Example: Trends Over Time

If you are analyzing trends over time, you need **time series data**.

## Time Series Data

**Time series data** is data that includes dates or times.

It helps show how something changes over a period of time.

Examples:

- monthly sales revenue
- daily website visits
- yearly customer growth
- hourly traffic volume
- weekly product returns

### Important Lesson

If your question involves change over time, your data must include time-related information.

---

# 4. How Much Data to Collect

## Main Question

How large should your dataset be?

If you are collecting your own data, you need to make reasonable decisions about **sample size**.

---

## Sample Size

A **sample size** is the amount of data you collect from a larger population.

A sample should be large enough and representative enough to support reliable analysis.

---

## Random Sample

A **random sample** is a sample selected randomly from existing data.

This may be enough for some projects.

Example:

Randomly selecting 1,000 customer survey responses from a database of 100,000 responses.

---

## Strategic Data Collection

Some projects need more focused data collection.

Instead of using a random sample, you may need to collect data based on specific criteria.

Examples:

- customers from a certain region
- users in a certain age group
- transactions from a specific time period
- customers who abandoned shopping carts
- employees from a specific department

### Important Lesson

Each project has different data needs, so the sample size and sampling method should match the business question.

---

# 5. Time Frame

## Main Question

What time period should the data cover?

If you are collecting your own data, you need to decide how long to collect it.

This is especially important if you are studying trends over time.

---

## Long-Term Data Collection

If you need to understand patterns over a long period, you may need to collect data for weeks, months, or years.

Examples:

- customer satisfaction over one year
- sales trends over five years
- traffic patterns across different seasons
- product demand before and after a campaign

---

## Immediate Answers

If you need an answer quickly, you may not have time to collect new data.

In that case, you may need to use **historical data**.

---

## Historical Data

**Historical data** is data that already exists.

Examples:

- past sales records
- previous customer surveys
- old website analytics
- archived traffic reports
- previous financial reports

### Important Lesson

The time available for the project can affect whether you collect new data or use existing historical data.

---

# Data Collection Decision Guide

Use these questions to guide your data collection decisions:

## Step 1: What problem are you solving?

Choose data that directly helps answer the business question.

## Step 2: Do you need new data or existing data?

If you need an immediate answer, use historical data if it is available.

If you need to track new behavior or future trends, collect new data.

## Step 3: Who will collect the data?

Decide whether the data will be:

- first-party
- second-party
- third-party

## Step 4: How much data is needed?

Decide whether you need:

- a random sample
- a targeted sample
- a larger dataset
- data from the full population, if possible

## Step 5: What time frame is needed?

Decide whether the data should cover:

- days
- weeks
- months
- years
- a specific historical period
- a future tracking period

# Big Lesson

Data collection should always be connected to the business problem.

You should not collect data just because it is available.  
You should collect data because it helps answer the question.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Data collection requires planning.
- First-party data is data collected by you or your organization.
- Second-party data is collected directly by another group and then sold or shared.
- Third-party data is sold by a provider that did not collect it directly.
- Third-party data may come from many different sources and should be checked carefully.
- Choose data that helps solve the business problem.
- If analyzing trends over time, use time series data.
- Sample size should be chosen based on the needs of the project.
- Some projects can use random samples.
- Other projects need targeted or strategic data collection.
- The time frame matters, especially when tracking trends.
- If you need an immediate answer, you may need to use historical data.
- Historical data is data that already exists.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- More data is not always better if it does not answer the question.
- Data must be relevant to the analysis.
- Data should be checked for quality, accuracy, bias, and credibility.
- The source of the data affects how much you can trust it.
- Time constraints can affect your data collection choices.
- Good data collection helps make the analysis more accurate and useful.

---

# Final Summary

This reading explains the main considerations Data Analysts should think about before collecting data. Analysts need to decide how the data will be collected, whether it will come from first-party, second-party, or third-party sources, whether it helps solve the business problem, how much data is needed, and what time frame the data should cover. If the analysis involves trends over time, time series data is needed. If an answer is needed quickly, historical data may be the best option. The main lesson is that data collection should be planned carefully so the data is relevant, trustworthy, and useful for solving the business problem.
