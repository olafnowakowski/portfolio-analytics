# Requirement Elicitation Techniques

## Main Idea

After learning the four-step process for gathering business requirements:

1. **Preparation**
2. **Elicitation**
3. **Analysis**
4. **Validation**

this lesson focuses on specific techniques business analysts can use to **elicit requirements** from stakeholders.

Requirements elicitation means gathering the information needed to understand what a solution must do to solve a business problem.

---

# Common Requirement Elicitation Techniques

Common techniques include:

- brainstorming
- focus groups
- interviews
- prototyping
- interface analysis
- surveys or questionnaires

Each technique has strengths and weaknesses, so the analyst should choose the method that best fits the project, stakeholders, timeline, and resources.

---

# 1. Brainstorming

## What It Is

**Brainstorming** is when stakeholders come together to share ideas freely.

The goal is to collect many ideas and identify themes that may help the analysis later.

## Why It Is Useful

Brainstorming can help the analyst:

- gather a wide range of ideas
- discover creative solutions
- notice common themes
- understand different stakeholder perspectives

## Analyst’s Role

The analyst acts as the facilitator.

They need to:

- make sure everyone feels comfortable sharing ideas
- encourage participation
- keep the discussion focused on the requirements
- prevent the session from drifting into unrelated topics

## Important Lesson

Brainstorming is useful for creativity, but it must be managed carefully so it does not become disorganized.

---

# 2. Focus Groups

## What It Is

A **focus group** is a smaller, carefully selected group of stakeholders who discuss the project or business need.

Focus groups are more directed and focused than general brainstorming sessions.

## Why It Is Useful

Focus groups can help when:

- time is limited
- the analyst needs focused feedback
- a smaller group can provide better insight
- some stakeholders are less creative or less helpful individually

## Important Lesson

Focus groups are useful when the analyst wants targeted feedback from a curated group of stakeholders.

---

# 3. Interviews

## What They Are

**Interviews** are one-on-one or small-group conversations with stakeholders.

They are one of the most common ways to gather requirements.

## Why Interviews Are Useful

Interviews allow the analyst to:

- have deeper conversations
- ask follow-up questions
- understand stakeholder needs
- learn about pain points
- gather detailed information

## Taking Notes

During interviews, the analyst should take detailed notes and try not to leave anything out.

Sometimes an insight may become useful later, even if it did not seem important during the interview.

## Sharing Notes

It is good practice to share interview notes with the stakeholder afterward.

This helps:

- confirm accuracy
- avoid misunderstandings
- give the stakeholder a chance to add more thoughts
- make sure the analyst understood correctly

---

# Asking the Right Questions

One of the most powerful tools a business analyst has is the ability to ask good questions.

Useful interview questions include:

- What exactly is being done in this business process or project task?
- Who is responsible for this part of the process or task?
- What level of power or control does this person have over this part of the process?
- What problems or pain points exist in the current process?
- What outcome would you like to see?
- What should the solution avoid?

## Example

If you are analyzing a sales process and discover that the process has five different parts, each owned by a different team lead, then each team lead is an important stakeholder.

### Important Lesson

Good questions help identify the right stakeholders, responsibilities, and sources of useful information.

---

# Effective Communication During Interviews

Good interviews require more than asking questions.

The analyst also needs to communicate clearly and professionally with stakeholders.

When speaking with stakeholders, it helps to focus on:

- clarity
- empathy
- trust
- the purpose of the conversation
- next steps

---

# Four Steps for Communicating With Stakeholders

## 1. Express Empathy for the Stakeholder’s Perspective

### What It Means

Empathy means understanding and respecting the stakeholder’s feelings and point of view.

Some stakeholders may feel defensive during interviews, especially if the process being studied is not working well.

They may worry that:

- they are being judged
- they are being blamed
- their job performance is being criticized
- their role in the process is under threat

## Example Message

“I understand that this conversation might seem threatening, and I apologize if that is the case. I’m trying to understand how this process is currently structured, and if you have ideas for how to improve it, I’d love to learn from your expertise.”

## Important Lesson

Showing empathy helps reduce defensiveness and builds trust.

---

## 2. Disarm the Stakeholder When Appropriate

### What It Means

If a stakeholder is pushing back or acting adversarial, the analyst may need to calmly reduce tension.

This is not always necessary, but it can help when a stakeholder is resistant.

## Example Message

“I understand that we have different viewpoints and that you might think it’s a waste of time for me to investigate this process, and that’s okay. I’m just trying to learn, and I’d love to have more conversations so you can teach me what you know about this process.”

## Important Lesson

Disarming a stakeholder helps turn conflict into collaboration.

---

## 3. Clearly Communicate Your Task and Need

### What It Means

The analyst should clearly explain why they are reaching out and what they are trying to accomplish.

This helps the stakeholder understand the analyst’s role and intentions.

## Example Message

“My executive sponsor asked me to investigate this process and analyze how it is currently functioning. The goal is not to punish anyone or find someone to blame. We’re trying to improve things at a system level so that all stakeholders benefit.”

## Important Lesson

Clear communication helps stakeholders understand that the goal is improvement, not blame.

---

## 4. Provide Actionable Next Steps

### What It Means

After explaining the context, the analyst should tell the stakeholder what happens next.

This may include:

- asking a question
- scheduling a meeting
- sending materials to review
- requesting documents
- setting up a follow-up conversation

## Example Message

“Our manager asked me to explore this process and how it is currently functioning. Could we schedule some time this week to talk about it?”

## Important Lesson

Stakeholders should always know what the next step is.

---

# Why Interviews Matter

Interviewing stakeholders is often the backbone of the elicitation process.

Strong interviews can help the analyst:

- gather better requirements
- save time
- reduce confusion
- build stronger relationships
- improve stakeholder trust
- avoid wasted resources

---

# 4. Prototyping

## What It Is

**Prototyping** means creating a basic version or model of a solution so stakeholders can interact with it.

A prototype helps stakeholders understand the solution without needing to understand all the technical details.

## Why It Is Useful

Prototyping allows the analyst to:

- observe real user behavior
- gather feedback quickly
- test assumptions
- improve the solution through iterations
- see whether the proposed solution supports the requirements

## Low-Resolution Prototypes

Prototypes often start simple.

Examples include:

- paper drawings
- sketches
- cardboard models
- basic mockups

As the process continues, prototypes become more detailed and closer to the final solution.

---

# Hospital Scenario: Prototyping

## Situation

Patients have complained about hospital food.

During analysis, the team discovers that ceramic bowls and cups slow down order fulfillment because they require careful handling.

The team identifies more durable and shatterproof bowls and cups.

## Prototype Use

The team builds prototypes of the new bowls and cups to observe whether they improve performance.

The prototype helps answer:

- Do staff move orders faster?
- Are the new items easier to handle?
- Does the change improve the food delivery process?

## Possible Outcomes

If the findings support the hypothesis, the team may continue with the solution.

If not, the team may need to:

- review the analysis
- create a new prototype
- test again

### Important Lesson

Prototyping supports an iterative process where ideas are tested and improved.

---

# 5. Interface Analysis

## What It Is

**Interface analysis** studies how users interact with a product, system, or process.

It helps the analyst understand:

- user behavior
- pain points
- usability problems
- poor functionality
- process challenges

Interface analysis can also be done on competitors’ systems.

---

## Why It Is Useful

Interface analysis helps analysts see how people actually use a product or process, instead of only relying on what people say.

This can reveal problems that stakeholders may not mention directly.

---

# Hospital Scenario: Interface Analysis

## Situation

The hospital wants to improve food ordering and tracking.

The team believes that new, more user-friendly ordering software may help.

## Interface Analysis Use

Before fully building the software, the team creates a basic version that mimics the user interface and includes basic functions.

Stakeholders, such as hospital employees, test the prototype.

The analyst observes:

- how they use the tool
- where they struggle
- what they like
- what causes confusion
- what needs improvement

## Important Lesson

Interface analysis provides direct insight into real user behavior and helps improve the solution before full implementation.

---

# 6. Surveys and Questionnaires

## What They Are

**Surveys** and **questionnaires** are structured sets of questions sent to stakeholders.

They are useful when interviews, brainstorming, or meetings are not practical.

---

## Why Surveys Are Useful

Surveys help analysts:

- collect information remotely
- gather feedback from many people quickly
- ask specific questions
- keep responses focused on the project scope
- support asynchronous participation

## When Surveys Are Helpful

Surveys are useful when:

- stakeholders are in different locations
- time is limited
- many people need to be contacted
- in-person meetings would be too difficult
- the analyst wants focused answers

### Important Lesson

Surveys are scalable, but participation may be uneven.

---

# Pros and Cons of Elicitation Techniques

| Technique | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Brainstorming** | Collects many ideas and can produce creative or unexpected solutions. | Can become disorganized and distracting if not facilitated well. |
| **Focus Groups** | More focused and faster than large brainstorming sessions. | May not capture as many ideas as a larger group. |
| **Interviews** | Allows deep conversations with stakeholders. | Time-consuming and can be hard to schedule. |
| **Prototyping** | Allows real-time testing and quick adjustments. | Requires user time and feedback, which can be hard to organize. |
| **Interface Analysis** | Shows how users actually interact with a product or process. | Can take a lot of time and resources. |
| **Surveys/Questionnaires** | Quick and scalable way to gather information from many stakeholders. | Responses may be uneven and biased toward people willing to answer. |

---

# Choosing the Right Technique

There is no single best elicitation technique for every project.

The analyst should choose based on:

- project goals
- stakeholder availability
- project timeline
- complexity of the problem
- need for deep feedback
- number of stakeholders
- available resources

## Example

- Use interviews when you need detailed individual insight.
- Use surveys when you need feedback from many people quickly.
- Use focus groups when you need focused discussion.
- Use prototypes when stakeholders need to test a possible solution.
- Use interface analysis when user behavior matters.
- Use brainstorming when creativity and idea generation are important.

---

# Big Lesson

Requirements elicitation is not just collecting opinions.

It is a structured process where the analyst chooses the right technique to gather useful, accurate, and relevant information from stakeholders.

The quality of the final business requirements depends heavily on how well elicitation is done.

# Key Takeaways

- Requirement elicitation techniques help analysts gather business requirements.
- Common techniques include brainstorming, focus groups, interviews, prototyping, interface analysis, and surveys.
- Brainstorming is useful for generating many ideas.
- Focus groups are useful for focused discussion with selected stakeholders.
- Interviews are one of the most common and detailed elicitation methods.

- Clear communication helps reduce defensiveness and build trust.
- Prototyping allows stakeholders to test a model of the solution.
- Interface analysis shows how users interact with a product or process.
- Surveys are useful for gathering information from many people quickly.

- The analyst should choose techniques based on the project situation and stakeholder needs.

# Extra Important Notes

- Stakeholders may become defensive if they think they are being blamed.
- The analyst should explain that the goal is system improvement, not punishment.
- Interview notes should be shared with stakeholders to confirm accuracy.
- Prototypes do not need to be perfect at first.
- User behavior can reveal problems that interviews may miss.
- Surveys can be efficient, but responses may not represent every stakeholder equally.
- Strong elicitation improves the quality of the final solution.
