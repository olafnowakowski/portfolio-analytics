# Requirements Elicitation Process

## Main Idea

After understanding why business requirements matter, the next step is learning **how to gather them**.

The process of gathering business requirements is called **requirements elicitation**.

Requirements elicitation helps business analysts understand what stakeholders need, what problems exist in the current process, and what conditions a future solution must meet.

---

## What Is Requirements Elicitation?

**Requirements elicitation** is the process of collecting, understanding, analyzing, and confirming business requirements.

During this process, business analysts may:

- interview stakeholders
- analyze business processes
- review documentation
- study company systems
- gather feedback
- clarify business needs
- define what a solution must accomplish

### Important Lesson

Requirements elicitation helps create clarity for all stakeholders and guides the analyst toward the most realistic and useful solution.

---

# The 4 Steps of Requirements Elicitation

The requirements elicitation process can be organized into four main steps:

1. **Preparing for elicitation**
2. **Eliciting requirements**
3. **Analyzing requirements**
4. **Validating requirements**

---

# 1. Preparing for Elicitation

## What This Step Means

Before speaking directly with stakeholders, the business analyst needs to prepare by learning about the company, the project, and the business problem.

This helps the analyst ask better questions and understand the information they receive later.

---

## Documentation to Collect

Business analysts may collect documents and information such as:

### Company information

- business rules
- company structure
- legal requirements
- regulatory requirements
- internal processes

### Project details

- project scope
- business case
- previous work from other business analysts
- project background information

### Business intelligence

- market analysis
- competitor research
- KPI data
- business performance information

---

## Why Preparation Matters

Preparation helps the analyst understand:

- how the business works
- what the client or company needs
- what rules or limits affect the project
- what information already exists
- what areas need more investigation

---

## Subject Matter Experts

A business analyst may also need help from **subject matter experts**, also called **SMEs**.

### What Is an SME?

A **subject matter expert** is someone with deep knowledge of a specific part of the business.

SMEs may be:

- employees from specific departments
- technical experts
- outside consultants

### Why SMEs Are Useful

SMEs can help the analyst understand:

- technical documents
- complicated systems
- business rules
- department-specific processes

---

## Analyze Stakeholder Roles

During preparation, the analyst should review stakeholder roles.

A useful tool for this is the **power/interest grid**.

This helps identify which stakeholders are most important for requirements gathering.

### Important Lesson

Only include necessary stakeholders, especially those with high power and high interest.

Including too many people can make the analysis too broad and less focused.

---

## Connection to Problem Assessment

Some of this preparation may already have been done during the **problem assessment** phase.

However, if an analyst joins a project during the requirements elicitation phase, they should still review these steps to make sure they are properly prepared.

# Hospital Scenario: Preparing for Elicitation

## Scenario

A hospital has received complaints from patients and families about its food offerings.

The hospital brings in business analysts to help improve the food options without increasing costs too much.

---

## Questions to Ask During Preparation

The analyst should ask:

- How does the hospital food service currently work?
- What are the hospital’s business rules related to food service?
- What is the structure of the hospital’s food service department?
- What laws and regulations apply to hospital food service?
- What KPIs relate to food service, food quality, food delivery, and budget?
- Who are the stakeholders affected by this project?
- Which stakeholders have high power and high interest?
- Which stakeholders should be prioritized during analysis?

---

# 2. Eliciting Requirements

## What This Step Means

After preparation, the analyst begins directly collecting requirements from stakeholders.

This step involves interacting with stakeholders to understand their needs.

---

## What the Analyst Is Trying to Learn

During elicitation, the analyst tries to understand:

- what stakeholders need
- what outcomes they want
- what problems they experience
- what behaviors the final solution should have
- what outcomes should be avoided
- what pain points exist in the current process

---

## Important Lesson

At this stage, the analyst is not yet designing the solution.

The goal is to understand the problem from as many perspectives as possible.

---

# Hospital Scenario: Eliciting Requirements

For the hospital food service project, the analyst may interview stakeholders such as:

- hospital administration
- medical staff
- kitchen staff
- catering providers
- patients
- families

---

## What the Analyst Might Ask

The analyst may ask:

- How do you feel about the current food being served?
- What complaints have you heard most often?
- Are meals arriving on time?
- Are meals being delivered to the correct patients?
- Are dietary restrictions being handled correctly?
- What would an improved food service experience look like?
- What problems should the new process avoid?

---

## Why This Matters

These interviews help the analyst understand the food service problem through different viewpoints.

For example:

- patients may focus on taste and delivery time
- medical staff may focus on nutrition and dietary needs
- kitchen staff may focus on workflow and staffing
- administrators may focus on cost and efficiency

---

# 3. Analyzing Requirements

## What This Step Means

After gathering information, the analyst reviews the requirements and decides which ones are useful, realistic, and important.

The analyst must organize the information and remove requirements that do not belong in the final analysis.

---

## What Happens During Analysis

The analyst should:

- review all elicited requirements
- identify important themes
- remove requirements outside the project scope
- clarify vague requirements
- resolve contradictory requirements
- prioritize the most useful requirements
- settle on a clear set of requirements

---

## Handling Vague Requirements

If a requirement is unclear, the analyst should clarify it.

For example, if a stakeholder says:

- “The food quality is bad.”

The analyst needs to understand what that means.

It could mean:

- the food is not nutritious
- the food is cold
- the food is not fresh
- the food is not prepared properly
- there are not enough dietary options

---

## Handling Contradictory Requirements

Sometimes stakeholders disagree.

For example:

- some patients may like the food
- others may dislike it

This does not mean either group is wrong.

The analyst needs to look for patterns and decide which requirements are most relevant to the business problem.

---

# Hospital Scenario: Analyzing Requirements

In the hospital example, the analyst may discover that patients have different opinions about the food.

Some patients may enjoy it, while others may not.

But the analyst may also find a consistent issue:

- meals are arriving late
- orders are getting mixed up
- dietary needs are not always being met

These repeated themes can help confirm which requirements should be included in the final analysis.

---

## Important Lesson

Analyzing requirements helps turn a large amount of stakeholder feedback into a focused and usable list of requirements.

---

# 4. Validating Requirements

## What This Step Means

After analyzing requirements, the analyst must validate them.

**Validating requirements** means confirming that each requirement should be included in the project.

The analyst needs to make sure each requirement is clear, useful, realistic, and connected to the business problem.

---

# The 4 Validation Questions

For each requirement, the analyst should answer:

1. **Why?**
2. **What?**
3. **How?**
4. **When?**

---

## 1. Why?

### Question

Why is this requirement included?

### What It Helps Determine

This question helps explain:

- what problem the requirement solves
- why it matters
- how it connects to the business objective

### Hospital Example

If patients say food orders are arriving late, improving delivery speed may become a requirement.

Why?

Because faster delivery could:

- improve patient satisfaction
- keep food fresher
- reduce complaints

---

## 2. What?

### Question

What exactly does the stakeholder mean by this requirement?

### What It Helps Determine

This question helps clarify vague feedback.

### Hospital Example

If a patient says:

- “The food quality is poor.”

The analyst needs to ask what that means.

Possible meanings:

- the food is not healthy enough
- the food is not prepared well
- there are not enough dietary options
- the food is cold
- the food does not taste good

---

## 3. How?

### Question

How might this requirement be implemented?

### What It Helps Determine

This question helps identify:

- what process changes may be needed
- what resources may be required
- what obstacles could block implementation

### Hospital Example

To improve food delivery speed, the hospital may need a new order fulfillment workflow.

This could require:

- reassigning staff
- changing kitchen processes
- updating delivery procedures
- changing technology systems

This may create implementation challenges.

---

## 4. When?

### Question

When should implementation happen?

### What It Helps Determine

This question helps define the project timeline.

The analyst needs to consider:

- yearly priorities
- available resources
- deadlines
- whether the change is urgent
- whether the change can happen gradually

### Hospital Example

The analyst may need to determine whether the food service improvement must happen:

- before the end of the quarter
- before the fiscal year ends
- over several years

---

# Hospital Scenario: Validating Requirements

After answering why, what, how, and when for each requirement, the analyst will have much more clarity.

This helps the analyst create a better final list of business requirements.

# Full Requirements Elicitation Process Summary

## Step 1: Preparation

Focus:

- learn about the company
- review documentation
- understand KPIs
- analyze stakeholders
- prepare for stakeholder conversations

## Step 2: Elicitation

Focus:

- talk directly with stakeholders
- gather their needs
- understand pain points
- collect different perspectives

## Step 3: Analysis

Focus:

- review requirements
- remove unclear or unnecessary items
- resolve conflicts
- identify patterns
- choose a clear set of requirements

## Step 4: Validation

Focus:

- confirm each requirement belongs in the project
- answer why, what, how, and when
- make sure requirements are clear and realistic

---

# Big Lesson

Requirements elicitation is not just asking people what they want.

It is a structured process for preparing, gathering, analyzing, and validating information so the final requirements are useful and connected to the business problem.

Good elicitation helps the analyst avoid confusion, reduce project risk, and build a solution that meets real stakeholder needs.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Requirements elicitation is the process of gathering business requirements.
- It helps define what a solution must do to solve a business problem.
- The four steps are preparation, elicitation, analysis, and validation.
- Preparation includes learning about the company, reviewing documents, understanding KPIs, and analyzing stakeholders.
- Subject matter experts can help explain technical or complex information.
- Elicitation involves directly interacting with stakeholders to gather their needs and pain points.
- During elicitation, the analyst is not solving the problem yet; they are understanding it.
- Analysis helps organize feedback, remove unclear requirements, and resolve contradictions.
- Validation confirms that each requirement should be included.
- Each requirement should answer why, what, how, and when.
- A strong elicitation process creates clearer and more useful business requirements.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- The quality of requirements depends heavily on preparation.
- Stakeholders may describe symptoms, not the real problem.
- Vague requirements should always be clarified before moving forward.
- Contradictory feedback is normal and should be analyzed carefully.
- Requirements outside the project scope should be removed.
- Validation helps prevent unnecessary or unrealistic requirements from entering the final project plan.
- Good requirements lead to better solutions.

---

# Final Summary

Requirements elicitation is the process of gathering and refining business requirements. It includes four steps: preparing for elicitation, eliciting requirements, analyzing requirements, and validating requirements. During preparation, the analyst studies the company, KPIs, documents, and stakeholders. During elicitation, the analyst talks directly with stakeholders to understand their needs and pain points. During analysis, the analyst organizes the information, resolves vague or conflicting requirements, and narrows the list. During validation, the analyst confirms each requirement by answering why it matters, what it means, how it could be implemented, and when it should happen. This structured process helps create clear requirements that guide the project toward a realistic and useful solution.
