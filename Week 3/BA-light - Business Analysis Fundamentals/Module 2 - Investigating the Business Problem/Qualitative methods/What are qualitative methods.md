# Qualitative Methods for Problem Identification

How do qualitative and quantitative methods differ in their approach to business analysis?
- Qualitative methods focus on capturing subjective experiences and perceptions through methods like interviews and focus groups, whereas quantitative methods emphasize objective measurements and numerical data

## Main Idea

Sometimes numbers and large amounts of data are not enough to explain a business problem.

Quantitative data can show **what** is happening, but qualitative methods help explain **why** it is happening.

Qualitative methods focus on the human side of problems, such as:

- feelings
- opinions
- motivations
- perceptions
- frustrations
- experiences
- decision-making behavior

---

# Quantitative vs Qualitative Data

## Quantitative Data

Quantitative data is numerical.

Examples:

- customer satisfaction scores
- number of complaints
- sales figures
- survey ratings
- checkout wait times
- website visits

Quantitative data helps answer:

- What happened?
- How much did it change?
- How often does it happen?

---

## Qualitative Data

Qualitative data is descriptive and experience-based.

Examples:

- customer interviews
- focus group comments
- stories from customers
- observations of behavior
- employee feedback

Qualitative data helps answer:

- Why did it happen?
- How do people feel about it?
- What are customers really experiencing?
- What hidden problems exist?

---

# Why Qualitative Methods Matter

If a company has already done surveys and analyzed a lot of data but still does not know how to improve customer satisfaction, it may need qualitative methods.

Qualitative methods help uncover:

- root causes
- hidden frustrations
- emotional reactions
- customer expectations
- opportunities for improvement

### Important Lesson

Numbers can show that customers are unhappy, but qualitative methods can explain why they are unhappy.

---

# Main Qualitative Methods

The lesson discusses several qualitative methods:

- observation
- one-on-one interviews
- focus groups
- storytelling
- workshops

Each method helps the analyst understand the problem from a different perspective.

---

# Scenario: Sports Retail Store

## Situation

You manage a mid-sized retail store that sells:

- sports clothing
- exercise equipment

The store has a loyal customer base, but recently there has been:

- lower customer satisfaction
- more complaints about the checkout process

You know customers are unhappy, but you do not yet know the exact cause.

To understand the problem better, you decide to use qualitative methods.

---

# Step 1: Observation

## What You Do

You visit the store during rush hour and observe customers as they:

- shop
- look for items
- wait in line
- interact with checkout staff

You also observe:

- body language
- frustration
- heated exchanges with staff
- customer behavior in the checkout area

---

## Why Observation Helps

Observation helps you see the real customer experience as it happens.

Customers may not always explain their frustration clearly in surveys, but their behavior can reveal problems.

### Important Lesson

Observation can reveal problems that numbers alone may hide.

---

# Step 2: One-on-One Interviews

## What You Do

You conduct interviews with a diverse group of customers who recently visited the store.

These interviews allow customers to explain:

- their experiences
- their frustrations
- their expectations
- what they liked
- what they disliked

---

## What You Discover

Many customers feel overwhelmed by the checkout process.

Common complaints include:

- checkout is difficult during peak hours
- it takes too long to buy just one item
- queues are too long
- it is hard to locate items quickly

### Important Lesson

Interviews help uncover specific customer pain points in detail.

---

# Step 3: Focus Groups

## What You Do

You organize a focus group with:

- regular customers
- first-time customers

The goal is to hear different customer perspectives.

Participants discuss:

- store strengths
- store weaknesses
- shopping experience
- checkout process
- staff interactions

---

## What You Discover

A new theme appears:

- customers mention unfriendly staff interactions

This becomes an important insight because it was not the only issue at first.

### Important Lesson

Focus groups can reveal patterns and themes that may not appear in individual interviews.

---

# Step 4: Storytelling

## What Storytelling Means

Storytelling involves asking customers to share personal stories about their experiences.

These stories may include:

- positive interactions
- negative interactions
- emotional moments
- frustrating checkout experiences
- helpful or unhelpful staff encounters

---

## Why Storytelling Helps

Stories provide context and emotional detail.

They help the analyst understand how the checkout process affects customer satisfaction on a deeper level.

### Important Lesson

Storytelling helps reveal the emotional impact of a business problem.

---

# Key Findings from Qualitative Research

After observation, interviews, focus groups, and storytelling, the manager identifies several key problems:

- checkout process is too slow
- checkout area is poorly organized
- queues are too long during peak hours
- customers struggle to find items
- staff interactions can feel unfriendly
- customers want a smoother and faster shopping experience

---

# Step 5: Workshop for Solutions

## What You Do

After gathering insights, you organize a workshop to present findings and develop solutions.

You include people close to the problem, such as:

- sales staff
- customer support staff
- Thomas from the IT department

Including IT gives a different perspective and helps create a more complete solution.

---

## Why Workshops Help

Workshops allow teams to:

- review findings together
- brainstorm solutions
- combine different perspectives
- decide on practical improvements
- create shared ownership of the solution

---

# Solutions Created from the Qualitative Insights

The team decides on a multifaceted approach.

This means the solution has several parts instead of just one fix.

---

## 1. Improve the Checkout Area

Based on interview and focus group feedback, the team restructures the checkout area.

Goals:

- improve customer flow
- reduce wait times
- make the checkout process less stressful

---

## 2. Add Self-Checkout Stations

The store plans to install more self-checkout stations.

This helps customers who:

- only want to buy one item
- do not want to wait in a long line
- prefer faster checkout options

---

## 3. Train Staff for Peak Hours

The store schedules staff training on helping customers more efficiently during busy times.

Training focuses on:

- assisting customers quickly
- reducing checkout delays
- managing queues
- improving customer service

---

## 4. Improve Staff Communication and Empathy

Because unfriendly staff interactions were a recurring theme, the store invests in training programs focused on:

- empathy
- problem-solving
- effective communication
- customer support

The goal is to empower employees to solve customer issues quickly.

---

## 5. Improve Inventory Management

Customers also had trouble finding items.

To solve this, the store implements a new inventory management system.

The system provides real-time updates on:

- stock levels
- item locations

---

## 6. Improve Store Navigation

The store adds:

- clear signage
- aisle markers

This helps customers find what they need more easily.

---

## 7. Create a Customer Feedback Mechanism

The store creates a way for customers to continue giving feedback.

Customers are rewarded with vouchers for suggestions about improving service.

The store also welcomes and responds to complaints quickly.

### Important Lesson

Customer feedback should not be a one-time activity. It should be continuous.

---

# Results

Over time, the qualitative methods and strategic changes lead to improvements in:

- customer satisfaction
- customer loyalty
- shopping experience
- staff interactions
- checkout speed
- ease of finding products

Customers appreciate that the store listens to their feedback and takes action.

---

# Big Lesson

Qualitative methods help businesses understand customer experience from multiple perspectives.

They allow analysts and managers to move beyond numbers and uncover the real causes of dissatisfaction.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Quantitative data shows what is happening.
- Qualitative data helps explain why it is happening.
- Qualitative methods explore emotions, opinions, motivations, and perceptions.
- Observation helps reveal real customer behavior.
- Interviews provide detailed individual experiences.
- Focus groups help identify shared themes and patterns.
- Storytelling reveals emotional context and deeper customer experiences.
- Workshops help teams turn insights into solutions.
- In the sports retail store example, the main issues were slow checkout, long queues, hard-to-find items, and unfriendly staff interactions.
- The solution included checkout redesign, self-checkout stations, staff training, inventory improvements, better signage, and ongoing feedback.
- Qualitative insights can turn customer experience problems into growth opportunities.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Large datasets may still fail to explain customer dissatisfaction.
- Customers may describe problems differently in interviews than they do in surveys.
- Observing real behavior can reveal pain points customers forget to mention.
- Emotional experiences matter because they strongly affect customer loyalty.
- Including people from different departments can lead to better solutions.
- Continuous feedback helps a company keep improving.
- Qualitative methods are especially useful when the problem involves people, behavior, emotions, or experience.

---

# Final Summary

This lesson explains how qualitative methods help business analysts and managers understand the human side of business problems. While quantitative data can show that customer satisfaction is declining, qualitative methods help explain why. In the sports retail store example, observation, interviews, focus groups, storytelling, and workshops revealed that customers were frustrated by long checkout lines, difficulty finding items, and unfriendly staff interactions. The store responded by improving the checkout area, adding self-checkout stations, training staff, improving inventory systems, adding signage, and creating a continuous feedback system. The main lesson is that qualitative methods help uncover root causes and hidden opportunities by exploring real customer experiences and emotions.
