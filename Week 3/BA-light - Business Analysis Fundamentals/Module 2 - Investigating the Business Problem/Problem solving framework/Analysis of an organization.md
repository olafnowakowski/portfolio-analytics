### Why do you need to perform an organizational analysis? 
- To enable informed decision-making and strategic planning
> Performing organization analysis before deciding on the competitive strategy allows us to understand the organization's internal capabilities and limitations (strengths and weaknesses) and assess the influence of external factors such as market trends, competitor actions, and regulatory changes.

# Strategic Analysis Techniques for Business Problems

## Main Idea

When a business faces a serious challenge, such as a new competitor entering the market, the owner or analyst needs to carefully study the situation before making decisions.

In this example, you own a successful restaurant on a busy street, but a new competitor may soon open nearby.

You need to decide what to do:

- sell the business
- prepare to compete
- change or pivot the business strategy
- improve your current operations

To make the right decision, you need to use **strategic analysis techniques**.

---

# Why Strategic Analysis Matters

The restaurant industry is highly competitive.

Success depends not only on good food, but also on:

- strong strategy
- customer experience
- financial health
- competitive positioning
- employee culture
- ability to adapt to market changes

Strategic analysis helps you understand your current position and choose the best next move.

---

# Main Strategic Analysis Techniques

The lesson focuses on three useful techniques:

1. **Financial Analysis**
2. **Organizational Culture Assessment**
3. **SWOT Analysis**

These tools help you understand the business from different angles.

---

# 1. Financial Analysis

## What It Is

**Financial analysis** helps you understand the financial health and performance of your restaurant.

It helps answer questions like:

- Is the restaurant profitable?
- Are costs too high?
- Are sales increasing or decreasing?
- Is cash flow healthy?
- How does the restaurant compare to competitors?

---

## Important Financial Metrics to Examine

### Revenue

Revenue is the money your restaurant earns from sales.

You should review:

- total sales
- sales by time period
- sales by menu item
- sales trends over time
- peak demand periods
- low-demand periods

### Why Revenue Matters

Revenue analysis helps identify:

- when customers buy the most
- which products or services perform best
- where there may be opportunities for growth

---

## Profitability

Profitability shows how much money the restaurant keeps after costs.

You should review:

- profit margins
- revenue compared to expenses
- whether the business model is sustainable

### Why Profitability Matters

A restaurant can have high sales but still struggle if costs are too high.

Profitability analysis helps identify opportunities to reduce costs and improve financial performance.

---

## Cost Structure

Cost structure means breaking down all restaurant expenses.

Important costs include:

- cost of goods sold, also called **COGS**
- labor costs
- rent
- utilities
- overhead expenses
- operating expenses

---

## Cost of Goods Sold

**COGS** means the direct cost of producing the food and drinks sold by the restaurant.

Examples:

- ingredients
- raw food materials
- packaging, if applicable

### Why COGS Matters

If COGS is too high, the restaurant may lose profitability even if sales are strong.

---

## Fixed and Variable Costs

### Fixed Costs

Fixed costs stay mostly the same regardless of how many customers you serve.

Examples:

- rent
- insurance
- licenses
- some salaries

### Variable Costs

Variable costs change depending on sales volume.

Examples:

- ingredients
- hourly labor
- supplies
- packaging

### Important Lesson

Understanding fixed and variable costs helps you identify where the restaurant can improve efficiency.

---

## Cash Flow

Cash flow measures the movement of money in and out of the business.

You should analyze the restaurant’s cash flow statement to monitor:

- money coming in
- money going out
- rent payments
- payroll
- supplier payments
- capital expenses

### Why Cash Flow Matters

Even a profitable restaurant can struggle if it does not have enough cash available to pay bills on time.

Maintaining cash reserves and managing cash flow protects financial stability.

---

# Competitive Benchmarking

## What It Is

**Competitive benchmarking** means comparing your restaurant’s performance to industry standards or competitors.

You might compare metrics such as:

- average bill size
- table turnover rate
- profit margins
- sales volume
- customer satisfaction
- labor cost percentage

---

## Why Benchmarking Matters

Benchmarking helps you understand:

- how your restaurant compares to competitors
- whether your pricing is competitive
- whether your operations are efficient
- where improvement is needed

---

## Where to Find Benchmarking Data

Useful sources may include:

- industry reports
- trade associations
- industry organizations
- market research platforms
- business databases
- competitor financial reports, if available

### Important Lesson

If you can access competitor financial information, you should analyze it because it may help shape your future strategy.

---

# 2. Organizational Culture Assessment

## What It Is

**Organizational culture assessment** means studying the values, behaviors, communication patterns, and work environment inside the restaurant.

In hospitality, culture is very important because employees directly affect the customer experience.

---

## Why Culture Matters in Restaurants

Restaurant employees interact with customers every day.

A strong culture can improve:

- customer service
- employee motivation
- teamwork
- employee retention
- customer loyalty
- dining experience

---

## Factors to Assess

When studying restaurant culture, examine:

- leadership and management style
- employee engagement
- employee satisfaction
- customer experience
- brand reputation
- team collaboration
- communication between staff

---

## Benefits of a Strong Organizational Culture

A strong culture promotes:

- employee engagement
- employee satisfaction
- employee retention
- better teamwork
- stronger collaboration
- customer-centered service

This can lead to:

- attentive service
- personalized customer interactions
- memorable dining experiences
- repeat customers

---

## Culture and Innovation

Restaurants with a culture of experimentation and agility are better able to adapt.

They may be more willing to:

- test new menu items
- use new technologies
- respond to customer trends
- take smart risks
- differentiate from competitors

### Important Lesson

A restaurant with a flexible and innovative culture can respond better when a new competitor enters the market.

---

# 3. SWOT Analysis

## What It Is

**SWOT analysis** is a strategic framework used to study:

- **Strengths**
- **Weaknesses**
- **Opportunities**
- **Threats**

It helps a business understand both internal and external factors.

---

# Strengths

## Meaning

Strengths are the things your restaurant does well.

They are what set your restaurant apart from competitors.

## Examples of Strengths

- loyal customer base
- unique cuisine
- excellent customer service
- prime location
- strong brand reputation
- talented chef or staff
- high-quality ingredients
- popular menu items

### Why Strengths Matter

Identifying strengths helps you know what to protect and leverage when competition increases.

---

# Weaknesses

## Meaning

Weaknesses are internal problems or areas where the restaurant needs improvement.

## Examples of Weaknesses

- limited marketing budget
- inconsistent food quality
- operational inefficiencies
- confusing menu
- outdated decor
- slow service
- weak online presence
- poor staff communication

### Why Weaknesses Matter

Recognizing weaknesses allows you to fix internal problems before competitors take advantage of them.

---

# Opportunities

## Meaning

Opportunities are external chances for growth or improvement.

A new competitor may create pressure, but it can also push the restaurant to improve.

## Examples of Opportunities

- diversifying the menu
- expanding delivery services
- launching a loyalty program
- improving online ordering
- offering catering
- using emerging food trends
- adding organic options
- offering farm-to-table dishes
- adding vegetarian or vegan items

### Why Opportunities Matter

Capitalizing on opportunities helps the restaurant stay competitive and adapt to changing customer preferences.

---

# Threats

## Meaning

Threats are external risks that could hurt the business.

## Examples of Threats

- a new competitor opening nearby
- customers switching to another restaurant
- economic downturns
- changing consumer preferences
- rising food costs
- negative online reviews
- labor shortages
- rent increases
- supply chain issues

### Why Threats Matter

Identifying threats helps you prepare contingency plans and reduce risk.

---

# Example: New Competitor on the Same Street

If a new restaurant opens nearby, it could threaten your business by:

- taking some of your customers
- reducing your market share
- forcing price competition
- attracting attention with new menu items
- creating stronger customer expectations

A SWOT analysis can help you decide how to respond.

---

- whether the business is healthy

- whether revenue is growing or shrinking
- whether cash flow is stable
- how you compare financially to competitors

## Organizational Culture Assessment Helps You Understand

- whether employees are engaged
- whether service quality is strong
- whether the team can adapt
- whether leadership supports innovation
- whether the culture improves customer experience

## SWOT Analysis Helps You Understand

- what the restaurant does well
- what needs improvement
- where growth opportunities exist
- what risks could harm the business

# Big Lesson

Strategic analysis helps business owners and analysts make better decisions.

Instead of reacting emotionally to a new competitor, you can use data and structured thinking to choose the best response.

- Do not respond to competition without analyzing your current position first.
