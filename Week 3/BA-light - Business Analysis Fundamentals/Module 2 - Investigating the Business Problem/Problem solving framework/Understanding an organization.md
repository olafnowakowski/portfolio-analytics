# Understanding the Organization Before Solving Problems

## Main Idea

Before you can fully understand a business problem, you need to understand the organization where the problem is happening.

A problem does not exist by itself. It exists inside a specific business environment, with its own culture, structure, goals, policies, finances, and people.

### Important Lesson

To solve a problem well, a business analyst must understand both:

- the problem itself
- the context that created the problem

---

# Why Organizational Context Matters

When a company has a complex problem, such as:

- declining sales
- poor communication
- process inefficiency
- low morale
- customer complaints

it is not enough to only look at the surface issue.

You also need to ask:

- What situation led to this problem?
- How does the company operate?
- Who makes decisions?
- What rules guide the work?
- What is the company trying to achieve?
- What cultural or financial pressures might be involved?

---

# Understanding the Organization

Understanding an organization means learning more than just what the company sells or does.

You need to understand:

- who the company is
- how it operates
- where it is heading
- what it values
- how people communicate
- how decisions are made
- what constraints affect the business

---

# Key Documents to Review

A business analyst can start understanding an organization by reviewing important documents.

These documents help explain the company’s purpose, structure, goals, processes, and challenges.

---

## 1. Mission and Vision Statements

### What They Are

Mission and vision statements describe the organization’s purpose and future direction.

They act like a compass for the company.

### Why They Matter

They help you understand:

- the company’s core values
- long-term goals
- overall purpose
- what the organization wants to become

### Simple Explanation

The mission explains why the company exists.  
The vision explains where the company wants to go.

---

## 2. Strategic Plans

### What They Are

Strategic plans outline the company’s goals and initiatives for success.

They explain what the organization is trying to accomplish and how it plans to get there.

### Why They Matter

Strategic plans help analysts align problem-solving efforts with the company’s long-term direction.

### Example

A healthcare company may want to provide minor injury express care in easily accessible locations.

Its goals may include:

- increasing access to care
- improving accessibility
- improving patient experience
- maintaining high-quality medical care
- building brand recognition in the community

### Important Lesson

A solution should support the company’s strategy, not work against it.

---

## 3. Organizational Charts

### What They Are

Organizational charts show the company’s structure.

They usually display:

- departments
- reporting lines
- hierarchy
- roles
- responsibilities
- key decision-makers

### Why They Matter

Organizational charts help analysts understand:

- who reports to whom
- who has decision-making power
- who owns certain processes
- how communication flows
- which stakeholders may matter most

### Simple Explanation

An organizational chart helps you understand the “cast of characters” in the company and how they relate to each other.

---

## 4. Policies and Procedures

### What They Are

Policies and procedures explain the rules for how work gets done.

They define the company’s:

- dos and don’ts
- legal requirements
- compliance expectations
- operational standards
- employee guidelines

### Why They Matter

They help analysts understand:

- company culture
- work norms
- process limits
- legal or compliance restrictions
- how employees are expected to behave

### Important Risk

If an analyst does not understand company policies, they might recommend a solution that violates rules or creates resistance.

---

## Examples of Policies

Examples may include:

- remote work policies
- customer data policies
- security policies
- privacy policies
- employee conduct policies
- approval procedures

### Important Lesson

A solution must fit within company policies and procedures unless changing those policies is part of the approved project.

---

## 5. Service Blueprints

### What They Are

A **service blueprint** is a visual tool that maps the full customer journey and the internal processes that support it.

It shows:

- customer touchpoints
- front-stage interactions
- behind-the-scenes operations
- people involved
- systems involved
- supporting processes

### Why They Matter

Service blueprints help analysts see how a service actually works from beginning to end.

They show both:

- what the customer sees
- what happens behind the scenes

### Important Lesson

Service blueprints help reveal where problems may occur in the customer experience or internal workflow.

---

## 6. Financial Reports

### What They Are

Financial reports show the company’s financial health.

They may include information about:

- revenue
- expenses
- profitability
- cash flow
- budgets
- financial challenges

### Why They Matter

Financial reports help analysts understand:

- company priorities
- financial pressure
- business constraints
- opportunities
- risks

### Important Phrase

“Follow the money” means that understanding finances often helps explain how decisions are made.

---

# Understanding Company Culture

Company culture is harder to understand than documents because it is not always written down.

Culture includes:

- how people communicate
- how decisions are made
- how leaders behave
- how employees interact
- what behaviors are rewarded
- what employees believe about the company

---

## How to Learn About Company Culture

### 1. Observe the Workplace

Watch:

- office dynamics
- team interactions
- nonverbal cues
- meeting behavior
- how people communicate formally and informally

### 2. Talk to Employees

Have conversations during:

- coffee breaks
- team meetings
- casual gatherings
- workshops
- company events

Ask open-ended questions about:

- employee experiences
- company values
- communication patterns
- perceptions of company culture

### 3. Build Relationships

Connect with people across:

- departments
- teams
- roles
- hierarchy levels

This helps you understand the organization from more than one point of view.

### 4. Observe Leadership

Leaders strongly influence culture.

Pay attention to whether leaders:

- encourage open dialogue
- ask for feedback
- support collaboration
- communicate clearly
- micromanage employees
- create trust or fear

---

# Researching Company Culture

Observation is important, but research also helps.

You can look at employee comments on websites such as:

- Glassdoor
- The Muse
- Indeed
- Rate My Employer
- other employee review platforms

### Important Warning

Do not rely only on what someone inside the organization tells you about the culture.

To understand culture properly, you need to:

- research it
- observe it
- compare multiple perspectives

---

# Why Understanding the Company Helps Problem-Solving

Understanding the organization helps you:

- identify root causes
- tailor solutions to the company’s reality
- anticipate how solutions will affect people
- avoid resistance
- understand constraints
- evaluate whether the solution works
- make recommendations that fit the organization

---

# Example: Declining Sales

Imagine you are asked to solve a problem with declining sales.

Without understanding the company, you might suggest generic solutions such as:

- team-building exercises
- sales training
- employee recognition programs

But these may not solve the real issue.

---

## Deeper Organizational Context

After studying the organization, you might discover that sales are declining because of:

- low morale in the sales team
- lack of transparency in decision-making
- employees feeling micromanaged
- company cash flow problems
- leadership pressure to improve sales quickly
- managers reacting by controlling the sales team too closely

### Important Lesson

The visible problem may be declining sales, but the root cause could be connected to culture, leadership, finances, or communication.

---

# Why Generic Solutions May Fail

Generic solutions often fail because they do not address the actual cause of the problem.

For example:

- sales training will not fix low morale caused by micromanagement
- team-building will not fix cash flow pressure
- recognition programs will not fix poor communication from leadership

### Important Lesson

Solutions must be designed for the organization’s specific situation.

---

# What a Business Analyst Should Do

A business analyst should:

- study the organization before proposing solutions
- review key documents
- understand company goals
- identify decision-makers
- learn policies and procedures
- analyze financial conditions
- observe company culture
- talk to employees
- challenge assumptions
- connect the problem to the company’s unique environment

---

# Big Lesson

A problem cannot be fully understood without understanding the organization around it.

A business analyst needs to look at the bigger picture before recommending a solution.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Before understanding a business problem, you need to understand the organization.
- Organizational context includes structure, culture, goals, policies, procedures, finances, and people.
- Mission and vision statements show the company’s purpose and direction.
- Strategic plans show long-term goals and priorities.
- Organizational charts show reporting lines, departments, and decision-makers.
- Policies and procedures explain how work is supposed to happen.
- Service blueprints show the customer journey and supporting internal processes.
- Financial reports reveal priorities, constraints, and risks.
- Company culture should be studied through observation, conversations, and research.
- Leadership behavior strongly shapes organizational culture.
- Generic solutions may fail if they do not match the company’s real situation.
- Understanding the organization helps analysts identify root causes and design better solutions.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Context matters as much as the problem itself.
- A business problem may be caused by hidden organizational issues.
- Written documents explain the official organization, but observation reveals how the company really works.
- Culture can affect whether a solution succeeds or fails.
- Financial pressure can influence leadership behavior and employee morale.
- Good problem-solving requires understanding both formal systems and informal dynamics.
- A strong business analyst does not only ask “What is the problem?” but also “What environment created this problem?”

---

# Final Summary

This lesson explains that before solving a business problem, a business analyst must understand the organization where the problem exists. This means reviewing mission and vision statements, strategic plans, organizational charts, policies, procedures, service blueprints, and financial reports. It also means studying company culture through observation, conversations, networking, leadership behavior, and external research. Understanding the company helps analysts identify root causes, tailor solutions, anticipate impacts, and evaluate effectiveness. Without this context, analysts may recommend generic solutions that fail to address the real issue. The main lesson is that effective problem-solving depends on understanding the organization as deeply as the problem itself.
