# Defining the Right Business Problem

## Main Idea

The success of a project often depends on identifying the **right problem** before trying to solve it.

A business analyst should not rush into solutions. Instead, they should spend time understanding the real problem, its root cause, and the people affected by it.

Albert Einstein is quoted as saying:

> Given one hour to save the world, I would spend 59 minutes defining the problem.

The message is that defining the problem correctly is often more important than jumping quickly into action.

---

# Why Problem Definition Matters

In fast-paced business situations, people often want to solve problems quickly.

However, a common mistake is to:

- jump to conclusions
- focus on symptoms
- ignore the root cause
- create solutions that do not solve the real issue

A good business analyst helps prevent this by asking deeper questions and challenging assumptions.

---

# Symptom vs Root Cause

A symptom is a visible sign of a problem.

A root cause is the real reason the problem is happening.

## Simple Example: Blister on Your Foot

If you have a blister, you could put a band-aid on it.

But the band-aid only treats the symptom.

The real problem may be uncomfortable shoes.

A better solution would be to change your shoes.

### Important Lesson

Treating symptoms may give temporary relief, but fixing the root cause prevents the problem from continuing.

---

# Business Example 1: Pharmaceutical Company

## Scenario

A large pharmaceutical company is experiencing declining employee productivity.

Because productivity is dropping, the drug approval process is taking longer and longer.

---

## First Reaction

The first assumption might be:

- employees are slow
- the process needs automation
- creating an online system for drug documentation will speed things up

So the company might decide to invest in automation.

---

## What a Business Analyst Might Discover

A skilled business analyst may investigate more deeply and find that the real issue is not individual productivity or the overall process itself.

Instead, the root cause may be:

- poor quality documentation
- incomplete documentation
- inconsistent information
- too many checks needed to collect missing details

---

## Better Solution

Instead of immediately automating the process, the company could:

- create a standardized template for drug documentation
- make sure documentation is consistent and complete
- train employees through workshops
- teach employees how to fill out the templates accurately

---

## Why This Matters

If the company focused only on automation, it might spend time and money on a solution that does not fix the real issue.

### Incorrect focus:
- automation updates

### Real need:
- better documentation quality
- fewer unnecessary checks
- clearer employee training

### Important Lesson

Solving the wrong problem wastes resources and leaves the true issue unresolved.

# Business Example 2: The Turkana Frozen Fish Project

## Scenario

In 1971, the World Bank and the Norwegian government worked together on a project in Kenya.

The goal was to improve the lives of the Turkana people, a semi-nomadic tribe living near Lake Turkana.

---

## Project Idea

The project planned for Turkana fishermen to catch fish such as:

- Nile perch
- tilapia

The fish would then be:

- frozen
- sold in Kenyan cities
- exported to other African countries

---

## Investment

The project invested:

- $22 million

This money was used to build a large fish processing plant.

---

## Expected Outcome

The expected results were:

- more fishing jobs
- more fish processing jobs
- higher incomes
- reduced local poverty

At first, this sounded like a good idea.

---

# Why the Project Failed

The project was a major failure.

The fish processing plant closed shortly after completion, making the investment mostly worthless.

---

## The Core Mistake

The project focused on fixing local poverty, which was treated as the main problem.

But poverty was more of a symptom.

The project did not properly understand the real cultural, economic, and practical context of the Turkana people.

---

# Problems the Project Missed

## 1. Turkana Culture Did Not Support Fishing

The Turkana people were semi-nomadic.

They mainly lived on:

- milk
- meat from cattle

They rarely ate fish.

Fishing was also considered a low-status or derogatory occupation.

Only local Turkana people who did not own cattle were likely to fish.

### Important Lesson

A solution that conflicts with local culture is unlikely to succeed.

---

## 2. The Fish Freezing Plant Required Too Much Electricity

The fish processing plant needed more electricity than was available in the entire Turkana district.

This made the plant impractical to operate.

---

## 3. Refrigeration Costs Were Too High

Because refrigeration was expensive, the frozen fish became too costly.

As a result, people could not afford to buy it.

---

## 4. Fishing Was Seasonal and Unsustainable

A deeper analysis would have shown that fishing in the area was not a stable, long-term economic solution.

---

## 5. The Project Made the Situation Worse

The consultants moved many Turkana people to the lakeshore, gave them boats, and taught them to fish.

When the project failed, those people had no reliable way to support themselves.

Many had to depend on food aid to survive.

### Important Lesson

A poorly designed solution can make the original problem worse.

---

# What Should Have Happened

A proper business analysis should have included:

- deeper investigation
- feasibility testing
- engagement with all concerned parties
- understanding local culture
- understanding economic realities
- challenging assumptions
- asking whether the proposed solution was practical

If this had happened, the project likely would not have been considered viable.

---

# Consequences of Misidentifying a Problem

Misidentifying a business problem can lead to:

- wasted money
- wasted time
- misplaced effort
- failed projects
- solutions that do not work
- damage to affected communities or stakeholders
- problems becoming worse

---

# Role of the Business Analyst

A key responsibility of the business analyst is to make sure the team identifies the right problem.

Business analysts should:

- engage all relevant parties early
- ask probing questions
- challenge assumptions
- understand different perspectives
- uncover hidden challenges
- study the business environment carefully
- avoid superficial fixes
- define the problem clearly before recommending solutions

---

# Journey Without a Map Analogy

Starting a project without a clear problem definition is like going on a journey without a map.

You may arrive somewhere, but it might not be the right destination.

### Important Lesson

A project can be completed and still fail if it solves the wrong problem.

---

# Why Stakeholder Engagement Matters

Engaging all relevant parties helps the analyst gain a fuller understanding of the situation.

Different stakeholders can reveal:

- hidden challenges
- cultural issues
- process problems
- practical limitations
- risks
- assumptions that may be wrong

### Important Lesson

Limited viewpoints can lead to incorrect problem definitions.

---

# Challenging Assumptions

Business analysts must challenge assumptions because stakeholders may already believe they know the problem.

For example:

- The pharmaceutical company may assume productivity is the issue.
- The World Bank may assume poverty can be solved through fishing jobs.

But both assumptions may be incomplete or wrong.

### Important Lesson

Assumptions should be tested before solutions are built.

---

# Big Lesson

The best business solutions come from solving the right problem, not just acting quickly.

A strong business analyst helps make sure the team does not confuse symptoms with root causes.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Project success depends on identifying the right problem.
- Business analysts should spend time defining the problem before trying to solve it.
- A symptom is not the same as a root cause.
- Solving symptoms may not fix the actual issue.
- In the pharmaceutical example, automation seemed like the solution, but the real issue was poor documentation quality.
- In the Turkana project, poverty was treated as the problem, but the proposed fishing solution ignored culture, infrastructure, cost, and sustainability.
- Misidentifying a problem can waste resources and cause project failure.
- Poorly planned solutions can make the situation worse.
- Business analysts should engage stakeholders early, ask deeper questions, and challenge assumptions.
- A proper problem definition helps prevent superficial fixes.
- The right solution must address the real problem, not just the visible symptoms.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Fast action is not useful if it is aimed at the wrong problem.
- Root cause analysis is critical in business analysis.
- Stakeholders may experience the problem differently, so multiple perspectives matter.
- Feasibility should be tested before investing heavily in a solution.
- Cultural, economic, technical, and operational realities must be considered.
- A business analyst should act like an investigator, not just a solution provider.
- A well-defined problem creates the foundation for a successful project.

---

# Final Summary

This lesson explains why correctly defining the business problem is one of the most important responsibilities of a business analyst. Many projects fail because teams rush to fix symptoms instead of understanding the root cause. The pharmaceutical company example shows how a business might wrongly invest in automation when the real issue is poor documentation quality. The Turkana frozen fish project shows how a large project failed because it ignored cultural, economic, and infrastructure realities. In both cases, better problem analysis could have prevented wasted resources and poor outcomes. The main lesson is that business analysts must engage stakeholders, ask probing questions, challenge assumptions, and clearly define the real problem before proposing solutions.
