# Root Cause Analysis and the Five Whys

## Main Idea

When solving business problems, it is important to find the **root cause**, not just fix the visible symptoms.

A quick fix may make the problem look solved for a short time, but if the real cause is still there, the problem will continue.

---

## Simple Example: Leaking Pipe

Imagine there is a leaking pipe and water is on the floor.

A bad solution would be:

- deliver a robotic floor mop
- clean the water quickly
- say the problem is fixed

But the real problem is not the wet floor.

The real problem is:

- the pipe is still leaking

### Important Lesson

The wet floor is only a **symptom**.  
The leaking pipe is the **root cause**.

If you only clean the floor, the water will keep coming back.

---

# Symptom vs Root Cause

## Symptom

A **symptom** is the visible effect of a problem.

Examples:

- wet floor
- declining sales
- late deliveries
- customer complaints
- low productivity

## Root Cause

A **root cause** is the real underlying reason the problem exists.

Examples:

- leaking pipe
- poor product quality
- bad process design
- unclear documentation
- wrong materials
- lack of training

### Important Lesson

Solving symptoms gives temporary relief.  
Solving the root cause creates a lasting solution.

---

# What Is Root Cause Analysis?

## Definition

**Root Cause Analysis**, also called **RCA**, is a structured method used to find the underlying cause of a problem.

Instead of only looking at symptoms, RCA helps analysts:

- investigate deeper
- identify contributing factors
- understand why the problem exists
- find the fundamental cause
- recommend a lasting solution

---

## Why Root Cause Analysis Matters

RCA is useful because it helps teams avoid:

- guessing
- jumping to conclusions
- wasting resources
- solving the wrong problem
- repeating the same issue again and again

### Important Lesson

Understanding the **why** behind the **what** is critical when solving complex problems.

---

# The Five Whys Technique

## What It Is

The **Five Whys** is a problem identification technique used in root cause analysis.

It works by repeatedly asking:

- **Why?**

The goal is to move past the first obvious answer and uncover deeper causes.

---

## Why It Works

The first answer usually identifies a symptom or surface-level issue.

By asking “why” multiple times, the analyst can uncover:

- deeper causes
- process failures
- hidden connections
- the real source of the problem

### Important Lesson

The Five Whys helps peel back layers of a problem until the root cause becomes clearer.

---

# Real-Life Business Example: Declining Sales

## Scenario

A small but successful company starts experiencing declining sales.

The sales team works hard, but sales continue to drop.

Management tries several quick fixes:

- extra bonuses
- more sales training
- daily motivational talks

But none of these solve the problem.

---

## Initial Assumption

Management may assume the issue is with the sales team.

They may think:

- salespeople are not motivated enough
- salespeople need more training
- salespeople are not working hard enough

But this may only focus on symptoms.

---

# Jane the CEO Uses Root Cause Analysis

Jane, the CEO, decides not to guess.

Instead, she chooses to use root cause analysis to find the real reason behind the sales decline.

She creates a diverse team from different departments:

- sales
- marketing
- customer service
- product development

---

## Why a Cross-Functional Team Helps

A cross-functional team is useful because each department sees the problem from a different perspective.

For example:

- sales may understand customer objections
- marketing may understand market messaging
- customer service may hear complaints
- product development may understand product quality and materials

### Important Lesson

Different perspectives reduce the chance of missing an important cause.

---

# Step 1: Define the Problem Clearly

The team begins by defining the problem:

- sales are declining

They do not immediately decide why sales are declining.

Instead, they gather facts and begin asking questions.

---

# Step 2: Use the Five Whys

The team starts asking why.

## Five Whys Example

### 1. Why are sales declining?

Because customers are purchasing less than before.

### 2. Why are customers buying less?

Because product quality has decreased.

### 3. Why has product quality decreased?

Because the company changed the materials used in manufacturing.

### 4. Why did the materials change?

Because material prices increased sharply in the past six months.

### 5. Why did material prices increase?

Because the company extended its payment terms to 90 days.

---

# What the Team Discovered

The team discovered that declining sales were not caused by poor sales effort or weak marketing.

The real issue was connected to product quality.

## Root Cause Chain

The chain looked like this:

1. The company extended payment terms to 90 days.
2. Suppliers increased material prices.
3. The company changed to cheaper materials to protect profit margins.
4. The cheaper materials lowered product quality.
5. Customers became less satisfied.
6. Customers bought less.
7. Sales declined.

---

# Cause Behind the Cause

The team did not stop at “product quality decreased.”

They kept asking why and discovered the deeper cause:

- longer payment terms caused material prices to rise
- higher material prices caused a change in materials
- changed materials hurt quality
- lower quality reduced customer purchases

### Important Lesson

Root cause analysis can reveal a domino effect, where one decision causes several connected problems.

---

# What Was Not the Root Cause?

The team discovered that the sales decline was not mainly caused by:

- lack of sales training
- lack of motivation
- weak marketing
- sudden changes in customer preferences

These could have been wrong assumptions.

The real issue was:

- a manufacturing materials change that reduced product quality

---

# Practical Solution

After identifying the root cause, the team could recommend useful actions.

Possible solutions included:

- return to the previous manufacturing materials
- negotiate discounts for shorter payment terms
- look for alternative suppliers
- improve material quality while protecting margins

---

## Expected Results

If the company fixes the material issue:

- product quality improves
- customer satisfaction increases
- customers begin buying again
- sales start rising

### Important Lesson

A good solution addresses the real cause, not just the visible symptom.

---

# Why Root Cause Analysis Was Better Than Guessing

Before RCA, management tried to fix sales performance by focusing on the sales team.

But the sales team was not the root problem.

Without RCA, the company might have kept wasting money on:

- bonuses
- training
- motivational meetings

These actions would not fix product quality.

---

# Key Lessons from Jane’s Story

## 1. Do Not Focus Only on Symptoms

Declining sales were the symptom.

The root cause was a chain of decisions that led to lower product quality.

---

## 2. Use Cross-Functional Collaboration

The team included people from several departments.

This helped them examine the issue from multiple angles and avoid missing important information.

---

## 3. Gather Data Instead of Guessing

The team did not rely only on assumptions.

They gathered information about:

- customer behavior
- product quality
- material changes
- supplier pricing
- payment terms

---

## 4. Ask Why Repeatedly

The Five Whys helped the team move from the surface problem to the deeper business cause.

---

## 5. Recommend a Practical Solution

Once the root cause was found, the team could recommend specific actions that would actually solve the issue.

---

# Big Lesson

Root cause analysis helps businesses solve problems more effectively by identifying the real reason behind the issue.

The Five Whys is a simple but powerful tool for uncovering deeper causes.

Instead of treating symptoms, business analysts should investigate why the problem is happening and use evidence to support the solution.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Root cause analysis focuses on the underlying cause of a problem.
- A symptom is only the visible sign of a deeper issue.
- Fixing symptoms does not usually create a lasting solution.
- The Five Whys technique helps uncover deeper causes by repeatedly asking why.
- A cross-functional team can provide different perspectives and better insight.
- In the declining sales example, the problem was not poor sales effort.
- The real issue was a change in manufacturing materials that lowered product quality.
- The material change happened because payment terms were extended and prices increased.
- Root cause analysis helped the team recommend practical solutions.
- Understanding the why behind a problem leads to stronger and longer-lasting solutions.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Root cause analysis prevents businesses from wasting resources on the wrong fix.
- A problem may have multiple contributing factors, not just one cause.
- Asking “why” should be supported by facts and evidence.
- Business analysts should avoid assuming they already know the cause.
- Root cause analysis is useful in both professional and personal problem-solving.
- The goal is not just to solve the current issue, but to prevent it from coming back.
