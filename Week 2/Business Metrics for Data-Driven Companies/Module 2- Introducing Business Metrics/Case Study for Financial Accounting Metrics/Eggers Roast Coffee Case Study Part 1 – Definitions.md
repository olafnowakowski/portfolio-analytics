# Profitability Metrics vs Cash Flow Metrics

## Main Idea
This lesson introduces an important difference between:

- **profitability**
- **cash flow**

A business can look profitable on paper but still fail if it runs out of cash.

The case study uses a startup called **Egger’s Roast Coffee** to explain key financial terms and show why fast growth can sometimes be dangerous.

---

# Why This Topic Matters
Business analytics is not the same as financial accounting, but business analytics uses some accounting concepts to understand business metrics.

The goal of this case study is to show that:

- profit and cash flow are different
- a business can be profitable but still collapse
- rapid sales growth can create serious cash flow problems
- tracking the right metrics is essential for business survival

---

# Profit vs Cash Flow

## Profit
**Profit** is shown in a company’s **profit and loss statement**, also called a **P&L statement**.

Profit usually focuses on whether revenue is greater than costs.

## Cash Flow
**Cash flow** is shown in a separate report called the **statement of cash flows**.

Cash flow focuses on the actual movement of money:

- money coming into the business
- money leaving the business
- when that money is received or paid

### Important lesson
Profit and cash flow are so different that corporate financial reports discuss them separately.

---

# Why Cash Flow Can Be More Urgent Than Profit
Some companies can survive for years without being profitable.

Example from the lesson:

- Amazon survived and grew for a long time while not showing large profits

But companies that run out of cash can disappear quickly.

Examples from the lesson:

- eToys
- Pets.com
- Webvan

Even profitable companies can collapse if they cannot meet short-term cash obligations.

Example:

- AIG, one of the world’s largest and most profitable insurance companies, nearly collapsed in the fall of 2008 because of short-term cash problems.

### Big lesson
A company can be profitable and still fail if it does not have enough cash available when payments are due.

---

# Rapid Growth Can Be Dangerous
One common reason profitable companies run out of cash is **uncontrolled or unplanned sales growth**.

### Important idea
Too much success too quickly can hurt or even kill a business.

Why?

Because the business may need to spend cash immediately to produce and deliver products, but customers may not pay until later.

---

# Case Study: Egger’s Roast Coffee

## Business idea
Egger’s Roast Coffee is a startup coffee roasting business.

The plan is to:

1. buy raw coffee beans in bulk
2. roast and package them
3. sell them wholesale to a grocery store chain at a higher price

---

## Two Growth Scenarios
The case study compares two revenue scenarios:

1. **Safe, steady revenue growth**
2. **Rapid, exciting, but eventually fatal sales growth**

The purpose is to show that fast growth can look good in terms of profit but be dangerous in terms of cash flow.

---

# Egger’s Roast Coffee Business Model

## Wholesale price
A friend who works as a buyer for a large grocery chain promises to buy as much coffee as Egger’s Roast Coffee can produce.

The wholesale price is:

- **$6 per pound**

## Supply contract
Egger’s Roast Coffee will sign a supply contract agreeing to deliver as much coffee as the grocery chain needs.

There will be financial penalties if Egger’s Roast Coffee fails to deliver the full amount on time.

### Important detail
The contract guarantees a profit per pound, but it also creates risk if the company cannot keep up with demand.

---

# Payment Terms: Net 60

The grocery chain pays suppliers on average **60 days after receiving the merchandise**.

This payment term is called:

- **Net 60**

## Why companies use Net 60
This gives the grocery store time to sell the merchandise before paying suppliers.

### Important lesson
Net 60 means Egger’s Roast Coffee does not get paid immediately after delivering coffee.

---

# Production Timeline

Egger’s Roast Coffee expects it will take about:

- **30 days** to roast, package, and deliver beans after receiving the raw green beans

So the timeline is:

1. receive raw beans
2. pay for raw beans immediately
3. spend 30 days processing and delivering coffee
4. wait 60 more days for the customer to pay

### Total wait for cash:
- **90 days**

---

# Cost of Raw Coffee Beans

Egger’s Roast Coffee finds a supplier for raw green coffee beans.

Cost:

- **$2 per pound**

Payment terms:

- **COD**, meaning **cash on delivery**

## COD meaning
**Cash on delivery** means Egger’s Roast Coffee pays for the beans when they arrive.

### Important lesson
The company pays cash immediately for raw materials, but does not receive cash from the customer until much later.

---

# Variable Costs

## What are variable costs?
**Variable costs** are costs that increase when production increases.

If the company roasts more beans, these costs go up.

## Main variable cost
Raw green coffee beans:

- **$2 per pound**

If the company buys one more pound, it pays another $2.

## Other variable costs
Other variable costs include:

- fuel used in roasting
- paper packaging
- hourly wages for workers
- transportation costs to deliver beans

These additional variable costs are expected to be:

- **$2 per pound**

## Total variable cost
Raw beans:

- $2 per pound

Additional variable costs:

- $2 per pound

Total variable cost:

- **$4 per pound**

---

# Capital Investment

## What the company needs
Egger’s Roast Coffee needs to buy a machine that can roast and package large amounts of coffee quickly and consistently.

Cost of machine:

- **$540,000**

Expected useful life:

- **3 years**

## Initial investment
The owner plans to put:

- **$800,000 cash** into the business

After buying the machine:

- $800,000 - $540,000 = **$260,000 cash cushion**

The company can also borrow up to:

- **$800,000** from friends and family in an emergency

---

# Depreciation

## Why depreciation is needed
From an accounting point of view, the company needs to include the cost of the roasting machine in the cost of producing coffee.

But it would not make sense to assign the full $540,000 machine cost to the first pound of coffee.

That would make the first pound cost:

- $540,004

and every later pound would appear to use the machine for free.

That would not be fair or accurate.

---

## What is depreciation?
**Depreciation** is the process of spreading the cost of a capital asset over time because the asset wears out or loses value.

In this case, the $540,000 roasting machine is spread over:

- 3 years
- 36 months

## Monthly depreciation
$540,000 / 36 months = **$15,000 per month**

So the machine is treated as losing $15,000 in value each month.

---

## Straight-Line Depreciation
The method used here is called **straight-line depreciation**.

## What it means
The asset is assumed to lose the same amount of value each month.

In this case:

- the machine loses **$15,000 of value per month**

### Important note
Machines do not actually wear out perfectly evenly, but accounting often uses this simplifying assumption.

---

# Depreciation as a Fixed Cost

For this model, depreciation is treated as a **fixed cost**.

## Why?
Because producing one more or one less pound of coffee does not change the monthly depreciation amount.

The machine is not considered to wear out faster or slower based on small production changes.

---

# General and Administrative Expenses

## What are G&A expenses?
**General and administrative expenses**, or **G&A expenses**, are business costs that stay mostly the same each month.

Examples:

- rent for the building
- utilities
- insurance
- business licenses
- salary for office manager
- salary for bookkeeper

These costs happen whether the company roasts:

- 1 pound of coffee
- or 10,000 pounds of coffee

---

## Allocating G&A Costs
The company assigns part of these fixed costs to each pound of coffee.

Monthly G&A expenses:

- **$10,000 per month**

Expected monthly production:

- **25,000 pounds**

G&A cost per pound:

- $10,000 / 25,000 = **$0.40 per pound**

---

# Fixed Costs Per Pound

## Depreciation per pound
Monthly depreciation:

- $15,000

Monthly production:

- 25,000 pounds

Depreciation per pound:

- $15,000 / 25,000 = **$0.60 per pound**

## G&A per pound
Monthly G&A:

- $10,000

Monthly production:

- 25,000 pounds

G&A per pound:

- $10,000 / 25,000 = **$0.40 per pound**

## Total fixed cost per pound
$0.60 + $0.40 = **$1.00 per pound**

---

# Profit Per Pound

## Selling price
Egger’s Roast Coffee sells coffee for:

- **$6 per pound**

## Variable cost
Total variable cost:

- **$4 per pound**

## Fixed cost allocation
Total fixed cost allocation:

- **$1 per pound**

## Profit per pound
$6 - $4 - $1 = **$1 profit per pound**

---

# First-Year Profit Estimate

Expected annual volume:

- **300,000 pounds**

Profit per pound:

- **$1**

Expected first-year profit:

- **$300,000**

## Monthly profit estimate
Monthly production:

- 25,000 pounds

Profit per pound:

- $1

Monthly profit:

- **$25,000**

### Why the business looks good
From a profitability point of view, the business appears successful because every pound sold creates profit.

---

# Booking Revenue

The business records, or **books**, revenue when it receives an order.

This means the company may show profit on paper before it has actually received cash from the customer.

### Important lesson
Booked revenue is not the same as cash received.

---

# What Could Go Wrong?

From a profit perspective, the business looks great.

But from a cash flow perspective, the situation is more dangerous.

## Why?
Because cash leaves the business long before cash comes back in.

---

# Cash Flow Analysis Is About Timing

Cash flow analysis focuses on **when** money moves.

In Egger’s Roast Coffee:

- raw beans are paid for immediately
- processing and delivery take 30 days
- the grocery chain pays 60 days after receiving the coffee

So when the company receives an order:

- it spends cash immediately
- but gets paid about **90 days later**

---

# Negative Float

## What is negative float?
**Negative float** happens when a company must spend cash now but receives payment later.

In this case:

- cash goes out immediately
- cash comes in 90 days later

This creates a cash gap.

### Important lesson
Negative float is common in many businesses and can create serious cash flow pressure.

---

# Positive Float

## What is positive float?
**Positive float** happens when a company receives cash now but delivers the product or service later.

Examples of businesses with positive float:

- insurance companies
- banks

### Why positive float is good
The company gets cash upfront and has time before it must deliver the service or pay claims.

The speaker calls this a “delightful” situation.

---

# Accounts Receivable

## What are accounts receivable?
**Accounts receivable** are amounts customers owe the company for products or services already delivered but not yet paid for.

In this case, after Egger’s Roast Coffee delivers coffee, the grocery chain owes money.

Until the grocery chain pays, that money is recorded as accounts receivable.

---

## Important distinction
Accounts receivable count as revenue for profit calculations, but they do not count as cash flow because the company has not actually received the money yet.

### Simple meaning
The company earned the money on paper, but the cash is not in the bank yet.

---

# Aged Accounts Receivable

## What are aged accounts receivable?
**Aged accounts receivable** tracks how long unpaid customer balances have been owed.

It helps answer:

- how much money customers owe
- how long the money has been unpaid
- whether payments are late
- whether cash collection is becoming risky

This is also called:

- **aged receivables**

---

# Key Financial Terms

## Profit
Money left after costs are subtracted from revenue.

## Cash Flow
The movement of actual cash into and out of the business.

## P&L Statement
A **profit and loss statement** shows revenue, costs, and profit.

## Statement of Cash Flows
A financial report that shows actual cash coming in and going out.

## Net 60
A payment term meaning the customer pays 60 days after receiving the merchandise.

## COD
**Cash on delivery**, meaning payment is made when goods arrive.

## Variable Costs
Costs that increase as production increases.

Examples:
- raw materials
- hourly labor
- fuel
- packaging
- shipping

## Fixed Costs
Costs that do not change directly with the number of units produced.

Examples:
- rent
- utilities
- insurance
- licenses
- office salaries
- depreciation

## Capital Investment
A large investment in a long-term asset, such as equipment.

## Depreciation
The process of spreading the cost of a long-term asset over its useful life.

## Straight-Line Depreciation
A depreciation method where the asset loses the same value every period.

## G&A Expenses
General and administrative expenses, such as rent, utilities, insurance, licenses, and office salaries.

## Negative Float
A situation where the business pays cash before receiving cash.

## Positive Float
A situation where the business receives cash before delivering the product or service.

## Accounts Receivable
Money customers owe for goods or services already delivered.

## Aged Receivables
A measure of how long accounts receivable have remained unpaid.

# Big Lesson
A business can appear profitable while still facing serious cash flow danger.

In Egger’s Roast Coffee:

- the company earns profit on every pound sold
- but it pays costs immediately
- and waits 90 days to receive payment

This timing gap can create cash shortages, especially if sales grow too quickly.

# Key Takeaways
- Profitability and cash flow are different concepts.
- Profits are shown in a profit and loss statement.
- Cash flow is shown in a statement of cash flows.
- A profitable company can still go out of business if it runs out of cash.
- Rapid sales growth can create cash flow problems.
- Egger’s Roast Coffee sells coffee for $6 per pound.
- Total variable cost is about $4 per pound.
- Fixed cost allocation is about $1 per pound.
- Profit is expected to be $1 per pound.
- The business looks profitable on paper.
- The cash flow problem comes from paying suppliers immediately but getting paid by customers 90 days later.
- Net 60 means payment happens 60 days after the customer receives goods.
- COD means cash is paid when goods are delivered.
- Negative float can be dangerous because cash leaves before cash comes in.
- Accounts receivable are amounts customers owe but have not yet paid.
- Aged receivables track how long unpaid money has been owed.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- Profit is not the same as money in the bank.
- Revenue can be booked before cash is received.
- Cash flow timing is one of the most important things a business must manage.
- Fast growth can increase cash needs because the company must pay for more materials and production before customers pay.
- Business analysts need to understand financial timing, not just profit margins.
- A company should track both profitability metrics and cash flow metrics to avoid serious business problems.

# Final Summary
This lesson explains the difference between profitability and cash flow using the example of Egger’s Roast Coffee. The business appears profitable because it sells coffee for $6 per pound, has $4 in variable costs, and allocates $1 in fixed costs, leaving $1 profit per pound. However, the company must pay for raw beans immediately while customers pay 90 days later because of processing time and Net 60 payment terms. This creates negative float, where cash leaves the business long before it comes back in. The key lesson is that a company can be profitable on paper but still fail if it runs out of cash. Tracking cash flow, accounts receivable, and payment timing is essential for business survival.
