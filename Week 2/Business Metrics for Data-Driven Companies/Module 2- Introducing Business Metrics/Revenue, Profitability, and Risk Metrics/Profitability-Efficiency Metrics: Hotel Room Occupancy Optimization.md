# Profitability Metrics: Wasting Inventory, Hotel Rooms, and Airline Seats

## Main Idea

Not all inventory is a physical product sitting on a shelf.

Some inventory is a **wasting asset**, meaning it loses all value if it is not sold by a certain time.

Two important examples are:

- airline seats
- hotel rooms

If an airline seat or hotel room is not sold before its deadline, the opportunity to earn money from it disappears forever.

---

## What Is a Wasting Asset?

A **wasting asset** is something that loses value over time and may become completely worthless if it is not sold before a deadline.

### Examples

- A hotel room for tonight
- An airline seat on a scheduled flight
- Fresh restaurant food
- Fresh-baked bread

### Important Lesson

A hotel room that is empty tonight cannot be sold tomorrow for tonight.  
An airline seat on a flight that already departed has no value anymore.

---

# Airline Seats as Wasting Inventory

## Airline Seat Example

Imagine an airline has a scheduled flight from:

- Raleigh-Durham Airport
- to San Francisco

Once the flight takes off, any empty seat has lost its value.

---

## Variable Cost of Filling a Seat

The extra cost of putting one more passenger in an empty seat is very small.

It may include:

- extra fuel to carry the passenger and baggage
- a bottle of water
- peanuts or a small snack

### Important Point

Most of the airline’s costs are already fixed.

---

## Fixed or Sunk Costs for Airlines

The airline cannot reduce most costs just because one seat is empty.

Examples of fixed or sunk costs:

- airplane lease
- maintenance
- pilot pay
- crew pay
- airport-related costs

### Important Lesson

If the seat is not sold before the flight, the money spent to create that seat is wasted.

---

# Hotel Rooms as Wasting Inventory

## Hotel Room Example

A hotel room that goes unrented for one night is also a lost opportunity.

Once the night passes, the hotel can no longer sell that room for that date.

---

## Variable Cost of Renting a Room

The extra cost of renting one room is usually low.

Examples:

- clean linens
- small bottle of shampoo
- cleaning supplies

---

## Fixed or Sunk Costs for Hotels

Most hotel costs are fixed and must be paid whether the room is rented or not.

Examples:

- building costs
- rent or mortgage
- staff salaries
- utilities
- maintenance
- insurance

### Important Lesson

Most of the cost of making the room available is already spent.  
If the room is empty, the hotel loses the chance to earn revenue from it.

---

# Variable Pricing

## What Is Variable Pricing?

**Variable pricing** means charging different prices for the same product or service depending on conditions.

In the hotel example, the speaker stayed in the same hotel room from Saturday through Friday, but the hotel charged a different price for each night.

### Important Lesson

Hotels often change room prices based on demand.

---

## How Hotels Decide Variable Prices

Hotels use **business analytics** to decide room prices.

They study data such as:

- occupancy rates
- day of the week
- season
- holidays
- business travel patterns
- demand compared to supply

---

# Occupancy Rate

## What Is Occupancy Rate?

**Occupancy rate** measures what percentage of available rooms are rented.

### Simple Meaning

Occupancy rate tells the hotel how full it is.

---

## Example Hotel Assumption

Assume a hotel has charged a fixed rate of:

- **$150 per basic room**
- every night
- for the past two years

The hotel has an average occupancy rate of:

- **66.4%**

The speaker notes that average U.S. hotel occupancy rates were:

- **62.3% in 2013**
- **64.4% in 2014**

So 66.4% is slightly above average.

---

# Why Average Occupancy Alone Is Not Enough

A single average occupancy number does not give enough useful information.

To find better insights, the hotel should break the data down by:

- day of the week
- season
- holidays
- business travel periods

### Important Lesson

High-level averages can hide important patterns.

---

# Occupancy by Day of the Week

When the hotel sorts occupancy data by day of the week, a pattern appears.

The hotel has more travelers during the week.

The peak day is:

- **Wednesday**

The hotel has fewer travelers on:

- Saturday
- Sunday

---

## What This Pattern Suggests

Because demand is higher during the week and lower on weekends, the hotel is probably mostly a **business hotel**.

Business travelers usually want to be home on:

- weekends
- holidays

---

# Pricing Strategy by Day of the Week

Based on weekly occupancy patterns, the hotel might increase total occupancy by changing prices.

Possible strategy:

- charge lower prices on Monday, Tuesday, and Thursday
- charge much lower prices on Friday, Saturday, and Sunday
- treat Wednesday as a special case

### Important Lesson

The hotel can use pricing to increase demand on low-occupancy days.

---

# Wednesday as a Special Case

Wednesday has the highest average occupancy, but the hotel should look deeper.

When Wednesday data is broken down by season, there are clear patterns.

---

## Example Wednesday Occupancy Patterns

The transcript gives examples:

- Wednesday before Thanksgiving: **35% occupancy**
- Wednesday after Christmas week: **40% occupancy**
- Wednesdays in August: **75% occupancy**
- Average Wednesday: **95% occupancy**
- Wednesdays in September, October, March, and April: **100% occupancy**

---

## What 100% Occupancy Means

If the hotel is 100% full, it may be turning away guests.

This means:

- demand is higher than supply
- the hotel may be charging less than it could
- prices may be too low during peak periods

### Important Lesson

When demand exceeds supply, the hotel may be able to raise prices.

---

# Wednesday Pricing Strategy

The speaker suggests charging different Wednesday prices depending on the season.

Possible pricing approach:

- less than $150 for holidays and August
- $150 for normal Wednesdays
- more than $150 for Wednesdays in peak business travel months

Peak business travel months mentioned:

- September
- October
- March
- April

---

# How Much Should Prices Change?

The question becomes:

## By how much should prices be raised or lowered?

This requires more complex models that compare:

- price
- demand
- occupancy
- customer behavior

The best way to build these models is empirical.

### Empirical Means

Based on real-world testing and evidence.

---

# Testing Prices

Hotels can test different prices and observe whether rooms sell:

- too slowly
- too quickly
- at the right pace

This helps the hotel understand what price works best for each date.

---

# Online Travel Agency Sites

## What Are OTA Sites?

**OTA** stands for **Online Travel Agency**.

Examples include websites where people book hotels online.

The transcript mentions:

- Expedia
- Hotwire
- Priceline
- Qunar
- Travelocity

---

## How Hotels Use OTAs

Hotels may assign a block of rooms to an OTA at a pre-agreed price.

If the OTA sells the rooms, the hotel earns revenue.

If the OTA does not sell all the rooms, the unsold rooms may be released back to the hotel to sell directly.

---

## Why OTAs Are Useful for Testing

OTAs help hotels test prices for specific dates.

For example, a hotel can see whether a room block sells out at a certain price.

This gives data about demand.

---

## Why OTAs Are Not Ideal for Hotels

OTAs usually charge commissions.

The transcript says commissions may be:

- **10% to 25%** of the room rate

Because of this, hotels usually prefer to sell directly to customers when possible.

---

# Rack Rate

## What Is Rack Rate?

The **rack rate** is the hotel’s listed, undiscounted room price.

It is the regular published price before discounts.

---

## Challenge With Changing the Rack Rate

If a hotel lowers its published rack rate, that lower price can become the new maximum price even for less price-sensitive customers.

This is a problem because some last-minute business travelers may have been willing to pay more.

### Important Lesson

Hotels want to discount rooms to some customers without lowering the price for everyone.

---

# Floor Rate

## What Is Floor Rate?

The **floor rate** is the lowest price a hotel can charge without losing money.

It is based on:

- allocated fixed costs per room
- variable costs for renting the room

### Simple Meaning

The floor rate is the hotel’s break-even price for a room.

---

# The Hotel’s Pricing Goal

Ideally, the hotel wants to sell rooms at different prices to different customer groups.

The hotel wants to:

- offer low prices to vacation travelers
- keep higher prices for business travelers
- avoid leaving rooms empty
- avoid lowering prices for everyone

This is where opaque inventory markets become useful.

---

# Opaque Inventory Market

## What Is an Opaque Inventory Market?

An **opaque inventory market** is a booking market where customers see a hotel room offer without knowing the hotel’s name before they buy.

Customers may see:

- city
- price
- hotel star rating
- general category

But they do not know the exact hotel until after purchase.

---

## Rules of Opaque Inventory

In opaque inventory markets:

- the hotel name is hidden before purchase
- customers only learn the hotel after buying
- purchases are usually non-changeable
- purchases are usually non-refundable

---

## Why Opaque Pricing Works

Opaque pricing allows hotels to offer lower prices without publicly advertising that the hotel is discounting rooms.

This helps hotels protect their regular pricing.

### Important Lesson

Opaque markets allow hotels to sell some rooms cheaply without damaging their higher business traveler prices.

---

# Why Business Travelers Avoid Opaque Inventory

Opaque inventory is less attractive to business travelers because it involves:

- uncertainty
- no changes
- no refunds
- no control over the exact hotel

Business travelers usually need reliability and flexibility.

This allows hotels to separate vacation travelers from business travelers.

---

# Two Prices for the Same Room

Opaque inventory allows hotels to maintain at least two prices for the same room on the same night:

1. a higher visible price for business travelers
2. a lower hidden price for price-sensitive travelers

---

# Size of the Opaque Inventory Market

The speaker says exact statistics are hard to find, but estimates the opaque inventory market may represent:

- **2% to 4%** of hotel room sales in the United States and Canada

The speaker also suggests this market may not be growing.

---

# Why Fancy Hotels Hide Discounts

Fancy hotels do not want to publicly advertise that they rent some rooms for:

- **30% to 60% below usual rates**

especially when the rooms are identical and on the same night.

This could damage the hotel’s brand image and pricing power.

---

# Profitability of Opaque Pricing

Opaque pricing deals may be barely profitable for hotels.

Why?

Because prices are often close to the floor rate.

### Important Lesson

Opaque pricing helps recover some revenue from rooms that might otherwise go empty, but it is not usually the most profitable pricing method.

---

# Promotional Rates

Instead of opaque pricing, hotels can use other pricing models between:

- rack rate
- floor rate

One example is a **promotional rate**.

---

## What Is a Promotional Rate?

A promotional rate is a discounted price offered to selected customers.

Hotels may offer promotional rates to:

- loyal customers
- mailing list members
- mobile text list subscribers
- people connected to the hotel brand

---

## Why Promotional Rates Work

Customers with some brand connection may be willing to pay more than the opaque floor price.

This means promotional rates can help hotels fill rooms while earning more than they would in opaque markets.

---

# When Should a Hotel Offer a Discount?

The transcript asks:

## When should a hotel offer a room at 80% of its rack price to a loyal customer?

Answer:

A hotel should offer the room at 80% of rack price when there is less than an 80% chance that the room will be rented at the full list price before it expires.

---

## Why This Makes Sense

If the room is unlikely to sell at full price, discounting it can be better than leaving it empty.

But if the room is likely to sell at full price, discounting it too early loses money.

### Important Lesson

Discounting should depend on the probability of selling the room at full price before the night passes.

---

# Three Potential Hotel Room Prices

A hotel room usually has at least three possible prices for each night:

1. **Rack rate**
   - the listed full price

2. **Floor rate**
   - the break-even minimum price

3. **Promotional rate**
   - a discounted price between rack rate and floor rate

---

## Which Price Stays Constant?

Only the **floor rate** is usually constant.

The other prices vary based on:

- day of the week
- season
- demand
- business travel patterns
- holidays
- booking timing

---

# Why Hotels Can Have Many Prices

Because listed and promotional rates change by day and season, the same type of hotel room could theoretically be rented for over:

- **1,000 different prices in one year**

### Important Lesson

Hotel pricing is highly dynamic because the value of the room changes depending on timing and demand.

---

# Big Lesson

Hotel rooms and airline seats are wasting assets because they lose all value if they are not sold by a deadline.

Business analytics helps companies manage these assets by using variable pricing.

Hotels use occupancy data, day-of-week patterns, seasonal demand, OTA testing, opaque inventory markets, and promotional rates to maximize revenue from rooms that would otherwise go empty.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Not all inventory is a physical product on a shelf.
- Airline seats and hotel rooms are wasting assets.
- If an airline seat is empty after departure, its value becomes zero.
- If a hotel room is empty for one night, that night’s revenue opportunity is lost forever.
- Airline and hotel costs are mostly fixed or sunk costs.
- The variable cost of filling an airline seat or hotel room is usually low.
- Hotels use variable pricing to adjust room prices based on demand.
- Occupancy rate measures how full a hotel is.
- Average occupancy alone is not enough; hotels must analyze occupancy by day and season.
- Business hotels usually have higher weekday occupancy and lower weekend occupancy.
- Wednesday may be a peak business travel day.
- Hotels may charge more during peak demand and less during low demand.
- OTAs help hotels test room prices but charge commissions.
- Rack rate is the full listed room price.
- Floor rate is the break-even minimum price.
- Opaque inventory hides the hotel name until after booking and helps hotels sell discounted rooms without harming their brand.
- Promotional rates can be offered to loyal customers when full-price sale is unlikely.
- A hotel should offer an 80% rack-rate discount when the chance of selling at full price is less than 80%.
- A hotel room can have many possible prices depending on season, day, and demand.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Wasting inventory creates urgency because unsold inventory becomes worthless.
- Variable pricing helps businesses recover revenue from inventory that might otherwise expire.
- Price discrimination allows hotels to charge different prices to different customer groups.
- Business travelers are usually less price-sensitive and value certainty.
- Vacation travelers are usually more price-sensitive and may accept uncertainty for a lower price.
- Analytics helps hotels decide when to discount and how much to discount.
- The goal is to maximize total revenue, not simply maximize occupancy.

---

# Final Summary

This lesson explains how airline seats and hotel rooms are examples of wasting inventory. Their value disappears if they are not sold by a specific deadline. Because most of the costs of flights and hotel rooms are fixed, selling an otherwise empty seat or room can be valuable even at a lower price. Hotels use business analytics and occupancy rate data to create variable pricing strategies based on day of the week, season, demand, and customer type. They may use OTAs, opaque inventory markets, and promotional rates to sell rooms at different prices without damaging their full-price market. The main lesson is that dynamic pricing helps companies maximize revenue from perishable inventory before its value disappears.
