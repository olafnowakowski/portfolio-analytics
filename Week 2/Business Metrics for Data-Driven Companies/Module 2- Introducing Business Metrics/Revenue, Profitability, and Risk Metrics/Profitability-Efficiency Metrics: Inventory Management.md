# Profitability Metrics: Inventory Management

## Main Idea

This lesson focuses on **profitability metrics**, also called **efficiency metrics**.

The main example is **inventory management**, which is one of the biggest ways operating companies can:

- reduce costs
- improve efficiency
- maximize profitability
- avoid lowering the quality of their products

Inventory management is important because products that sit unsold for too long can reduce profits and create cash flow problems.

---

## What Is Inventory?

**Inventory** usually means finished products waiting to be sold.

Examples:

- products sitting on a retail store shelf
- products stored in a warehouse
- products moving between the factory and the customer

### Important Lesson

The longer a product stays in inventory before being sold, the less efficient it is for the company.

Time spent in inventory reduces profits.

---

# Why Inventory Time Should Be Minimized

There are four main reasons why companies want to reduce the amount of time products spend in inventory:

1. **Negative float**
2. **Fixed cost of storage**
3. **Wastage**
4. **Obsolescence**

---

# 1. Negative Float

## What It Means

Negative float happens when a company spends money before it receives money back.

In inventory management, this often means:

- the manufacturer already paid to make the product
- but does not get paid until the product is sold

This is similar to the problem from the **Egger’s Roast Coffee** case study.

---

## Why Negative Float Is Dangerous

A company needs **working capital** to cover the time between:

- paying to produce inventory
- and receiving payment from customers

Working capital often comes with interest costs.

### Important Point

The longer inventory takes to sell, the longer the company waits to get paid.

That means:

- more working capital is needed
- more interest may need to be paid
- cash flow pressure increases

---

## Example: Retail Clothing Stores

Retail clothing stores are especially vulnerable to cash flow problems caused by unsold inventory.

If clothing does not sell, the company may run out of cash while waiting for inventory to move.

The transcript gives examples of U.S. retail chains that declared bankruptcy in the first six months of 2015:

- Wet Seal
- Cache
- Simply Fashion

### Important Lesson

A company can get into financial trouble if too much cash is trapped in unsold inventory.

---

# 2. Fixed Cost of Storage

## What It Means

It costs money to store inventory every day.

Storage costs may include:

- heating
- lighting
- air conditioning
- rent
- security
- warehouse maintenance
- retail store maintenance

The longer a product sits on the shelf, the more storage cost must be assigned to that product.

---

## Example: Furniture Warehouse

Imagine a furniture warehouse and showroom with:

- fixed costs of **$100,000 per year**
- space to display **200 luxury dining room tables**
- average selling price of **$1,200 per table**

---

## If Inventory Turns Over Once Per Year

If the company sells 200 tables per year:

- storage cost = $100,000
- number of tables sold = 200

Storage cost per table:

- $100,000 / 200 = **$500 per table**

This is a very high storage cost per item.

---

## If Inventory Turns Over Five Times Per Year

If inventory turns over five times per year, the company sells more tables using the same storage space.

Storage cost per table becomes:

- $100 per table

This is much more efficient.

### Important Lesson

Faster inventory turnover spreads fixed storage costs across more units, reducing the cost per product.

---

# 3. Wastage

## What It Means

**Wastage** happens when inventory loses value while waiting to be sold.

Some products can lose all their value if they are not sold quickly.

---

## Examples of Complete Wastage

Some products become worthless if they are not sold immediately, such as:

- fresh-baked bread
- restaurant food
- certain fresh grocery items

If these products are not sold on the day they are made, their value may become zero.

---

## Products With Expiration Dates

Many products have expiration dates or sell-by dates.

Examples include:

- grocery products
- dairy products
- fresh food
- packaged food with legal expiration limits

In many countries, certain products cannot legally be sold after their sell-by date.

### Important Lesson

Every unsold unit of perishable inventory can become a total loss.

---

## Special Case: Hotel Rooms and Airline Seats

Hotel rooms and airline seats are also examples of inventory that can expire.

If they are not sold by a specific time:

- an empty hotel room for that night loses its value
- an empty airline seat on a flight loses its value

Their inventory value becomes zero after the date passes.

---

# 4. Obsolescence

## What It Means

**Obsolescence** happens when inventory becomes outdated or unwanted.

Items that stay in inventory for six months or more may be products that customers no longer want to buy.

---

## Reasons Inventory Becomes Obsolete

Inventory may become obsolete because:

- too much was produced
- worldwide demand was lower than expected
- a better product replaced it
- fashion changed
- customer preferences changed

### Important Lesson

The longer an item stays in inventory, the greater the risk that it will eventually need to be disposed of without being sold.

---

# Days Inventory

## What It Is

**Days inventory** means the average number of days inventory is held before it is sold.

It is an important business metric because it helps companies understand how efficiently inventory is moving.

### Simple Meaning

Days inventory tells you how long products usually sit before being sold.

---

## Why Days Inventory Matters

Tracking days inventory helps companies understand:

- how fast inventory sells
- whether products are sitting too long
- whether cash is trapped in inventory
- whether storage costs are too high
- whether products may become wasted or obsolete

---

# Estimating Days Inventory for Public Companies

Many companies do not publicly share their exact days inventory data.

However, for publicly traded companies, you can estimate it using information in annual reports.

Public companies usually report:

- inventory on hand at the end of the year
- annual cost of goods sold

---

## Days Inventory Formula

Days inventory can be estimated as:

**Inventory on hand / Annual cost of goods sold × 365**

This works because inventory and cost of goods sold are usually valued using the same accounting method.

So the comparison is consistent.

---

# Walmart Example

The transcript uses Walmart as an example.

For the fiscal year ended January 2014, Walmart reported:

- year-end inventory = **$44.9 billion**
- annual cost of goods sold = **$358.1 billion**

Calculation:

- 44.9 / 358.1 = about 1/8
- 1/8 × 365 = about **46 days**

So Walmart’s estimated days inventory was about:

**46 days**

---

## Why This Is Impressive

Walmart sells all its inventory on average within about 46 days.

This is impressive because Walmart stores carry a very large number of products.

A typical Walmart store has more than:

- **100,000 distinct product SKUs**

---

# What Is a SKU?

A **SKU** means **Stock Keeping Unit**.

It is a unique identifier for a specific product.

For example:

- a red shirt in size medium may have one SKU
- the same shirt in blue may have a different SKU
- the same shirt in size large may have another SKU

SKUs help companies track inventory at the product level.

---

# Limits of the Days Inventory Metric

Days inventory is useful, but it does not explain everything.

It does not tell the company:

- which specific products are selling slowly
- why certain products are not selling
- whether a product is seasonal
- whether a product is out of fashion
- whether customers are returning it as defective

### Important Lesson

High-level inventory metrics are helpful, but companies need more detailed product-level metrics to make better decisions.

---

# Inventory Metrics at the SKU Level

Companies that use big data track inventory at a very detailed level.

Major retail chains such as:

- Walmart
- 7-Eleven
- Costco

track inventory turnaround and days inventory at the individual SKU level.

They may also break this down by:

- geographic region
- country
- state
- city
- individual store

---

## How Companies Track Inventory Movement

Companies can track inventory by comparing:

- when an item is reordered
- how many units are reordered
- when the item is delivered
- when the item is stocked on the shelf
- cash register sales data
- exact time of sale
- exact SKU sold

This helps the company understand how inventory is moving in real time.

---

# Example: Snow Shovels in North Carolina

Imagine a store in North Carolina sells:

- **50 snow shovels per year**
- all sales happen between **December and March**

It would not make sense for the store to hold snow shovel inventory between:

- April and November

### Important Lesson

Inventory expectations should be adjusted for region and season.

A product may sell well in one location or season but poorly in another.

---

# Best Practices for Inventory Management

In a strong inventory management system, each product SKU should have its own expected days inventory.

That expected number should be adjusted for:

- region
- season
- product type
- historical sales patterns
- demand forecasts

A mathematical model can help determine:

- how much shelf space should be given to each product
- how much inventory should be held
- when the product should be reordered

---

## Warning Signs

If the actual days inventory is different from the expected days inventory, that is a warning sign.

It may mean:

- the product is selling slower than expected
- demand has changed
- too much inventory was ordered
- not enough inventory was ordered
- the product is seasonal
- the product is defective
- customer preferences have changed

### Important Lesson

Unexpected inventory patterns should be investigated quickly.

---

# The Problem of Too Little Inventory

Inventory problems are not only caused by having too much inventory.

A company can also have too little inventory.

If an item is out of stock, customers may leave without buying anything.

---

## Lost Customer Problem

It is hard to directly measure how many customers leave because a product is out of stock.

But a good proxy metric is:

**the number of times inventory for a specific SKU reaches zero**

If inventory reaches zero often, the store may be losing customers.

---

## Why Zero Inventory Is Bad

Having zero inventory can hurt:

- sales
- customer satisfaction
- brand reputation
- customer loyalty

Customers expect stores to have products available.

If the store often runs out, customers may go somewhere else.

---

# Walmart and SKU Reduction

The lesson mentions that Walmart once tried to reduce the number of SKUs it carried.

But according to Bloomberg reporter Megan McArdle, Walmart had to bring some SKUs back because removing them caused:

- customer complaints
- lost sales

### Important Lesson

Reducing inventory may lower costs, but if it causes customers to leave unhappy, it can hurt the business.

---

# Empty-Handed Customer Metric

A strong retail practice would be to track customers who leave empty-handed.

The speaker suggests that when a customer leaves a big-box retailer without buying anything, a marketing researcher could ask:

**Was there a particular product you were looking for that we did not have on the shelf?**

They could offer a small incentive, such as:

- a $5 gift certificate

This would help the company learn which missing products are costing them sales.

---

# Checkout Question Example

The speaker noticed that their local grocery store staff began asking at checkout:

**Was there anything you couldn’t find today?**

This is a useful way to collect customer feedback about out-of-stock items.

The speaker jokes that they are tempted to answer:

**emu eggs**

to see whether a data analyst might decide the store should stock them.

---

# Big Lesson

Inventory management is one of the most important areas for improving profitability.

Companies need to avoid:

- holding inventory too long
- wasting perishable goods
- letting products become obsolete
- running out of products customers expect to buy

The best companies track inventory at the SKU level and use data to adjust inventory by location, season, and demand.

  - negative float

  - wastage

  - inventory on hand / annual cost of goods sold × 365
