# Risk Metrics: Leverage, Reputation, and Product Recall Risk

## Main Idea

This lesson focuses on **risk metrics**.

A major source of business risk often comes from **excessive leverage**, meaning a company has borrowed too much money compared to what it owns or is worth.

The lesson also explains another important type of risk: **reputational risk**, especially when a company sells contaminated food products.

Costco is used as an example of how a company can use customer and purchase data to manage product recall risk quickly and effectively.

---

## What Is Business Risk?

Business risk is the possibility that something could damage a company’s ability to survive, grow, or make money.

Examples include:

- too much debt
- customers defaulting on loans
- bad publicity
- product recalls
- contaminated food
- damaged brand reputation
- loss of customer trust

---

# Excessive Leverage as a Source of Risk

## What Is Leverage?

**Leverage** means using borrowed money to finance business activity.

A company is highly leveraged when it has a lot of debt compared to its equity or actual value.

### Simple Meaning

The company is using borrowed money to increase potential returns, but this also increases risk.

---

## Why Excessive Leverage Is Dangerous

If a company owes more money than it is worth, it is unlikely to survive for long.

This is especially risky when:

- the company depends on borrowing
- the company depends on customers repaying loans
- the company has little equity compared to debt

### Important Lesson

Borrowed money can magnify profits when things go well, but it also magnifies losses when things go wrong.

---

# Banking Example Before the 2008 Financial Crisis

Before the 2008 financial crisis in the United States, banks were allowed to borrow:

- **$33 for every $1 of equity**

This means banks could be highly leveraged.

---

## Example Bank

Imagine a bank has:

- **$10 million in equity**

The bank is allowed to borrow:

- **$330 million in debt**

The bank can then lend out that borrowed money.

---

## Profit Example

Suppose the bank can:

- borrow money at **2%**
- lend money out at **3%**

The difference is:

- **1% profit spread**

If the bank lends out $330 million, it can make about:

- **$3.3 million gross profit per year before expenses**

This creates a return of:

- **33% on the bank’s $10 million in equity**

---

## Why This Is Risky

The problem is that only a small percentage of loans need to go bad for the bank to become insolvent.

The bank has:

- $10 million in equity
- $333 million total exposure

The equity is only about:

- **3% of the total amount**

So if just around 3% of the loans become unrecoverable or default, the bank can lose all its equity.

---

## Insolvency

**Insolvent** means a company cannot pay what it owes or its debts are greater than its assets.

### Important Lesson

When leverage is very high, even a small loss can destroy the business.

---

# Risk From Customer Defaults

If a business makes loans and its customers default, the risk can become much larger when the company is also leveraged.

## Default

A **default** happens when a borrower fails to repay money they owe.

### Why This Matters

If many customers fail to repay loans, a leveraged lender may not have enough equity to absorb the losses.

This can cause the company to collapse.

---

# Reputational Risk

## What Is Reputational Risk?

**Reputational risk** is the risk of damage to a company’s brand, image, or future ability to sell.

A company’s reputation is important because customers need to trust the business.

---

## Example: Food Contamination

Restaurants, manufacturers, grocery stores, and retailers are especially vulnerable to reputational risk if they sell food that is:

- tainted
- contaminated
- unsafe
- likely to make customers sick

### Important Lesson

A food safety problem can hurt customers and seriously damage the company’s brand.

---

# Costco Example: Product Recall Risk Management

Costco is used as an example of successful risk management using big data.

Costco can respond quickly to product recalls because it has detailed records about:

- customers
- memberships
- purchases
- product IDs
- contact information

---

## Costco’s Key Risk Metric

The important metric for Costco is:

**How long does it take to contact every potentially at-risk customer after a level one product recall?**

This measures how quickly Costco can respond when customers may be in danger.

---

## Level One Product Recall

A **level one product recall** is a serious product recall where the product may create a major health risk.

In this kind of situation, speed matters.

### Important Lesson

The faster the company contacts affected customers, the more harm it can prevent.

---

# Why Costco Can Respond Quickly

Costco requires customers to buy an annual membership.

Because of this, Costco has a membership database that often includes:

- customer names
- addresses
- email addresses
- phone numbers
- mobile phone numbers

Costco also stores records of every purchase made by every customer.

---

## Purchase Records at SKU Level

Costco tracks purchases at the individual product ID level, also called the **SKU level**.

## SKU Meaning

**SKU** stands for **Stock Keeping Unit**.

It identifies a specific product.

### Why SKU-Level Data Matters

If a specific product is recalled, Costco can search its database and find exactly which customers bought that product.

---

# Product Recall Examples

The transcript mentions two product recalls in spring 2014:

- peanut butter
- peaches

In each case, Costco was able to identify more than:

- **50,000 customers**

who had bought the recalled product.

Costco contacted those customers within:

- **24 hours**

---

## How Costco Contacted Customers

Costco used the fastest available methods, including:

- email
- phone calls
- letters to mailing addresses

### Important Lesson

Because Costco had detailed customer and purchase data, it could prevent many people from getting sick.

---

# Big Data and Risk Management

Costco’s recall system is an example of using big data for risk management.

Costco connects:

- customer data
- product data
- purchase history
- contact information

This allows the company to act quickly when a product becomes dangerous.

---

# Costco and Salmonella Outbreak Example

The lesson also explains a more complex example from January and February 2010.

## What Happened?

A total of:

- **272 people**
- in **44 different states**

became ill with the same genetically identical strain of salmonella.

The United States Centers for Disease Control, or **CDC**, tried to find the source of the outbreak but was initially unsuccessful.

---

## How Costco Data Helped

Someone realized that Costco purchase records could be analyzed.

Since Costco membership reaches about:

- **one-quarter of U.S. families**

it was reasonable to assume that about one-quarter of the 272 sick people may have shopped at Costco.

That would be around:

- **70 people**

---

## Finding the Common Product

Analysts could then look at what food items those Costco customers had purchased in common.

The only common product was:

- **pepper salami**

The pepper salami was made by:

- **Daniele International**
- based in Pascoag, Rhode Island

---

## Finding the Source of Contamination

The contamination was eventually traced to:

- one 25-pound box of crushed red pepper

The crushed red pepper came from:

- **Wholesome Spice**
- based in Brooklyn, New York

The speaker jokes that it was “not so wholesome after all.”

---

# Why This Example Matters

This example shows that detailed purchase data can help trace a health problem back to its source.

Without Costco’s records, it may have taken much longer to identify the contaminated product.

### Important Lesson

Data can reduce risk not only by responding to known problems, but also by helping investigators discover the source of a problem.

# Important Risk Metrics From This Lesson

## Leverage Ratio

Measures how much debt a company has compared to equity.

High leverage increases risk.

## Default Rate

Measures how many borrowers fail to repay loans.

This is especially important for banks and lenders.

## Recall Response Time

Measures how quickly a company contacts affected customers after a product recall.

## Customer Exposure

Measures how many customers may be affected by a dangerous or defective product.

## Product-Level Purchase Tracking

Tracks which customers bought which products at the SKU level.

This helps identify affected customers quickly.

## Reputational Risk

Measures or monitors the risk of damage to the company’s brand and future sales.

---

# Big Lesson

Risk metrics help companies identify and respond to threats before they become disasters.

The two major risk examples in this lesson are:

1. **Financial risk from leverage**
   - Too much debt can make a company collapse after small losses.

2. **Reputational and safety risk from product recalls**
   - Dangerous products can harm customers and damage the company’s brand.

Costco’s example shows how strong data systems can reduce risk by helping the company respond quickly and accurately.

# Extra Important Notes

- Risk metrics are often about survival, not just performance.
- Leverage makes both gains and losses larger.

- Customer-level purchase records are powerful tools for risk management.
- Data systems that seem useful for marketing can also be extremely valuable for safety and crisis response.
