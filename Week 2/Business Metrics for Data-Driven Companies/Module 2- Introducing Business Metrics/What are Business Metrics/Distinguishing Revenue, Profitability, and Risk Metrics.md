# Three Categories of Business Metrics

## Main Idea
All business metrics can be grouped into **three broad categories**:

1. **Revenue metrics**
2. **Profitability metrics**
3. **Risk metrics**

A helpful way to identify which category a metric belongs to is to ask:

> Who in the company depends on this information and asks for it?

Different teams care about different types of metrics because they support different business decisions.

---

# The Three Metric Categories

## 1. Revenue Metrics
Revenue metrics are connected to:

- sales
- marketing
- customer growth
- selling products or services

These metrics are usually used by:

- sales teams
- marketing teams
- vice presidents of sales
- vice presidents of marketing

### Simple meaning
Revenue metrics tell the company how well it is attracting customers and selling products.

---

## 2. Profitability Metrics
Profitability metrics are connected to:

- efficiency
- logistics
- production
- operations

These metrics are usually used by:

- operations teams
- production teams
- logistics teams
- chief operating officers

### Simple meaning
Profitability metrics tell the company how efficiently it creates and delivers products or services.

---

## 3. Risk Metrics
Risk metrics are connected to:

- risk management
- financial stability
- debt
- future uncertainty
- company survival

These metrics are often used by:

- risk managers
- creditors
- banks
- outside investors
- financial analysts

### Simple meaning
Risk metrics tell the company what dangers could hurt the business and how serious those risks are.

---

# Revenue Metrics

## What revenue metrics measure
Revenue metrics are **outward-facing**.

They show how well or badly the company is:

- marketing its products
- selling its products
- attracting customers
- converting potential customers into buyers

---

## Sales Team Revenue Metrics
The sales team usually wants to know:

- how many units of each product were sold
- sales during a specific time period
- how current sales compare to the same time last year
- how current sales compare to the year before that

They may also break down sales by:

- region
- product
- new customers
- repeat customers

---

## Sales Funnel Metrics
Sales teams also care about the **sales funnel**.

The sales funnel includes:

- potential future customers
- where those customers are in the buying process
- how close they are to making a purchase

### Important note
The sales funnel is the step-by-step process of moving a potential customer toward buying something.

---

## Marketing Team Revenue Metrics
The marketing team wants to measure how effective marketing campaigns are.

They may ask:

- How many people saw an advertisement?
- How many people opened or saw an email marketing piece?
- How many people saw a mail offer?
- What percentage of people responded?
- How many people clicked or took action?

### Big rule
Anything that relates directly or indirectly to selling is a **revenue metric**.

---

# Profitability Metrics

## What profitability metrics measure
Profitability metrics focus on how efficiently a company creates and delivers products or services.

These are also called **operational metrics** or **efficiency metrics**.

---

## Examples of profitability metrics
Profitability metrics may include:

- how much cash is tied up in unsold inventory
- how much production is wasted or spoiled
- how often the company cannot meet customer demand because inventory or production is too low
- what percentage of products from a production line are defective
- how much is spent on raw materials per unit
- how much is spent on labor per unit
- how efficiently products are produced and delivered

---

## Example: Spoiled Inventory
If a company sells mangoes and does not sell them in time, the mangoes may rot.

That is a profitability issue because:

- the company paid for the mangoes
- the product became unsellable
- the company lost potential revenue
- the waste reduced efficiency

---

## Example: Insufficient Inventory
At the other extreme, a company may not produce or store enough products.

If customers urgently want something but the company cannot provide it, the company can lose sales.

This is also a profitability issue because the company failed to meet demand efficiently.

---

## Why Profitability Metrics Matter
A company can have high and growing revenue but still fail to be profitable if it cannot deliver its products or services efficiently.

### Important lesson
Revenue growth alone does not guarantee profitability.

A company must also control costs, reduce waste, manage inventory, and run operations efficiently.

---

## Large Established Companies
Large companies that do not have much room to grow revenue can still improve profitability by improving operational efficiency.

For example, they can:

- reduce waste
- improve production processes
- lower variable costs
- reduce defects
- improve inventory management

---

# Risk Metrics

## What risk metrics measure
Risk metrics track dangers that could hurt the company.

They help measure and reduce possible threats to:

- financial health
- company survival
- long-term growth
- investor confidence
- debt repayment ability

---

## Example: Debt and Interest Payments
If a company spends a large part of its monthly net cash flow on debt interest, it is vulnerable.

A small drop in revenue from something like a recession could make the company unable to pay its debts.

This could lead to insolvency.

### Insolvent means:
The company cannot pay what it owes.

---

## Secured Creditors
Secured creditors may have the right to seize company assets if they are not paid on time.

If that happens, the business could be forced to close.

### Important lesson
Debt increases risk because the company must keep making payments even if business conditions get worse.

---

## Net Cash Out
The speaker says **net cash out** is one of the most important risk metrics to track.

This helps answer:

> How many months can the company survive at the current burn rate?

### Burn rate means:
How quickly the company is spending its available cash.

---

## Example: Churn as a Risk Metric
**Churn** is another risk metric, especially for subscription-based companies.

### Churn rate means:
The rate at which subscribers stop using or paying for a service.

If a company has high churn, many new subscribers leave within a year.

---

## Why High Churn Is Risky
High churn is risky because eventually there may be fewer people left who have never been customers before.

This makes it harder to:

- keep growing revenue
- replace lost customers
- maintain steady revenue

### Important lesson
Companies with loyal, long-term customers are less risky because they do not need to constantly find new customers to survive.

---

## Risk Metrics in Finance
Some risk metrics are especially important in the financial industry.

### Banks and credit cards
Banks that issue credit cards track:

- exposure to potential customer defaults
- what percentage of customers may default in the next six months
- what percentage of customers are already in default

### Default means:
A customer fails to pay back money they owe.

---

## Risk Metrics for Money Managers
Money managers may use risk metrics such as:

- volatility of returns
- maximum historical drawdown from high water mark

These are used as signals of portfolio risk exposure.

### Volatility means:
How much returns move up and down.

### Maximum drawdown means:
The largest drop from a previous high point.

---

# Risk and Leverage

## Main idea
The speaker says most risk metrics are related in some way to **leverage**.

### Leverage means:
Using borrowed money to finance business activity.

If a company depends heavily on borrowed money, any problem becomes more dangerous.

### Important lesson
A company with a lot of debt faces greater risk because it must repay borrowed money even when things go wrong.

Why?

# Big Lesson
A strong company tracks all three types of metrics.

A company needs:

- revenue metrics to understand sales and marketing
- profitability metrics to understand efficiency and operations
- risk metrics to understand threats and financial danger

### Important point
A great company uses all three perspectives together.

It needs people who think about:

- growth
- efficiency
- risk

---

# Key Takeaways
- Business metrics fall into three main categories:
  - revenue metrics
  - profitability metrics
  - risk metrics
- Revenue metrics relate to sales, marketing, customers, and growth.
- Profitability metrics relate to efficiency, production, logistics, and operations.
- Risk metrics relate to dangers, debt, uncertainty, and company survival.
- A useful way to classify a metric is to ask which people in the company depend on it.
- Revenue metrics are outward-facing because they show how well the company sells and markets.
- Profitability metrics show whether the company can deliver products or services efficiently.
- A company can grow revenue but still fail if operations are inefficient.
- Risk metrics help companies understand and reduce threats.
- Churn is a risk metric for subscription-based businesses.
- Debt and leverage increase risk.
- A strong company tracks revenue, profitability, and risk metrics together.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- Metrics are useful when they help answer a business question or guide a decision.
- Different departments often care about different metrics.
- Revenue without profitability can still be a problem.
- Profitability without risk management can still leave a company vulnerable.
- Risk metrics are especially important for companies with debt or subscription models.
- The best analysis considers growth, efficiency, and risk at the same time.

---

# Final Summary
Business metrics can be grouped into revenue, profitability, and risk metrics. Revenue metrics show how well a company markets and sells its products. Profitability metrics show how efficiently the company creates and delivers products or services. Risk metrics show possible dangers that could harm the company, such as debt, churn, or financial instability. Each category is important because a business must grow revenue, operate efficiently, and manage risk to succeed. A great company tracks all three types of metrics and uses them together to make better business decisions.
