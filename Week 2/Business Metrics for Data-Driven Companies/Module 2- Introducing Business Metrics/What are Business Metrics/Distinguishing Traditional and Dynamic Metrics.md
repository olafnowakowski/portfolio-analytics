# Traditional vs Dynamic Business Metrics

## Main Idea
Business metrics can be used to help answer the key business question:

> What change in our business processes can we make right now to increase revenue, maximize profitability, or reduce risk?

The lesson explains the difference between **traditional business metrics** and **dynamic business metrics**.

---

# Traditional Business Metrics

## What they are
**Traditional business metrics** include standard financial and managerial accounting measures.

Examples:

- quarterly net cash flow statements
- profit and loss statements
- balance sheet changes
- shareholder equity changes

## Where they come from
Traditional metrics come mostly from older accounting and reporting methods.

They are connected to **after-the-fact reporting**, meaning they often tell a company what already happened.

### Historical note
Some traditional metrics were once innovative, such as **double-entry bookkeeping**, which was developed by bankers in Florence, Italy around 500 years ago.

---

## Why traditional metrics still matter
Traditional metrics are still very important and worth studying.

They help businesses understand:

- financial health
- profits and losses
- cash flow
- balance sheet changes
- long-term performance

## Limitation of traditional metrics
The problem is that decisions based on traditional metrics often happen after long periods of discussion.

These decisions may take:

- months
- years

Because of this, traditional metrics may not always help businesses act quickly.

---

# Dynamic Business Metrics

## What they are
**Dynamic business metrics** are metrics that can be used to make faster, more urgent business decisions.

They are designed to help answer:

> What process should we change right now?

Dynamic metrics are useful because they can help businesses respond quickly to problems or opportunities.

---

# Two Attributes of Dynamic Business Metrics

A business metric is dynamic if it meets two main conditions.

---

## 1. The Metric Changes Significantly in a Short Time

A dynamic metric should change meaningfully over short periods, usually within:

- a month
- or less

If the metric does not change often, it is not very dynamic.

---

## Example: Store Rent

### Not dynamic
For one stand-alone retail store in a mall, the monthly rent on a three-year lease is a business metric.

It relates to:

- efficiency
- profitability

But it is **not dynamic** because the rent will not change anytime soon.

There is no reason to track it frequently because the business cannot do much about it in the short term.

---

### Dynamic in a different context
For a national retail chain with **1,000 mall-based stores**, rent can become a dynamic metric.

If the company is negotiating about **seven new three-year leases per week**, then it should track:

- average monthly rent per square foot on new leases

This becomes dynamic because the company is actively making new lease decisions every week.

---

## 2. The Company Can Take Short-Term Action to Affect the Metric

A metric is dynamic if the company can take specific actions that visibly or significantly affect it in the short term.

If the company cannot influence the metric soon, it is not useful for dynamic tracking.

---

# Context Matters

Whether a metric is dynamic depends on the business situation.

## Example: Retail Chain Lease Renewals
If a retail chain is not opening or closing stores and is only renewing leases with pre-negotiated terms, then average rent per square foot may not be very dynamic.

Why?

Because the company has little ability to change it.

---

## Example: GAP Closing Stores
The lesson mentions that in June 2015, GAP announced it would close **25% of its U.S. retail stores**.

In that situation, GAP could use the announcement to renegotiate with landlords.

The company could say something like:

- We may leave at the end of the lease.
- You may need to find a new tenant.
- A new tenant may not pay as much per square foot as we do.
- So let’s renegotiate now.

In this case, **average monthly rent per square foot for newly renegotiated leases** becomes a dynamic metric.

### Why?
Because it is a place where the company could save money soon.

---

## Business Strategy Note
The speaker points out that announcing all bad news at once can sometimes be a good strategy.

Why?

Because it can create leverage for the company to renegotiate costs or make major changes more effectively.

---

# Impact and Sensitivity of Metrics

Another important question is:

> How much impact can a business process change have on the metric?

Some metrics are affected by many factors, while others respond clearly to specific changes.

---

## Noisy Metrics

A metric is **noisy** if many different things affect it.

### Example
Quarterly revenue is a noisy metric because it can be affected by many factors, such as:

- customer behavior
- seasonality
- sales cycles
- economic conditions
- advertising
- pricing
- competition
- government contracts
- external shocks

Because many things influence it, it may be hard to know what caused the change.

---

## Twitchy Metrics

A metric is **twitchy** if it responds strongly and specifically to a change the company makes.

Dynamic metrics are usually twitchy.

### Simple meaning
Small changes in a business process can create a big visible effect on the metric.

---

# Why Aggregate Data Can Be Weak

Traditional metrics like total quarterly revenue are often **aggregate numbers**.

This means they combine many different pieces of data into one big number.

The lesson mentions the idea that:

> aggregate data is often not very useful by itself

## Why?
Because it can hide the details needed for action.

For example, a new advertising campaign might not clearly show up in quarterly revenue if most customers are government agencies with long-term contracts and slow sales cycles.

### Important lesson
High-level numbers may not show whether a specific business change actually worked.

---

# Example of a Dynamic Metric: Page Load Time

A strong example of a dynamic metric is the relationship between:

- page load time
- online shopping cart completion
- purchase rate

## What the lesson explains
The percentage of people who fill an online shopping cart and complete a purchase is very sensitive to how fast pages load.

Studies have shown that pages loading in **three or more seconds** are much less likely to lead to sales than pages loading in under three seconds.

The lesson says:

- **40% of web users abandon a page if it does not load within 3 seconds**

---

## Why this is dynamic
Page load time is dynamic because:

- it can change quickly
- the company can improve it
- small changes can strongly affect sales
- it connects directly to customer behavior

This makes it a powerful business metric.

---

# Edge Caching and Content Delivery Networks

Because page speed matters so much, there is an entire industry focused on improving load times.

## Edge caching
**Edge caching** means storing copies of a website closer to users physically around the world.

This reduces the time it takes for a page to load.

## Content Delivery Networks
Companies like **Akamai** provide content delivery networks.

Retail companies pay these networks to store copies of their websites near customers.

### Why this matters
Saving even one or two seconds in page load time can improve customer experience and increase purchases.

---

# Mobile Load Time Problem

The speaker mentions a project done by Duke students for a website performance monitoring company.

They found that on mobile devices, many retail company webpages were taking **20 seconds or more** to load.

This is a major issue because slow mobile load times can cause customers to leave before buying.

---

## Important Recommendation
If a company does not already test mobile load time performance in all global markets where it operates, it should start immediately.

### Why?
Because customers in different locations may experience different page speeds.

A company may think its website works well, but remote users around the world may experience very slow loading times.

---

# Big Lesson

Dynamic business metrics are useful because they help businesses make timely, practical decisions.

A good dynamic metric should:

- change meaningfully in a short time
- be affected by specific business actions
- connect to revenue, profitability, or risk
- help answer what the business should change right now

---

# Key Takeaways

- Traditional business metrics include accounting measures like cash flow, profits and losses, and balance sheet changes.
- Traditional metrics are important, but they often support slower decision-making.
- Dynamic business metrics are more urgent and action-focused.
- A metric is dynamic if it changes significantly over a month or less.
- A metric is dynamic if the company can take short-term actions to affect it.
- Whether a metric is dynamic depends on business context.
- Store rent may not be dynamic for one store, but it can be dynamic for a large chain negotiating many leases.
- Noisy metrics are affected by many factors and are harder to interpret.
- Twitchy metrics respond strongly to specific business changes.
- Aggregate metrics like total revenue can hide important details.
- Page load time is a strong dynamic metric because it directly affects online purchase behavior.
- Slow website loading can seriously reduce sales.
- Mobile load time testing should be done across all global markets where the company is active.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Dynamic metrics are especially useful for making fast, practical business decisions.
- A number is not automatically useful just because it is measurable.
- The best metrics are tied to actions the company can actually take.
- Context determines whether a metric is useful or dynamic.
- Businesses should avoid relying only on high-level aggregate metrics.
- Metrics become more valuable when they show where a specific process change can create impact.

---

# Final Summary
Traditional business metrics, such as quarterly cash flow, profits and losses, and balance sheet changes, are important but often support slower decisions. Dynamic business metrics are different because they change quickly and can be influenced by short-term business actions. A metric is dynamic when it changes significantly within a short period and when the company can take specific actions to affect it. Dynamic metrics are often “twitchy,” meaning they respond clearly to process changes, unlike noisy aggregate metrics such as total quarterly revenue. Page load time is a strong example of a dynamic metric because even small delays can reduce online purchases. The main goal of dynamic metrics is to help businesses decide what process they should change right now to increase revenue, improve profitability, or reduce risk.
