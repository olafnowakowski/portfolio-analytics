# Defining the Problem Statement in Business Analysis

## Main Idea
After you:

1. understand the business problem and objectives
2. identify and analyze the stakeholders

the **third and final activity in the problem assessment step** is to **define the problem statement**.

A problem statement helps clearly define the exact problem your analysis is trying to solve.

---

## What Is a Problem Statement?
A **problem statement** is a clear description of the issue happening in a business process.

It helps the team understand:

- what is going wrong
- why the project is being done
- what the scope of the project is
- what constraints exist
- what success should look like

### Simple meaning
A problem statement is like an **educated guess** or **hypothesis** about what is wrong in the current business process.

---

## Why the Problem Statement Is Important
A well-defined problem statement is important because it:

- gives the project a clear direction
- helps define the project scope
- keeps the team focused on one central issue
- provides context for the analysis
- makes it easier to decide what should be investigated

### Important lesson
The problem statement helps focus the project on the question that needs to be answered by a business solution.

---

## What a Problem Statement Does
A good problem statement should:

- describe the problem clearly
- explain why the project matters
- identify any constraints
- define success criteria
- guide further investigation

### Important note
A problem statement does **not** try to solve the problem yet.

It explains **what is wrong**, not **how to fix it**.

---

# A Problem Statement Is Not a Solution

## Very important rule
A problem statement should **not propose a solution**.

At this stage, the goal is to:

- describe the issue
- understand what is happening
- prepare for deeper analysis

You usually do **not** have enough evidence yet to recommend a solution.

### Important lesson
If you jump to a solution too early, you may solve the wrong problem.

---

# Qualities of a Good Problem Statement

The reading explains 4 key qualities of a strong problem statement.

---

## 1. Specific

### What this means
The problem statement should focus on **one problem only**.

It should include enough detail so it is clear:

- exactly what the problem is
- where in the business process the problem occurs
- which stakeholders are involved

### Why this matters
If the problem statement is too broad, the analysis may become unclear or unfocused.

---

## 2. Relevant

### What this means
The problem statement should connect directly to an important business goal or KPI.

Examples:
- increasing efficiency
- increasing customer satisfaction
- improving profitability
- reducing costs

### Why this matters
This makes sure the project is actually worth doing and supports the company’s larger objectives.

### Important lesson
A problem statement should be based on the information gathered earlier about business objectives.

---

## 3. Short

### What this means
The problem statement should be concise and direct.

It should not be overly long or complicated.

### Why this matters
A short problem statement gives the team a clear direction for further investigation.

---

## 4. Not a Solution

### What this means
The problem statement should describe the issue, not tell people how to solve it.

### Why this matters
At this stage, the team is still investigating the problem and does not yet have enough analysis to recommend the best fix.

### Important lesson
The problem statement should explain what is going wrong in the process so you can later investigate ways to improve it.

---

# How to Think About a Problem Statement
You can think of the problem statement as a starting point for the analysis.

It helps answer questions like:

- What exactly is the issue?
- Why does it matter?
- Who is affected?
- What are the boundaries of the project?
- What would success look like?

This makes it easier to move into the next phase of deeper investigation.

---

# Big Lesson
The problem statement is one of the most important parts of the problem assessment stage because it turns a broad business issue into a focused problem that the team can investigate clearly.

Without a strong problem statement, the analysis can become:

- too broad
- confusing
- disconnected from business goals
- harder to communicate to stakeholders

---

# Key Takeaways
- Defining the problem statement is the third and final activity in the problem assessment step.
- A problem statement defines the exact issue the business analysis is trying to solve.
- It helps guide the project and define the project scope.
- A problem statement provides:
  - a description of the problem
  - context for why the project is being done
  - constraints
  - success criteria
- A problem statement can be thought of as a hypothesis or educated guess about what is wrong.
- A problem statement should **not** propose a solution.
- A strong problem statement should be:
  - specific
  - relevant
  - short
  - not a solution
- It should focus on one problem and connect to a business objective or KPI.
- A good problem statement gives the team a central question to investigate.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- A problem statement is meant to narrow the focus, not expand it.
- Including key stakeholders in the statement helps show who is affected.
- Connecting the problem to a KPI or business objective makes the analysis more valuable.
- Clear scope is important because it prevents the project from becoming too broad.
- The problem statement creates a foundation for future solution design, but it is not the solution itself.

---

# Final Summary
After understanding the business problem and objectives and identifying stakeholders, the next step is to define the problem statement. A problem statement clearly explains what issue the analysis is focused on, why it matters, what constraints exist, and what success should look like. It should be specific, relevant, short, and focused only on the problem, not the solution. In business analysis, a strong problem statement is essential because it gives the project direction, defines scope, and helps the team stay focused on the right issue.
