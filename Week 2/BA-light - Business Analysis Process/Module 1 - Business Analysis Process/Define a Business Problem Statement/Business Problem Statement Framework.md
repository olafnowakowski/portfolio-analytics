# Problem Statement Framework in Business Analysis

## Main Idea
A **problem statement framework** helps organize the most important information about a business problem so the project stays clear and focused.

A strong problem statement helps answer questions like:

- Why is this analysis being done?
- Who is involved in the project?
- How will success be measured?
- What limits or obstacles affect the project?
- What resources or conditions are needed for success?

This framework is useful because it summarizes the **goal**, **scope**, **stakeholders**, **success criteria**, and **limitations** of the analysis in one place.

---

# Why a Problem Statement Framework Matters
A problem statement framework helps:

- make the project easier to understand
- validate whether your understanding of the problem is accurate
- organize the information gathered from early research and stakeholder interviews
- reduce confusion and miscommunication
- clarify the boundaries of the project

### Big lesson
A good problem statement does not just describe the problem.  
It also gives structure to the analysis so everyone understands what the project is trying to achieve.

---

# Main Parts of the Problem Statement Framework

The framework includes 5 main parts:

1. **Question to be resolved**
2. **Context**
3. **Key stakeholders**
4. **Success criteria**
5. **Constraints**

---

## 1. Question to Be Resolved

### What it is
This is the main question the analysis is trying to answer.

It is informed by all the other parts of the framework and summarizes the core goal of the project.

### Why it matters
This section clearly states:

- what the business needs
- what the analysis is trying to solve
- what the eventual solution should address

### Important point
This should be written as a focused question, not as a solution.

---

## 2. Context

### What it is
The **context** explains why the company is facing the business problem.

This section includes background information that helps explain the situation.

### What it may include
- industry trends
- company circumstances
- market conditions
- changes in customer behavior
- operational issues
- anything else that adds useful detail to the problem

### Why it matters
Context helps explain:

- why the project is happening now
- what may be making the problem more difficult
- what surrounding factors need to be considered

---

## 3. Key Stakeholders

### What it is
This section lists the people who:

- have decision-making power
- are strongly affected by the project
- are important to the analysis process

### Why it matters
Stakeholders matter because they can:

- provide information
- influence decisions
- approve or reject changes
- support or block the project

### Important point
The reading reminds you that tools like a **power/interest grid** can help decide which stakeholders are most important.

---

## 4. Success Criteria

### What it is
The **success criteria** explain how success will be measured once the solution is implemented.

This section should include measurable targets.

### Important rule
The success criteria should follow the **SMART** framework:

- **Specific**
- **Measurable**
- **Action-oriented**
- **Relevant**
- **Time-bound**

### Why it matters
This makes success clear and measurable instead of vague.

---

## 5. Constraints

### What it is
**Constraints** are the limits or restrictions that could affect:

- the analysis
- the project timeline
- the possible solution

### Common constraints
- time
- budget
- staffing/resources
- regulations
- contracts
- material or supply issues

### Why it matters
Constraints help define what is realistic for the project.

---

# Real-World Scenario: Simply Dumplings

The reading uses **Simply Dumplings** to show how a problem statement framework might look in practice.

---

## Question to Be Resolved
**How can Simply Dumplings’ internal operations be improved to enhance profitability and overall performance?**

### Why this is a good question
It is:

- focused
- business-related
- aligned with profitability goals
- open enough for investigation
- not already assuming a solution

---

## Context
The framework gives these background details:

- Simply Dumplings has had **declining profitability over the past two years**
- There is a **competitive market environment** with changing customer preferences
- The company is facing **rising operating costs**, including:
  - ingredients
  - labor
  - rent

### Why this context matters
This shows that the profitability problem is likely affected by both:

- internal business operations
- outside market conditions

---

## Key Stakeholders
The framework lists these important stakeholders:

- **Olivia Kim** — Owner and Chief Executive Officer
- **Ethan Martinez** — Chief Financial Officer
- **Mei Chen** — Chief Operating Officer
- **Daniel Tanaka** — Board Member
- **Store Managers**

### Why these stakeholders matter
These people either:

- make decisions
- manage operations
- control finances
- or are directly involved in implementing changes

---

## Success Criteria
The framework includes these measurable goals:

- After changes are implemented, the next year should show either **break-even or profit** (**no loss**)
- Reduce **food and labor costs by 15% within the next 6 months**
- Attract new customers and achieve at least a **20% increase in sales within the next 12 months**

### Why these are strong success criteria
They are:

- specific
- measurable
- time-bound
- clearly connected to business goals

---

## Constraints
The framework lists these project limitations:

- Simply Dumplings must become profitable within **12 months** to avoid closing down businesses
- The company has budgeted **$80,000** for improvements
- Simply Dumplings currently has a **3-month contract** with a specific food supply company

### Why these constraints matter
These limits affect what solutions are realistic.

For example:

- the budget limits the size of possible changes
- the timeline adds urgency
- the supplier contract may restrict operational changes in the short term

---

# Why This Framework Is Useful
This framework helps bring all the important parts of the business problem together in one place.

It helps teams understand:

- the main question
- the background of the problem
- who matters most
- how success will be measured
- what limitations must be respected

### Big lesson
A problem statement framework creates clarity around the project and helps prevent confusion between stakeholders.

---

# Key Takeaways
- A problem statement framework helps organize important project information clearly.
- It supports communication with stakeholders and helps validate your understanding of the problem.
- The 5 main parts are:
  - question to be resolved
  - context
  - key stakeholders
  - success criteria
  - constraints
- The question to be resolved summarizes the main goal of the analysis.
- Context explains why the business problem exists and what factors affect it.
- Key stakeholders are the people with influence or impact in the project.
- Success criteria should be measurable and follow the SMART framework.
- Constraints are the limits that affect the project or possible solutions.
- In the Simply Dumplings example, the framework shows a profitability problem shaped by rising costs, competition, and operational issues.
- The framework makes project boundaries clearer and reduces miscommunication.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- A problem statement framework is not the same as a solution plan.
- It helps turn broad business problems into a more structured and manageable analysis.
- Good success criteria make it easier to evaluate whether the final solution worked.
- Constraints are especially important because they show what the company can realistically do.
- This framework is useful during early project planning and when checking alignment with stakeholders.

---

# Final Summary
A problem statement framework is a simple but powerful way to organize the key parts of a business analysis project. It helps define the main question, explain the business context, identify the important stakeholders, set measurable success criteria, and describe the constraints that may affect the work. In the Simply Dumplings example, this framework clearly shows that the company’s problem is declining profitability, explains the business conditions around that problem, identifies the decision-makers, sets measurable targets for improvement, and outlines the budget and time limits. Overall, the framework helps keep the project focused, realistic, and easy to communicate.
