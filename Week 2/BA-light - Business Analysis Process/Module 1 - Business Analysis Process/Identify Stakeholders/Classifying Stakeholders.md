# Power/Interest Grid in Stakeholder Analysis

## Main Idea
A **power/interest grid** (also called a **power/interest matrix**) is a tool used to classify stakeholders based on:

- their **power**
- their **interest**

This helps a business analyst create a communication plan that matches each stakeholder’s:

- level of influence
- level of involvement
- importance to the project

---

## What the Power/Interest Grid Is Used For
The power/interest grid helps you:

- classify stakeholders
- decide how closely to work with each stakeholder
- plan communication more effectively
- understand whose feedback should be prioritized
- know how to manage different stakeholder relationships

### Important lesson
Not every stakeholder should be managed the same way.  
Some need close attention, while others only need occasional updates.

---

# Reminder: What Power and Interest Mean

## Power
**Power** is how much influence a stakeholder has over:

- the project
- the business process
- decisions being made

## Interest
**Interest** is how involved or concerned a stakeholder is in:

- the project
- the business process
- the outcome

---

## Important Clarification
Power and interest should be judged based on the **specific project or business problem**, not the stakeholder’s importance in the company overall.

### Example
A senior leader may have high power in the company, but if they are not directly involved in a certain project, they may have **low power** for that specific project.

### Important lesson
Always analyze power and interest in relation to the **current business problem**, not the company as a whole.

---

# The 4 Quadrants of the Power/Interest Grid

---

## 1. High Interest, High Power

### Who they are
These are the **most important stakeholders**.

They:

- care strongly about the outcome
- are deeply involved in the business
- have strong influence over decisions

### How to manage them
You should:

- identify them early
- provide them with information regularly
- manage their needs and expectations carefully
- build strong relationships with them
- consider their opinions seriously
- make sure they support the project throughout

### Why they matter
These stakeholders usually have the biggest impact on whether the project succeeds.

---

## 2. High Interest, Low Power

### Who they are
These stakeholders care a lot about the project and are closely involved, but they do not have much decision-making power.

They often help with:

- details of the project
- day-to-day information
- implementation support
- practical insight about how things work

### How to manage them
You should:

- keep them informed
- make them feel heard
- acknowledge their input
- involve them in useful ways

### Why they matter
Even though they cannot make final decisions, they often provide important details that help the project move forward.

---

## 3. Low Interest, High Power

### Who they are
These stakeholders have strong influence over decisions, but they are not directly involved in the project or business process analysis.

### How to manage them
You should:

- keep them satisfied
- stay aligned with their guidelines
- make sure they remain supportive
- communicate enough so they stay on your side

### Why they matter
Even if they are not very involved, they can still strongly affect the project because of their power.

---

## 4. Low Interest, Low Power

### Who they are
These stakeholders:

- are not very interested in the project outcome
- are not very involved
- often may not even know the analysis is happening

### How to manage them
You should:

- monitor them
- provide relevant updates when needed
- watch in case their role changes over time

### Why they matter
Even though they are not very important right now, their influence or involvement could increase later.

---

# Summary of How to Manage Each Quadrant

| Quadrant | Meaning | How to Manage |
|---|---|---|
| **High Power, High Interest** | Most important stakeholders | **Manage closely** |
| **High Interest, Low Power** | Involved but less influential | **Keep informed** |
| **Low Interest, High Power** | Influential but less involved | **Keep satisfied** |
| **Low Interest, Low Power** | Less involved and less influential | **Monitor** |

# Why the Power/Interest Grid Matters
A business analyst often needs to gather important information by interviewing stakeholders.

To do that well, you need to understand:

- what each stakeholder values
- what each stakeholder needs
- how much influence they have
- how involved they are

### Why this helps
When you understand stakeholders properly, you can communicate with them in a way that is:

- relevant
- functional
- direct
- effective

This makes it easier to:

- interview them well
- gather useful information
- keep the project moving smoothly

---

# Big Lesson
Stakeholder management is not just about knowing who the stakeholders are.

It is also about knowing:

- how much power they have
- how much interest they have
- how to communicate with them
- how much attention each one needs

The power/interest grid gives you a structured way to do that.

---

# Key Takeaways
- A power/interest grid is used to classify stakeholders by their power and interest.
- It helps analysts create a communication plan based on each stakeholder’s influence and involvement.
- Power and interest should be judged based on the specific business problem, not the company overall.
- The four stakeholder groups are:
  - high power, high interest
  - high interest, low power
  - low interest, high power
  - low interest, low power
- High power, high interest stakeholders should be managed closely.
- High interest, low power stakeholders should be kept informed.
- Low interest, high power stakeholders should be kept satisfied.
- Low power, low interest stakeholders should be monitored.
- In the Simply Dumplings example:
  - Daniel Tanaka is high power, low interest
  - Olivia Kim, Ethan Martinez, and Mei Chen are high power, high interest
  - store managers are low power, high interest
  - customers and restaurant employees are low power, low interest
- Understanding stakeholders well helps analysts interview them better and gather stronger information.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- A stakeholder’s company title does not automatically decide their power in every project.
- Some stakeholders with low power may still be extremely valuable because they know the process details.
- Stakeholder involvement can change over time, so monitoring is important.
- Good stakeholder classification helps prevent over-communicating with some people and under-communicating with others.
- The power/interest grid is a practical tool for deciding how much attention each stakeholder needs.

---

# Final Summary
A power/interest grid is a stakeholder analysis tool that helps classify people based on how much influence they have and how involved they are in a project. This makes it easier for a business analyst to know how to communicate with each stakeholder and how much attention each one requires. The four main groups are manage closely, keep informed, keep satisfied, and monitor. In the Simply Dumplings example, executives are high power and high interest, the board member is high power and low interest, store managers are high interest and low power, and customers and employees are low power and low interest. Using this grid helps analysts manage relationships more effectively and gather the right information from the right people.
