# Stakeholder Analysis: Power and Interest

## Main Idea
After identifying the stakeholders in a project or business process, the next step is to **analyze them individually**.

A common way to do this is by looking at **two factors**:

- **Power**
- **Interest**

This helps a business analyst understand:

- how important each stakeholder is to the project
- how much influence they have
- how involved they are
- how to interact with them effectively

---

# What Power Means

## Definition
**Power** is the level of influence a stakeholder has on:

- the project
- the business process
- overall decision-making

### Simple meaning
Power tells you how much a stakeholder can affect what happens.

A stakeholder with high power may be able to:

- make final decisions
- approve or reject changes
- control resources
- influence project direction

---

# What Interest Means

## Definition
**Interest** is the level of involvement a stakeholder has with:

- the project
- the business process
- overall decision-making

### Simple meaning
Interest tells you how much the stakeholder cares about or is affected by the project.

A stakeholder with high interest is usually:

- closely involved
- more affected by outcomes
- more likely to want updates or input

---

# Important Clarification

## Power and interest are project-specific
A stakeholder’s power and interest in a **specific project** are not always the same as their power and interest in the company overall.

### Example
A senior leader or director may have a lot of power in the company generally, but may have **low power in a project** if:

- they are not directly involved
- the project is outside their department
- they are not part of the decision-making for that issue

### Important lesson
When doing stakeholder analysis, focus on the stakeholder’s:

- power in **this project or business process**
- interest in **this project or business process**

not their general company status.

---

# What Is Stakeholder Analysis?

## Definition
**Stakeholder analysis** is a technique used to:

- identify stakeholders
- analyze them
- classify them

This helps you decide:

- who matters most in the project
- how to communicate with each person
- what kind of information or support you may need from them

---

# How to Analyze Stakeholders

When analyzing stakeholders, classify each one as:

- **high power** or **low power**
- **high interest** or **low interest**

This gives you a simple way to understand how important they are to the project.

---

# Example from the Reading

## VP of Sales Example
Suppose the **VP of Sales** asks you to investigate the company’s sales process.

She would likely have:

- **high power** because she makes final decisions about that process
- **high interest** because the process belongs to her department

### Why this example matters
It shows that a person can have both high power and high interest when:

- they are directly connected to the problem
- and they have authority over the outcome

---

# Questions to Determine Stakeholder Interest

Not every stakeholder is easy to classify right away.

The reading gives helpful questions to decide how much **interest** a stakeholder has.

---

## 1. How is each stakeholder involved?

### Why this matters
The more involved a stakeholder is in the project or process, the more interest they usually have.

### Rule of thumb
- **High involvement = high interest**
- **Low involvement = low interest**

### Example
A **shareholder** may be financially invested in the company, but they usually are not involved in daily processes.

So for a specific business process project, they may have:

- **low interest**

---

## 2. Why is your work important to each stakeholder?

### Why this matters
A stakeholder’s interest also depends on how much the project affects them.

If your work has a big impact on their responsibilities, they will likely have higher interest.

### Example
A **manager** usually has high interest in a business process analysis because process changes can affect:

- how they manage their team
- how work is done
- performance expectations

---

# Question to Determine Stakeholder Power

To understand stakeholder **power**, the reading suggests asking:

## What can each stakeholder contribute?

### Why this matters
A stakeholder’s contribution helps show how much influence they have.

### Rule of thumb
A stakeholder has **high power** if they can:

- make final decisions
- strongly influence project outcomes
- approve or block changes
- provide important authority or resources

A stakeholder has **low power** if they:

- are not involved in decision-making
- do not influence outcomes much
- do not contribute significantly to the project direction

### Example
An **executive sponsor** is usually high power because they:

- initiated the analysis
- are highly involved
- influence the direction of the project

---

# Why This Classification Is Useful

Once you label stakeholders as high or low in power and interest, it becomes easier to understand:

- how to interact with them
- how often to communicate with them
- what kind of updates they need
- what role they may play in the project
- what support or information you may need from them

### Important lesson
Stakeholder analysis is not just about knowing who people are.  
It is about knowing **how to work with them**.

---

# Big Lesson
Not all stakeholders should be treated the same way.

Some people:

- care a lot and have strong decision-making power
- care a lot but cannot make decisions
- have power but are not very involved
- have little power and little interest

Understanding this helps a business analyst:

- prioritize communication
- manage expectations
- build better stakeholder relationships
- keep the project moving smoothly

---

# Key Takeaways
- After identifying stakeholders, the next step is to analyze them individually.
- Stakeholder analysis focuses mainly on **power** and **interest**.
- **Power** means the level of influence a stakeholder has on the project or decisions.
- **Interest** means the level of involvement or concern a stakeholder has in the project.
- Power and interest should be judged based on the specific project, not the company overall.
- A stakeholder analysis helps identify, analyze, and classify stakeholders.
- Stakeholders can be labeled as:
  - high power or low power
  - high interest or low interest
- To determine interest, ask:
  - How is the stakeholder involved?
  - Why is your work important to them?
- To determine power, ask:
  - What can the stakeholder contribute?
- A stakeholder who makes final decisions usually has high power.
- A stakeholder who is highly involved usually has high interest.
- Classifying stakeholders helps you understand how to communicate and work with them effectively.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- Senior job title does not automatically mean high power in every project.
- Financial investment does not always mean high interest in day-to-day project details.
- Managers often have high interest because process changes affect how they run their teams.
- Executive sponsors often have high power because they help launch and guide projects.
- Stakeholder analysis helps analysts decide where to focus their time and communication.

---

# Final Summary
After identifying project stakeholders, a business analyst needs to analyze them based on power and interest. Power shows how much influence a stakeholder has over the project or decisions, and interest shows how involved or affected they are. These levels should be judged based on the specific project, not the person’s role in the company overall. By asking how each stakeholder is involved, why the work matters to them, and what they can contribute, analysts can classify stakeholders as high or low in power and interest. This makes it easier to know how to engage with each person and prioritize communication throughout the project.
