# Primary Business Objectives in Business Analysis

## Main Idea
The first step in the business analysis process is to understand:

- the **business problem**
- the **business objectives**

If you want your analysis to stay focused and be useful, you need to know exactly what the company is trying to achieve.

---

## What Are Primary Business Objectives?
**Primary business objectives** are the main outcomes a company wants to accomplish.

These objectives are important because they:

- guide your analysis
- help you stay on track
- make sure your work supports the company’s larger goals

### Important point
Identifying primary objectives is one of the **first** and **most important** tasks in business analysis.

---

# Common Business Objectives

The reading explains 3 common business objectives that many companies have:

1. **Profitability**
2. **Finding the Right Customers**
3. **Human Resources**

---

# 1. Profitability

## What it means
A company needs to earn more money than it spends in order to continue operating.

### Profitability means:
- **revenue exceeds costs**

This sounds simple, but actually achieving profitability depends on many parts of the business working well.

---

## Growing Sustainably

### What it means
A business needs a growth plan that can continue over time without constantly depending on outside funding.

In high-growth situations, many companies:

- spend more money than they earn at first
- grow their business over time
- eventually reach a point where revenue catches up and they become profitable

This is called a **sustainable growth model**.

### Why it matters
If a company tries to grow but:

- cannot reduce costs
- or cannot improve revenue over time

then people may question whether its growth is sustainable.

---

## Positive Cash Flow

### What it means
A company has **positive cash flow** when:

- more money is coming into the business through sales and revenue
- than leaving the business through expenses

### Important detail
Even companies with positive cash flow may still need financing to support growth.

Examples of costs that affect cash flow:
- hiring employees
- marketing campaigns
- buying or leasing office space

These costs happen before expansion starts generating more revenue.

### Why it matters
To maintain profitability, a company has to manage cash flow carefully and make sure it can stay financially stable in the long term.

---

# 2. Finding the Right Customers

## Why this matters
A business cannot survive without customers, even if its product or solution is very good.

If customers:

- understand the product
- like it
- feel it solves their problem

they are more likely to tell others about it.

This creates strong **word-of-mouth growth**.

---

## Targeted Marketing
Finding the right customers usually requires **targeted marketing**.

This means marketing is aimed at the people most likely to use the product.

### Why this matters
A good marketing campaign helps the company:

- attract the right audience
- increase awareness
- build market presence

---

## Paid Marketing

### What it means
Most companies spend part of their budget to advertise their product to the right customers.

Examples include:
- Google Ads
- Facebook ads
- paid social media campaigns

### Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
The effectiveness of paid marketing is often measured using **customer acquisition cost (CAC)**.

### CAC formula:
- **cost of the marketing campaign / number of customers generated**

### Why CAC matters
It helps the company decide whether a campaign is worth the cost.

If CAC is too high, the campaign may not be efficient.

---

## Organic Marketing

### What it means
**Organic marketing** happens without direct cost to the company.

Examples:
- customers sharing posts on social media
- customers recommending the product by word of mouth

### Why it matters
Organic marketing is ideal because:

- it costs the company nothing
- it often means customers are happy
- it helps the business reach the right people naturally

The happier the customer is and the better the product solves their problem, the more likely they are to recommend it.

---

# 3. Human Resources

## What it means
Human resources refers to the people working for the company.

Employees are one of a company’s most important resources.

Hiring the right people allows the company to benefit from:

- experience
- skills
- passion
- productivity

---

## Training

### Why training matters
New employees need time, support, and resources to learn the business.

This includes:
- onboarding
- learning company processes
- building the skills needed for the role

### Why training costs money
Training costs the company because:

- materials and trainers are needed
- new employees usually produce less than experienced employees at first

### Important lesson
A strong training plan is important because it helps employees become productive more effectively.

---

## Employee Retention

### Why retention matters
When employees leave, it costs the company money.

These costs include:
- lost productivity
- job advertising
- recruiting agencies
- hiring replacements
- training new employees

### How companies improve retention
A company can improve employee retention by creating a work environment where employees:

- feel productive
- feel happy
- feel connected to the company
- feel empowered by their work

### Important lesson
High-value employees usually have many opportunities elsewhere, so keeping them engaged and supported is very important.

# Real-World Scenario: Simply Dumplings

## Company background
**Simply Dumplings** is a fast-casual Chinese restaurant with:

- **12 locations**
- across **Seattle**

They serve:
- dumplings
- appetizers

Their goal is to provide:
- high-quality food
- competitive prices
- a healthier alternative to standard fast food

---

## Business problem
Over the past **two years**, Simply Dumplings has struggled with:

- lower profitability
- declining profits

Because of this, the company is thinking about making **operational changes** in the less profitable locations in order to stay afloat.

As the business analyst, you are brought in to investigate the problem.

---

## What you learn from the COO
After speaking with the **chief operations officer (COO)**, you find out that the company’s primary business objectives are:

- reaching and maintaining **profitability**
- developing **brand loyalty**
- minimizing **production costs**

---

## Why this matters
The business problem is clearly connected to one of the company’s main goals: **profitability**.

That means the project is worth investigating because solving the problem will directly support an important company objective.

### Big lesson from the example
When you understand a company’s primary business objectives at the start, you can:

- focus your analysis better
- build solutions that support the business
- make sure your work contributes to the company’s overall success

---

# Why Primary Business Objectives Matter in Analysis

Understanding primary business objectives helps you:

- stay focused on the right problem
- connect your project to company goals
- create solutions that have real business value
- decide how success will be measured

### Important point
Business objectives are not just background information.  
They are the foundation that guides the whole analysis.

---

# Key Takeaways
- The first step in business analysis is understanding the business problem and objectives.
- Primary business objectives are the main outcomes a company wants to achieve.
- Identifying these objectives early helps guide the analysis and keep the project aligned with company goals.
- Common business objectives include:
  - profitability
  - finding the right customers
  - human resources
- Profitability depends on things like sustainable growth and positive cash flow.
- Finding the right customers often depends on effective paid and organic marketing.
- Customer acquisition cost (CAC) helps measure the effectiveness of paid marketing.
- Human resources includes training employees and keeping valuable employees in the company.
- Employee turnover is expensive, so retention matters.
- In the Simply Dumplings example, the main objectives were profitability, brand loyalty, and minimizing production costs.
- Knowing the objectives early helps the analyst build a solution that supports the company’s success.

---

# Extra Important Notes
- A business objective gives purpose to your analysis.
- If the project is not clearly connected to a business objective, it may not create much value.
- Profitability is affected by many business areas, not just sales.
- Marketing is not only about visibility — it is about reaching the right customers efficiently.
- Employees are a major business resource, so training and retention are strategic priorities.
- Understanding objectives early also makes it easier to define useful metrics later.

---

# Final Summary
Primary business objectives are the main results a company wants to achieve, and understanding them is one of the first and most important steps in business analysis. These objectives help guide the project and ensure the analysis supports the company’s larger goals. Common objectives include profitability, finding the right customers, and managing human resources effectively. The Simply Dumplings example shows how knowing the company’s main goals upfront gives the analyst a clear direction for investigating the problem and creating a solution that contributes to the company’s overall success.
