# Business Analysis Process: Final Key Takeaways

## Main Topic

This section summarizes the full **business analysis process** and the most important concepts from the course.

The business analysis process helps analysts identify, investigate, and solve business problems in a structured way.

---

# What Is a Business Analysis Process?

A **business analysis process** is a series of steps used to:

- identify business problems
- investigate the causes of those problems
- understand business goals and requirements
- develop possible solutions
- support implementation
- evaluate whether the solution created value

To do this well, Business Analysts need to understand:

- the business problem
- business goals
- stakeholder needs
- project requirements
- success criteria
- constraints
- possible solutions

---

# Main Stages of the Business Analysis Process

The business analysis process can be broadly divided into three main stages:

1. **Problem Assessment**
2. **Business Requirement Preparation**
3. **Solution Implementation**

---

# 1. Problem Assessment

## What It Means

**Problem assessment** is the first stage of the business analysis process.

It focuses on understanding the problem before trying to solve it.

During this stage, the analyst works to understand:

- what the problem is
- why the problem matters
- what business objectives are connected to the problem
- who the stakeholders are
- what success should look like
- what constraints may affect the project

---

## Main Activities in Problem Assessment

Problem assessment usually includes:

- understanding the business problem and objectives
- identifying and analyzing stakeholders
- defining the problem statement

---

# Business Objectives

## Definition

**Primary business objectives** are the outcomes you want to accomplish with your analysis.

They guide the project and help the analyst stay focused.

Examples of business objectives include:

- increasing profitability
- reducing costs
- improving customer satisfaction
- improving employee retention
- increasing revenue
- reducing risk
- improving operational efficiency

---

# Metrics and KPIs

## Metrics

**Metrics** are quantifiable measures used to track the status of a business or process.

Examples:

- number of website visitors
- sales revenue
- customer complaints
- shipping costs
- abandoned cart rate

---

## Key Performance Indicators

A **Key Performance Indicator**, or **KPI**, is a metric connected to a specific business goal.

All KPIs are metrics, but not all metrics are KPIs.

### Example

If the business goal is to reduce shipping costs, then a KPI could be:

- shipping cost per order

---

# Stakeholders

## What Is a Stakeholder?

A **stakeholder** is anyone affected by a business problem.

Stakeholders are important because they often:

- use the business process daily
- provide information about the problem
- give requirements
- approve decisions
- influence project success
- are affected by the final solution

---

# Internal and External Stakeholders

## Internal Stakeholders

**Internal stakeholders** are people or groups inside the business.

Examples:

- employees
- managers
- business owners
- executives
- project teams

## External Stakeholders

**External stakeholders** are people or groups outside the business who affect or are affected by the business.

Examples:

- customers
- suppliers
- government
- creditors
- shareholders
- society

---

# Stakeholder Management

## What It Means

**Stakeholder management** means understanding and managing the needs, interests, motivations, and concerns of stakeholders.

It helps the analyst:

- gather better information
- avoid conflict
- understand the environment around the problem
- identify risks
- build support for the project

---

# Stakeholder Analysis

## Definition

**Stakeholder analysis** is a technique used to identify, analyze, and classify stakeholders in a project.

It usually considers:

- stakeholder power
- stakeholder interest

---

# Power/Interest Grid

## Definition

A **power/interest grid** is a tool used to classify stakeholders based on their level of power and interest in a project.

It helps the analyst decide how to communicate with different stakeholders.

---

## Power/Interest Categories

| Stakeholder Type | Meaning | How to Manage |
|---|---|---|
| High Power, High Interest | Most important stakeholders | Manage closely |
| High Power, Low Interest | Influential but not deeply involved | Keep satisfied |
| Low Power, High Interest | Interested but limited decision power | Keep informed |
| Low Power, Low Interest | Limited involvement and influence | Monitor |

---

# Problem Statement

## Definition

A **problem statement** is a clear description of what is wrong in the current business process.

It explains:

- what the problem is
- why the project is being done
- who is affected
- what constraints exist
- what success criteria will be used

A problem statement is like a hypothesis about what is wrong.

### Important Note

A problem statement should not propose a solution.

---

# 2. Business Requirement Preparation

## What It Means

**Business requirement preparation** is the stage where the analyst gathers, analyzes, visualizes, and documents what the solution must achieve.

This stage helps turn the business problem into clear requirements and a practical path forward.

---

## Main Activities in Business Requirement Preparation

This stage usually includes:

- gathering and analyzing business requirements
- creating supporting visual models
- documenting business requirements

---

# Business Requirements

## Definition

**Business requirements** are specific conditions provided by stakeholders that a solution must meet to solve a business problem.

They help ensure the final solution:

- meets stakeholder needs
- supports business objectives
- solves the correct problem
- stays within scope

---

# Requirements Elicitation

## Definition

**Requirements elicitation** is the process of gathering business needs from stakeholders.

During elicitation, analysts may:

- interview stakeholders
- analyze business processes
- observe users
- run workshops or focus groups
- conduct surveys
- review documents
- study systems or interfaces

---

# Requirements Elicitation Techniques

## 1. Interviews

Interviews allow analysts to have detailed one-on-one conversations with stakeholders.

They are useful for:

- gathering deep insights
- understanding stakeholder needs
- clarifying unclear requirements
- discussing sensitive topics
- asking follow-up questions

---

## 2. Brainstorming

Brainstorming brings stakeholders together to generate ideas.

It helps analysts:

- collect different perspectives
- identify possible solutions
- observe common themes
- encourage creative thinking

---

## 3. Interface Analysis

Interface analysis allows analysts to watch users interact with a product, process, or system.

It helps uncover:

- user experience problems
- confusing steps
- poor functionality
- pain points
- opportunities for improvement

---

# Categories of Business Requirements

## Functional Requirements

**Functional requirements** describe how a product, service, or solution should work from the end user’s perspective.

They explain the features and functions the user directly interacts with.

---

## Operational Requirements

**Operational requirements** are procedures that must happen in the background so the product or process works over time.

---

## Technical Requirements

**Technical requirements** are technical challenges or conditions that must be addressed to implement the solution successfully.

---

## Transitional Requirements

**Transitional requirements** are steps needed to move from the old process or product to the new one.

Examples:

- training users
- migrating data
- updating documentation
- changing workflows

---

# Visual Models in Business Analysis

## Why Visual Models Are Important

Business analysis can become complicated because processes and systems may have developed over time without clear documentation.

Visual models help analysts and stakeholders understand:

- how processes work
- how business areas connect
- where gaps exist
- where inefficiencies happen
- who is responsible for each step
- how requirements relate to the process

---

# Process Flow Diagram

## Definition

A **process flow diagram** is a flowchart that shows a specific business process being analyzed.

It shows:

- process steps
- decisions
- actions
- sequence
- start and end points

---

# Process Flowchart

## Definition

A **process flowchart** is a visual representation of the sequence of steps and decisions needed to perform a process.

It is one of the most common process maps.

---

# Swimlane Diagram

## Definition

A **swimlane diagram** is similar to a flowchart, but it separates the steps by the team, person, or department responsible for them.

It is also called a **cross-functional map**.

## Why It Is Useful

It helps show:

- who owns each step
- where handoffs happen
- which teams are involved
- where responsibility may be unclear

---

# Value Stream Map

## Definition

A **value stream map** is a process diagram often used in **lean management**.

Lean management focuses on continuous improvement through small process changes that improve efficiency.

A value stream map analyzes the steps needed to:

- create a product
- deliver a service
- improve efficiency
- reduce waste

---

# SIPOC Diagram

## Definition

A **SIPOC diagram** is a simplified, high-level process map.

SIPOC stands for:

- **Suppliers**
- **Inputs**
- **Processes**
- **Outputs**
- **Customers**

It focuses on the essential elements of a process.

---

# Business Domain Model

## Definition

A **business domain model** is a visual model that shows different parts of a business and how they connect.

It gives a high-level, bird’s-eye view of the business.

---

## Business Domain Model vs Process Flow Diagram

| Model | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Process Flow Diagram | Shows a specific process step by step |
| Business Domain Model | Shows how business elements connect at a high level |

---

# Classes

## Definition

**Classes** are parts of a business process or system in a business domain model.

Examples:

- customer
- order
- product

Classes are usually shown as boxes.

---

# Associations

## Definition

**Associations** describe how classes connect to each other.

Example:

- a customer places an order
- an order contains a product
- the order connects the customer and product

---

# 3. Business Requirements Documentation

## What Is a BRD?

A **Business Requirements Document**, or **BRD**, is a formal document shared with stakeholders.

It outlines:

- the scope of the analysis
- the business requirements
- the business problem
- the proposed solution
- the project schedule
- constraints
- success measures

---

# Purpose of a BRD

The purpose of a BRD is to keep everyone aligned.

It acts as a reference point for decisions about:

- priorities
- timelines
- scope
- project management
- business goals
- requirements

---

# Why a BRD Matters

A well-written BRD helps:

- keep the analysis project organized
- keep the project on track
- clearly describe the problem
- define successful outcomes
- prevent confusion
- support stakeholder decisions

---

# Common BRD Components

Most BRDs include:

- project summary
- needs statement
- stakeholder information
- business requirements
- project scope
- project schedule
- measurable outcomes
- proposed solution
- project constraints

---

# BRD Components Explained

## Overall Project Summary

A concise overview of the project.

It includes:

- vision
- goals
- business context
- purpose
- scope overview

---

## Needs Statement

A clear explanation of the business need or problem.

It explains:

- why the project is needed
- what problem must be addressed
- what gap exists

---

## Measurable Outcomes

Specific metrics or KPIs that define project success.

They help answer:

- How will we know the project worked?
- What improvement are we measuring?

---

## Project Scope

Project scope defines what is included and excluded from the project.

It helps prevent scope creep.

---

## Stakeholders

This section lists the people, groups, or organizations involved in or affected by the project.

It may include their roles, influence, and interest.

---

## Business Requirements

Detailed statements of what the project must achieve to meet the business need.

---

## Proposed Solution

A description of the proposed approach to solving the problem.

It may include:

- process changes
- technology updates
- workflow improvements
- action plans

---

## Schedule

A timeline of the project.

It includes:

- phases
- milestones
- deadlines

---

## Constraints

Limitations the project must work within.

Examples:

- budget
- time
- resources
- staffing
- regulations
- technology limits

---

# Scope

## Definition

**Scope** is the work that the Business Analyst and stakeholders decide is part of the project.

Scope defines the project boundaries.

---

# In Scope

**In scope** means the work falls within the boundaries of the project.

It is part of the planned work.

---

# Out of Scope

**Out of scope** means the work was not planned as part of the project.

It should not be completed unless the scope is officially changed.

---

# Scope Creep

## Definition

**Scope creep** happens when the work or requirements of a project increase or change over time.

## Why It Is a Problem

Scope creep can cause:

- project delays
- budget problems
- incomplete work
- unhappy stakeholders
- project failure

---

# Best Practices for Writing a BRD

A strong BRD should:

- include specific requirements
- center the solution around the customer’s problem
- keep requirements updated
- leave space for creativity
- use affirmative statements
- avoid jargon
- include visuals when helpful
- align requirements with outcomes

---

# Insights

## Definition

**Insights** are actionable trends and patterns uncovered through analysis.

They are more than just statistics.

A statistic tells what happened.  
An insight explains what the data means and what action may be needed.

---

# 4. Solution Implementation

## What It Means

After the BRD is completed and shared, the project moves into solution implementation.

This stage brings the solution to life.

---

# Role of the Project Manager

Implementation is often handed off to the **Project Manager**.

The Project Manager usually manages:

- project execution
- tasks
- deadlines
- team members
- resources
- budget
- progress monitoring

---

# Role of the Business Analyst

The Business Analyst may still support implementation by:

- clarifying requirements
- explaining the BRD
- answering questions
- making sure the solution stays aligned with business needs

---

# Solution Iteration

## Definition

**Solution iteration** is the process of refining and improving a solution through repeated cycles of implementation and evaluation.

## Why It Matters

A solution may need to change after implementation because of:

- stakeholder feedback
- evaluation results
- changing requirements
- unexpected problems
- new business needs

---

# Evaluating Value Created

After implementation, the solution must be evaluated to see whether it created value.

This is done by tracking:

- success criteria
- KPIs
- supporting metrics
- long-term outcomes

---

# Dashboard

## Definition

A **dashboard** is a data visualization tool that displays KPIs and other important business metrics on one screen.

Dashboards help stakeholders quickly understand whether the solution is working.

---

# Big Course Summary

The business analysis process helps analysts move from identifying a problem to supporting a solution.

First, the analyst assesses the problem by understanding business objectives, identifying stakeholders, and defining the problem statement. Then the analyst prepares business requirements by gathering stakeholder needs, analyzing requirements, creating visual models, and documenting everything in a BRD. Finally, the chosen solution is implemented, iterated, and evaluated using metrics, KPIs, and dashboards.

The main goal of business analysis is to solve the right problem, meet stakeholder needs, support business goals, and create measurable value.
