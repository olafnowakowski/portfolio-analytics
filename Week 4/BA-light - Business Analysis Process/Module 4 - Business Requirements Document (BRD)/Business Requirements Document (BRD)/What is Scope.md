# Project Scope, Scope Creep, Budget, and Schedule

## Main Idea

A project needs clear boundaries.

If the project scope is not clear, the work can grow beyond the original problem. This can cause extra costs, delays, confusion, and even project failure.

---

# Faucet Example

## Original Problem

Your kitchen faucet is broken.

## Original Requirements

Any solution must meet these requirements:

- the new kitchen faucet must be functional
- the new kitchen faucet must look good

## What Happened

You found a faucet you liked, but it needed three sink holes instead of two.

Then you bought:

- a new sink
- new countertops
- a new tile backsplash
- possibly new cabinet pulls or cabinets

## What Went Wrong

The original problem was only:

**The faucet is broken.**

But the project turned into a kitchen remodel.

This happened because:

1. The requirements were not specific enough.
2. The project scope was not controlled.

---

# What Is Project Scope?

## Definition

**Project scope** is the work that the analyst and stakeholders agree is part of the project.

It defines the boundaries of the project.

Project scope explains:

- what work is included
- what work is not included
- what problem is being solved
- what goals must be achieved
- what outcomes are expected

---

# Why Scope Is Important

Clear project scope helps the analyst understand:

- how long the project will take
- how much the project will cost
- what work needs to be completed
- when the project is finished
- what should not be done

### Important Lesson

If a project is not clearly scoped, it is hard to manage.

---

# In Scope vs Out of Scope

## In Scope

**In scope** means the activity is inside the agreed project boundaries.

It is part of the project and supports the project goal.

## Out of Scope

**Out of scope** means the activity is outside the agreed project boundaries.

It was not planned as part of the project and does not directly support the project goal.

---

# Faucet Example: In Scope and Out of Scope

## In Scope

The following work was in scope:

- fixing the broken faucet
- replacing the broken faucet

This directly solves the original problem.

## Out of Scope

The following work was out of scope:

- replacing the sink
- replacing the countertops
- adding a tile backsplash
- changing cabinet pulls
- installing new cabinets

These activities did not directly solve the original problem.

---

# How to Decide If Something Is In Scope

Ask:

- Is this work clearly tied to the project goal?
- Does this work help solve the original problem?
- Was this work agreed on by stakeholders?
- Is this work required to meet the project requirements?

If the answer is no, the work is probably out of scope.

---

# What Is Scope Creep?

## Definition

**Scope creep** happens when the work or requirements of a project increase or change over time without proper control.

It usually happens when new ideas, opportunities, or requests are added after the project has already started.

---

# Why Scope Creep Is Dangerous

Scope creep can cause:

- the project to go over budget
- the project to be late
- the project to become incomplete
- stakeholders to become unhappy
- the team to lose focus
- confusion about priorities
- project failure

---

# Why Scope Creep Happens

Scope creep can happen when:

- requirements are vague
- stakeholders keep adding new requests
- the project goal is unclear
- the analyst does not define boundaries
- the team follows interesting ideas that are not part of the original plan
- stakeholders confuse related work with required work

---

# Analyst Responsibility

As a Business Analyst, your job is to focus on the work connected to the project.

You should complete only the work that is part of the agreed scope.

Work outside the project scope is not your responsibility unless the scope is officially changed and approved.

---

# Budget and Schedule

Project scope is closely connected to two other project management areas:

1. **Budget**
2. **Schedule**

---

## Budget

**Budget** is the amount of money available for the project.

If the scope grows, the project may need more money.

## Schedule

**Schedule** is the amount of time the team has to complete the project.

If the scope grows, the project may take longer.

---

# How Scope Changes Affect Budget and Schedule

Any change in scope can affect:

- project cost
- project timeline
- team workload
- resource needs
- stakeholder expectations

### Important Lesson

Even a small change in scope can create a larger impact on time and budget.

---

# Big Lesson

A project should stay focused on the original problem, goals, and agreed requirements.

If the scope is not controlled, a small project can turn into something much larger than planned.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Project scope defines what work is included in a project.
- Scope also defines what work is not included.
- In-scope work supports the project goal.
- Out-of-scope work does not directly support the project goal.
- The broken faucet example shows how unclear scope can turn a small fix into a full remodel.
- Scope creep happens when project work or requirements expand over time.
- Scope creep can cause delays, extra costs, incomplete work, unhappy stakeholders, and project failure.
- Requirements should be specific to help control scope.
- The Business Analyst should keep the team focused on the agreed project scope.
- Budget is the money available for the project.
- Schedule is the time available to complete the project.
- Changes in scope can affect both budget and schedule.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- Scope should be aligned with project goals and outcomes.
- Clear scope helps stakeholders understand project boundaries.
- If new work is requested, the analyst should check whether it supports the original project goal.
- Not every good idea belongs in the current project.
- A clear BRD can help control scope by documenting what is included and excluded.
- Scope control protects the project from becoming too large, too expensive, or too slow.

---

# Final Summary

Project scope is the agreed work that belongs inside a project. It creates a clear boundary between what the team should and should not do. In the kitchen faucet example, fixing or replacing the broken faucet was in scope, but replacing the sink, countertops, backsplash, and cabinets was out of scope. This is an example of scope creep, where the project grows beyond its original purpose. Scope creep can lead to delays, extra costs, incomplete work, unhappy stakeholders, and project failure. Because scope affects both budget and schedule, Business Analysts must define and manage scope carefully to keep the project focused and on track.
