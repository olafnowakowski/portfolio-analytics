# Business Requirements Document Sections

## Main Idea

A **Business Requirements Document (BRD)** is a formal document that explains the project, the business need, the requirements, and the proposed path toward solving the problem.

It gives stakeholders transparency into the analysis process and helps everyone understand:

- why the project exists
- what problem is being solved
- what requirements must be met
- what work is in scope
- what outcomes define success
- what constraints may affect the project

---

# Why Audience Matters

When creating and presenting a BRD, you need to know your audience.

Different stakeholders may care about different parts of the document.

For example:

- executives may care most about business goals, cost, and outcomes
- project managers may care about scope, schedule, and risks
- operational teams may care about requirements and implementation details
- technical teams may care about constraints and solution details

### Important Lesson

A BRD should be clear enough for all relevant stakeholders to understand and use.

---

# Common BRD Categories

Different companies may use different BRD templates, but most BRDs include sections such as:

- overall project summary
- needs statement
- measurable outcomes
- project scope
- stakeholders
- business requirements
- details of the proposed solution
- schedule
- constraints

---

# EcoMart Scenario

## Business Situation

EcoMart is a retail business trying to reduce shipping costs.

In recent years, EcoMart’s shipping volume has increased significantly because the company has grown.

Employees need to review their shipping processes and materials to find ways to improve the shipping workflow and reduce costs.

---

# 1. Overall Project Summary

## What It Is

The **overall project summary** provides the framing elements of the project.

It explains:

- the project vision
- the project goals
- the business context
- the intended outcome

This section gives stakeholders a quick summary without requiring them to read the entire BRD.

---

## EcoMart Example

EcoMart’s project summary could include:

- The company will reduce shipping costs by **5% over two years** by investing in more cost-efficient packaging supplies.
- The company will review shipping department processes and related processes across other departments to identify inefficiencies that affect shipping costs.

### Important Lesson

The project summary should quickly explain what the project is trying to achieve and why it matters.

---

# 2. Needs Statement

## What It Is

The **needs statement** explains the business need behind the project.

It clearly describes:

- the problem or gap
- why the project is needed
- what business issue must be addressed
- why stakeholders should care

---

## Relationship to Problem Statement

Earlier, you learned about a **problem statement**.

A problem statement clearly describes the problem, but it does not propose a solution.

The needs statement may use the problem statement as a starting point, but it may evolve after requirements elicitation.

---

## What an Effective Needs Statement Does

An effective needs statement should convince stakeholders that the project addresses a meaningful business need.

It should explain:

- what is wrong
- what could happen if nothing changes
- why the problem matters
- how the project supports business priorities

---

## EcoMart Example

EcoMart’s needs statement could include:

- Over the past two years, the company has grown and the number of products shipped has increased.
- If EcoMart continues using its existing systems, shipping costs may become unsustainable.
- Rising shipping costs could reduce profits by up to **20%**.
- The company needs to identify inefficiencies and explore ways to create a more cost-efficient shipping workflow.

### Important Lesson

The needs statement explains why the project must happen.

---

# 3. Measurable Outcomes

## What They Are

**Measurable outcomes** are the metrics or KPIs used to judge whether the project is successful.

They help keep stakeholders aligned on what success means.

---

## Why They Matter

Measurable outcomes help avoid confusion because everyone can see how success or failure will be measured.

They also help keep the project connected to the company’s goals.

---

## EcoMart Example KPIs

### KPI 1: Cost of Packaging Supplies

Related goal:

- Reduce shipping costs by **5% over two years** by investing in more cost-efficient packaging supplies.

### KPI 2: Time From Order Placement to Shipment Leaving the Warehouse

Related goal:

- Review shipping and related department processes to identify inefficiencies that affect shipping costs.

---

## Important Lesson

KPIs make project success measurable instead of vague.

---

# 4. Project Scope

## What It Is

**Project scope** defines what work is included and what work is excluded from the project.

It creates clear boundaries for the analysis.

---

## Why Scope Matters

A clear scope helps prevent **scope creep**.

Scope creep happens when a project slowly becomes larger and more complex than originally planned.

This can cause:

- delays
- higher costs
- confusion
- missed deadlines
- unclear priorities
- project failure

---

## EcoMart Example Scope

EcoMart’s project scope could include:

- Analysis will include a review of the shipping workflow related to customer orders.
- Analysis will review contracts with packaging providers.
- Analysis will be limited to processes and costs directly related to customer shipping.
- The project will not include processes outside customer shipping.

---

## Out-of-Scope Example

During analysis, the team might find inefficiencies in:

- shipping internal materials to factories
- receiving raw materials from suppliers

However, these areas are outside the project scope if the project is focused only on customer shipping costs.

### Important Lesson

A BRD helps the team avoid chasing unrelated problems.

---

# 5. Stakeholders

## What It Is

The **stakeholders** section lists the people, teams, or organizations involved in or affected by the project.

Stakeholders may be shown as:

- a simple list
- a list with descriptions
- a power/interest grid

---

## EcoMart Example Stakeholders

EcoMart stakeholders could include:

- shipping contractor
- warehouse management
- product transportation team
- shipping coordinators
- factory packaging manufacturer

---

## Why Stakeholders Matter

Stakeholders are important because they may:

- provide requirements
- approve decisions
- explain current processes
- identify risks
- help implement solutions
- be affected by project outcomes

---

# 6. Business Requirements

## What They Are

**Business requirements** are the needs the solution must meet.

These requirements come from the elicitation, analysis, and validation process.

By the time they appear in the BRD, they should already be reviewed and confirmed.

---

## EcoMart Example Requirements

EcoMart’s business requirements could include:

- Use more environmentally sustainable materials in boxes.
- Improve product delivery time.
- Save human resources costs through automation.
- Do not change the customer experience.

---

## Important Lesson

Business requirements describe what the solution must satisfy to solve the problem successfully.

---

# 7. Details of the Proposed Solution

## What It Is

This section explains how the proposed solution will address the business need and intended outcomes.

It may include:

- process changes
- new workflows
- supporting visual models
- prototypes
- supplier changes
- system changes
- operational changes

---

## EcoMart Example Proposed Solution

EcoMart will reduce shipping costs by 5% by:

- reducing the variety of box sizes from **12 sizes to 4 sizes**
- changing box suppliers from **BoxedUp Co.** to **GreenBox Co.**

---

## Why This Section Matters

A project cannot move forward without a clear explanation of how the solution will solve the problem.

### Important Lesson

The solution section connects the requirements to practical action.

---

# 8. Schedule

## What It Is

The **schedule** outlines the planned timeline of the project.

It should include:

- project phases
- deadlines
- expected completion dates
- important milestones

---

## Why Schedule Matters

Clear deadlines help stakeholders understand project progress.

Unclear deadlines can create confusion about whether the project is on track.

Deadlines should be:

- transparent
- realistic
- clearly communicated

Unrealistic deadlines can cause delays and make communication with stakeholders more difficult.

---

## EcoMart Example Schedule

### Phase 1: Analysis of Shipping Supply Chain Complete

- Deadline: End of first quarter
- Analysis period: January 15 to March 31

### Phase 2: Analysis of Internal HQ Supply Processes Complete

- Deadline: April 15
- Analysis period: April 1 to April 15

### Phase 3: Overall Analysis Complete and Initial Findings Meeting With Stakeholders

- Deadline: End of second quarter
- Period: May 1 to June 30

### Phase 4: Feedback Integration and Final Analysis

- Deadline: Start of fourth quarter
- Date: October 1

---

# 9. Constraints

## What They Are

**Constraints** are limitations that may affect the project.

Common constraints include:

- budget
- time
- resource availability
- staffing
- technical limits
- supplier limits
- legal or compliance requirements
- business rules

---

## Why Constraints Matter

Constraints help stakeholders understand what may limit the project or affect possible solutions.

They also help prevent unrealistic expectations.

---

## EcoMart Example Constraints

EcoMart’s constraints could include:

- All changes must be made before the end of the final quarter of the year.
- The cost of prototyping new materials cannot exceed the agreed-upon budget.
- Specific marketing messages must be printed on the outside of the box, which limits possible suppliers and box materials.
- After initial brainstorming and scoping, only one employee will have capacity to act as liaison for the business analysis team.
- Because of limited liaison availability, the analysis team will not have access to stakeholders whenever needed.

---

# Why Constraints Are Important

Constraints can directly affect:

- schedule
- budget
- solution design
- available options
- stakeholder access
- supplier selection
- final recommendations

### Important Lesson

A good BRD makes limitations visible before they become problems.

---

# General Purpose of a BRD

A BRD should clearly present:

- the larger vision of the project
- the business need
- the requirements being addressed
- the proposed solution
- the project scope
- the success measurements
- the schedule
- the constraints

---

# Big Lesson

A BRD is not just a formality.

It is a project guide that helps stakeholders understand the project, make decisions, and stay aligned throughout the analysis process.

---

# Key Takeaways

- A BRD provides transparency into the analysis process.
- A BRD explains the rationale and proposed roadmap for solving a business need.
- Different companies may use different BRD templates.
- Most BRDs include a project summary, needs statement, measurable outcomes, scope, stakeholders, business requirements, proposed solution, schedule, and constraints.
- The project summary gives a quick overview of the project vision and goals.
- The needs statement explains why the project is necessary.
- Measurable outcomes define how success will be evaluated.
- Project scope defines what is included and excluded.
- Stakeholders are the people or groups involved in or affected by the project.
- Business requirements describe what the solution must meet.
- The proposed solution explains how the business need will be addressed.
- The schedule shows deadlines and project phases.
- Constraints explain limitations that may affect the project.
- A BRD helps keep the project focused, realistic, and aligned with business goals.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- A BRD should be written with the audience in mind.
- The BRD should be clear enough for stakeholders to understand.
- The needs statement may change as more requirements are discovered.
- Project scope is important because it protects the project from scope creep.
- KPIs help measure success using data.
- Constraints should be documented early so stakeholders understand limits.
- A good BRD connects business needs, requirements, solutions, and measurable outcomes.

---

# Final Summary

A Business Requirements Document, or BRD, is a formal document that explains the project’s business need, requirements, scope, stakeholders, solution, schedule, measurable outcomes, and constraints. In the EcoMart example, the company wants to reduce shipping costs by improving packaging supplies and reviewing shipping workflows. The BRD explains why the project is needed, how success will be measured, what work is in scope, who the stakeholders are, what requirements must be met, what solution is proposed, what timeline will be followed, and what constraints may limit the project. The main lesson is that a strong BRD gives stakeholders a clear, shared understanding of the project and helps keep the analysis focused, measurable, and aligned with business goals.
