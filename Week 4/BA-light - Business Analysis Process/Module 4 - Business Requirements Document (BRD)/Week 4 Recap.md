# Key Takeaways: BRD, Implementation, and Evaluation

## Main Topic

This section summarizes the final parts of the business analysis process:

- creating a **Business Requirements Document (BRD)**
- understanding project scope
- avoiding scope creep
- communicating insights
- supporting solution implementation
- evaluating the value created by the solution

---

# What Is the Purpose of a Business Requirements Document?

A **Business Requirements Document**, or **BRD**, is a formal document that records the business requirements for a project.

It is shared with stakeholders to explain:

- the scope of the analysis
- the business problem
- the project requirements
- the proposed solution
- the expected outcomes
- how success will be measured

---

## Why a BRD Is Important

A BRD helps keep the analysis project organized and on track.

It acts as a **touchstone**, meaning stakeholders can return to it when making decisions about:

- project priorities
- timelines
- project management
- business goals
- requirements
- scope

### Important Lesson

The BRD helps make sure the project continues to align with business objectives and stakeholder needs.

---

# Benefits of a Business Requirements Document

A strong BRD provides several benefits.

## 1. Clear Communication

The BRD helps everyone understand what the project is about and what needs to be done.

It reduces confusion between stakeholders, analysts, project managers, and implementation teams.

---

## 2. Alignment With Business Goals

The BRD connects the project to the company’s larger business objectives.

It helps make sure the project does not become disconnected from the original business need.

---

## 3. Scope Definition

The BRD clearly defines what work is included and what work is excluded.

This helps prevent scope creep.

---

## 4. Risk Management

The BRD can identify constraints, risks, and limitations early.

This helps the team prepare for possible problems before they happen.

---

## 5. Project Documentation

The BRD serves as an official record of:

- requirements
- project scope
- decisions
- goals
- expected outcomes

It can be used throughout the project lifecycle as a reference.

---

# Components of a Business Requirements Document

Most BRDs include the following common sections.

---

## 1. Overall Project Summary

The **overall project summary** is a concise overview of the project.

It usually includes:

- project vision
- goals
- business context
- project purpose
- high-level scope

### Purpose

This section helps stakeholders quickly understand the project without reading the entire document.

---

## 2. Needs Statement

The **needs statement** clearly explains the business need or problem the project is trying to solve.

It answers:

- Why is this project needed?
- What problem exists?
- What gap needs to be addressed?
- Why should stakeholders care?

### Important Lesson

The needs statement explains the reason for the project.

---

## 3. Measurable Outcomes

**Measurable outcomes** are the KPIs or metrics used to define project success.

They should be:

- specific
- measurable
- connected to business goals
- easy to track

### Example

A measurable outcome could be:

- reduce shipping costs by 5%
- decrease abandoned carts by 25%
- reduce page load time to under 2 seconds

---

## 4. Project Scope

**Project scope** defines the boundaries of the project.

It explains:

- what is included
- what is not included
- what work the team will complete
- what work is outside the project

### Important Lesson

Clear scope prevents the project from growing too large or moving away from the original goal.

---

## 5. Stakeholders

The **stakeholders** section lists the people, groups, or organizations involved in or affected by the project.

This may include:

- decision-makers
- project team members
- business users
- customers
- managers
- external partners

The section may also explain each stakeholder’s role, interest, or influence.

---

## 6. Business Requirements

**Business requirements** are detailed statements of what the project must achieve to meet the business need.

They may include:

- functional requirements
- non-functional requirements
- operational requirements
- technical requirements
- transitional requirements

### Important Lesson

Business requirements explain what the solution must do or satisfy.

---

## 7. Proposed Solution

The **proposed solution** describes the approach or method recommended to solve the business problem.

This may include:

- process changes
- technology implementation
- workflow updates
- system changes
- action plans

### Important Lesson

The proposed solution should connect directly to the business requirements and desired outcomes.

---

## 8. Schedule

The **schedule** explains the project timeline.

It includes:

- phases
- deadlines
- milestones
- expected completion dates

### Why It Matters

A clear schedule helps stakeholders understand project progress and expectations.

---

## 9. Constraints

**Constraints** are limitations the project must work within.

Examples include:

- budget limits
- time limits
- limited staff
- resource availability
- technical limits
- supplier limits
- legal or regulatory restrictions

### Important Lesson

Constraints help stakeholders understand what may limit the project or affect the solution.

---

# Best Practices for Writing a BRD

A good BRD should be clear, specific, and actionable.

---

## 1. Include Specific Requirements

Requirements should be clear and measurable.

### Weak Requirement

Improve the user experience.

### Better Requirement

Reduce the average page load time for the e-commerce website to less than 2 seconds.

---

## 2. Center the Solution Around the Customer’s Problem

The solution should stay focused on the original customer or business problem.

Do not let the project drift into unnecessary features or unrelated improvements.

---

## 3. Keep Requirements Updated

Requirements can change during a project.

The BRD should be updated when stakeholder needs or project conditions change.

### Important Lesson

An outdated BRD can cause the team to work from incorrect information.

---

## 4. Leave Space for Creativity

The BRD should explain the problem, requirements, and desired outcomes.

It should not over-design the solution too early.

This gives the project team room to create the best possible solution.

---

## 5. Use Affirmative Statements

Use clear, positive language.

### Better

The system will notify customers when their order ships.

### Avoid

The system will not leave customers uninformed.

---

# Project Scope

## Definition

**Scope** is the work that the Business Analyst and stakeholders decide is part of a project.

Scope defines the project boundaries.

---

## In Scope

**In scope** means the activity is part of the project.

It supports the project goal and is included in the agreed work.

---

## Out of Scope

**Out of scope** means the activity is not part of the project.

It was not planned as part of the project and should not be completed unless the scope is officially changed.

---

# Scope Creep

## Definition

**Scope creep** happens when the work or requirements of a project increase or change over time.

This can happen when new ideas, requests, or opportunities are added without proper control.

---

## Why Scope Creep Is a Problem

Scope creep can cause:

- higher costs
- missed deadlines
- unfinished work
- confused stakeholders
- project failure

### Important Lesson

Clear scope helps keep the project focused and manageable.

---

# Communicating Insights

## What Are Insights?

**Insights** are actionable trends and patterns found through analysis.

They are more than statistics.

A statistic tells what happened.  
An insight explains what the data means and what action may be needed.

---

## Example

### Statistic

The conversion rate dropped from 4.5% to 3.4%.

### Insight

The checkout process may be causing more visitors to leave before purchasing, so the checkout experience should be reviewed.

---

# Storytelling in Communication

Storytelling helps stakeholders remember and understand information.

Data is important, but stories make insights more meaningful and memorable.

### Important Lesson

A Business Analyst must be able to turn analysis into a clear story that stakeholders can understand and act on.

---

# Solution Implementation Stage

## What Happens During Implementation?

After the BRD is completed and shared with stakeholders, the chosen solution moves into implementation.

This is the stage where the solution is brought to life.

---

## Role of the Project Manager

Implementation is often handed off to the **Project Manager**.

The Project Manager usually oversees:

- project execution
- task assignments
- deadlines
- team coordination
- budget use
- resource allocation
- progress monitoring

---

## Role of the Business Analyst

The Business Analyst may still support implementation by:

- clarifying requirements
- explaining the BRD
- answering stakeholder questions
- helping ensure the solution matches the business needs

---

# Project Plan

A **project plan** explains how the project will be executed, monitored, and controlled.

It is informed by the BRD.

A project plan may include:

- scope
- objectives
- deliverables
- timeline
- budget
- resources
- risks
- quality criteria

---

# Solution Iteration

## Definition

**Solution iteration** is the process of refining and improving a solution through repeated cycles of implementation and evaluation.

---

## Why Iteration Matters

The first version of a solution may not fully solve the problem.

Changes may be needed because of:

- stakeholder feedback
- evaluation results
- changing requirements
- technical issues
- new business needs

### Important Lesson

Iteration is not failure. It is part of improving the solution until it creates the intended value.

---

# Evaluating the Value of the Solution

After implementation, the team must evaluate whether the solution worked.

This is done by tracking metrics connected to the success criteria.

---

## Example

If the goal was to reduce abandoned shopping carts, the team should track:

- abandoned cart rate
- conversion rate
- average order value
- gross profit margin
- website speed

---

# Dashboard

## Definition

A **dashboard** is a data visualization tool that displays important KPIs and business metrics on one screen.

Dashboards help stakeholders quickly understand whether the solution is working.

---

## Why Dashboards Are Useful

Dashboards can help track:

- project success metrics
- business KPIs
- supporting metrics
- trends over time
- progress toward goals

They can be used in presentations or shared with stakeholders for review.

---

# Big Lesson

A BRD helps turn analysis into a clear, organized project guide.

It defines the business need, scope, requirements, solution, schedule, constraints, and success measures.

After the BRD is complete, the project moves into implementation, where the solution is executed and later evaluated using metrics.

---

# Final Summary

The Business Requirements Document is the final product of gathering and documenting business requirements. It helps stakeholders understand the project scope, requirements, goals, proposed solution, schedule, constraints, and success measures. A strong BRD keeps the project organized, prevents scope creep, and helps stakeholders make decisions about priorities and timelines. After the BRD is completed, the project moves into implementation, usually led by the project manager. The solution may then go through iterations based on feedback and evaluation. Finally, the value of the solution is measured using success criteria, KPIs, supporting metrics, and dashboards. The main lesson is that business analysis does not end with identifying requirements; it continues through implementation, iteration, and value evaluation.
