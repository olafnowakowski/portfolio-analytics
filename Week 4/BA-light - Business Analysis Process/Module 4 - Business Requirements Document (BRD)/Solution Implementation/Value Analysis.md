# Evaluating the Value Created by a Solution

## Main Idea

The final step in the **business analysis process framework** is to evaluate the value created by the solution.

After a solution is implemented, the work is not automatically finished. The team needs to check whether the solution actually solved the business problem and created measurable value.

---

# Why Evaluation Matters

Once implementation is complete, it may feel like the team can move on to the next project.

However, the solution must be assessed over time to see whether it achieved the intended results.

A solution should have a clear impact on important business metrics.

### Important Lesson

A solution is only successful if it creates measurable improvement connected to the business problem.

---

# Long-Term Outcomes

Evaluation should focus on long-term outcomes, not only whether the solution was launched.

You need to ask:

- Did the solution solve the problem?
- Did the solution improve the target metric?
- Did the solution meet the success criteria?
- Did any new issues appear?
- Are further changes needed?

---

# Example: E-Commerce Checkout Process

## Scenario

You are a Business Analyst at an e-commerce store.

The company has a problem with abandoned shopping carts.

A solution is implemented to reduce cart abandonment by shortening the checkout process.

## Original Checkout Process

- 4 pages

## New Checkout Process

- 2 pages

---

# Why the Solution Needs Evaluation

The hypothesis is:

**If the checkout process has fewer pages, fewer customers will abandon their carts.**

This sounds logical, but it still needs to be tested with data.

The company should track whether the abandoned shopping cart rate actually decreases over time.

### Important Lesson

A logical idea is not enough. The team must validate the solution using data.

---

# Metrics to Track

The metrics tracked during evaluation are usually based on the **success criteria** defined earlier in the project.

Supporting metrics may also be tracked to provide extra insight.

---

# Success Criteria

## Example Success Criteria

**Decrease the abandoned shopping cart rate by 25% over the next quarter.**

This is useful because it is:

- specific
- measurable
- time-bound
- connected to the business problem

---

## How to Use This Metric

To evaluate success, the team should track:

- the abandoned shopping cart rate before the solution
- the abandoned shopping cart rate after the solution
- how the rate changes over time
- whether the rate reaches the target decrease by the end of the quarter

---

# Supporting Metrics

Supporting metrics provide additional information about the solution’s impact.

In the checkout example, supporting metrics may include:

- average order value
- gross profit margin
- website speed
- website conversion rate

---

## Why Supporting Metrics Matter

Supporting metrics can reveal related problems or side effects.

For example:

- If cart abandonment decreases but average order value also drops, the solution may need more review.
- If checkout pages are shorter but website speed is still slow, customers may still abandon carts.
- If conversion rate does not improve, the checkout process may not be the only problem.

### Important Lesson

Supporting metrics help identify future improvements and possible next iterations.

---

# Dashboards

## What Is a Dashboard?

A **dashboard** is a data visualization tool that shows important KPIs and business metrics on one screen.

Dashboards help teams monitor performance over time.

---

## What Dashboards Show

A dashboard may show:

- KPIs
- success criteria
- supporting metrics
- trends over time
- performance comparisons
- progress toward goals

---

# Why Dashboards Are Useful

Dashboards are useful because they allow stakeholders to quickly see whether the solution is working.

They can be used to:

- track metrics affected by the solution
- monitor business performance
- identify problems early
- support stakeholder decisions
- communicate progress clearly

---

# Dashboards in Presentations

Dashboards can be used in different ways.

They can be:

- shown during a presentation
- shared with stakeholders for individual review
- used by teams to monitor implementation results
- updated regularly to track ongoing performance

---

# Dashboard Example

The lesson mentions a dashboard that tracks KPIs for a pediatric center at a hospital.

This example shows how dashboards can display important metrics in one place so stakeholders can understand performance quickly.

---

# Dashboards and Solution Implementation

Dashboards are especially useful after a solution is implemented.

They help track whether the solution is producing the expected results.

For example, after changing the checkout process, a dashboard could show:

- abandoned cart rate
- conversion rate
- average order value
- website speed
- gross profit margin

This makes it easier to decide whether the solution worked or whether more iteration is needed.

---

# Evaluation and Iteration

If the solution does not meet the success criteria, the team may need to iterate.

This means the team may:

- review the data
- identify what is not working
- gather more feedback
- adjust the solution
- test again
- continue improving

### Important Lesson

Evaluation helps determine whether the solution is complete or whether more changes are needed.

---

# Big Lesson

The final step of business analysis is not just implementing the solution.

The final step is proving whether the solution created value.

This is done by tracking success criteria, KPIs, and supporting metrics over time.

---

# Key Takeaways

- The last step in the business analysis process framework is evaluating the value created by the solution.
- Implementation does not mean the work is fully complete.
- The solution should be evaluated using business metrics.
- Success criteria defined earlier in the project are usually the main metrics to track.
- Supporting metrics can provide additional insight.
- In the e-commerce example, the success criteria is reducing abandoned shopping cart rate by 25% over the next quarter.
- Supporting metrics may include average order value, gross profit margin, website speed, and conversion rate.
- Dashboards are useful tools for tracking KPIs and business metrics in one place.
- Dashboards can be used in presentations or shared with stakeholders.
- If the solution does not meet the success criteria, further iteration may be needed.
- Evaluation helps confirm whether the solution actually solved the business problem.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- A solution should be connected to measurable business impact.
- Data should be tracked over time, not just immediately after launch.
- The success criteria should be specific and time-bound.
- Supporting metrics can reveal problems that the main success metric does not show.
- Dashboards make it easier for stakeholders to understand progress.
- Evaluation protects the business from assuming a solution worked without evidence.
- The goal is to make sure the solution creates real value, not just that it was implemented.

---

# Final Summary

The final step in the business analysis process framework is evaluating the value created by the implemented solution. After implementation, the team must track whether the solution achieved the intended business outcome. In the e-commerce checkout example, the solution shortened checkout from four pages to two pages to reduce abandoned carts. Even though the idea makes sense, the team must validate it with data by tracking whether abandoned cart rate decreases by 25% over the next quarter. Supporting metrics such as average order value, gross profit margin, website speed, and conversion rate can provide additional insight. Dashboards are useful tools for tracking these KPIs and sharing progress with stakeholders. The main lesson is that a solution is only successful if it creates measurable value aligned with the project’s success criteria.
