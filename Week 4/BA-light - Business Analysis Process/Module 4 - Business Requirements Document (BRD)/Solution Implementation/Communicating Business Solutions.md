# Communicating Solutions Through Storytelling

## Main Idea

After completing analysis, a Business Analyst must communicate the solution clearly to stakeholders.

It is not enough to only present data, numbers, or statistics.  
You need to explain the findings in a way that stakeholders can understand, remember, and act on.

One powerful way to do this is through **storytelling**.

---

# Why Storytelling Matters

Stanford professor **Chip Heath** studied students watching presentations.

After 10 minutes:

- only **5%** remembered a statistic
- **63%** remembered a story

This shows that people are much more likely to remember information when it is presented as a story.

---

# Storytelling and Presentations

Stories are powerful because they make information easier to understand and remember.

Even presentations that contain important numbers can become more effective when the data is placed inside a story.

For example:

- numbers help prove the point
- stories help people remember the point

### Important Lesson

Data validates your message, but storytelling makes your message memorable.

---

# Storytelling in TED Talks

The lesson mentions that around **65% of the material in TED Talks is made up of stories**.

This shows that strong communicators often use stories to make ideas more engaging and memorable.

---

# Why Data Alone Is Not Enough

Business Analysts often go deep into detailed analysis.

They may work with:

- numbers
- requirements
- charts
- trends
- stakeholder feedback
- process details
- business rules

But stakeholders usually do not have all the same background context.

So the analyst must “come up for air” and explain the findings in a clear story.

### Important Lesson

After doing deep analysis, you need to translate your findings into a message stakeholders can understand.

---

# What Is Data Storytelling?

**Data storytelling** means turning data, numbers, and analysis into a clear and meaningful story.

It helps explain:

- what was found
- why it matters
- what action should be taken
- how the findings connect to business goals

---

# Crafting Your Message

When preparing to communicate a solution, focus on four main points:

1. **Identify insights**
2. **Identify the medium**
3. **Determine technical depth**
4. **Determine overall depth**

---

# 1. Identifying Insights

## What Are Insights?

**Insights** are actionable trends and patterns found through analysis.

They go beyond simple statistics.

A statistic reports a number.  
An insight explains what the number means and how the business can use it.

---

## Statistic vs Insight

### Statistic

“The conversion rate dropped from 4.5% to 3.4%.”

### Insight

“The lower conversion rate suggests that more visitors are leaving before buying, so the checkout process should be investigated.”

---

## Why Insights Matter

The goal of analysis is not just to report numbers.

The goal is to discover patterns and trends that help the business take informed action.

### Important Lesson

An insight should help the business decide what to do next.

---

## How to Summarize an Insight

Before presenting your findings, summarize the insight in one clear sentence or a few short sentences.

This helps make your message focused.

### Example

“Customers are abandoning the checkout process because the new payment gateway has increased payment processing time.”

This is clearer than simply showing several charts.

---

# 2. Identifying the Medium

## What Is a Medium?

The **medium** is the format used to communicate your insights.

Common mediums include:

- live presentations
- written emails
- spreadsheets
- slide decks
- data visualizations

---

## Common Ways Analysts Share Insights

Business Analysts often share insights through:

- data visualizations
- slide decks
- emails

---

## Choosing the Right Medium

The best medium depends on:

- audience size
- importance of the message
- level of detail needed
- whether the communication is formal or informal
- whether stakeholders need to discuss the findings
- stakeholder power and interest

---

## Working With Your Manager

Usually, the analyst and manager decide the best medium before communicating to stakeholders.

This is especially important when presenting to stakeholders with high power and high interest in the project.

### Important Lesson

A Business Analyst should be prepared to communicate through different formats.

---

# 3. Determining Technical Depth

## What Technical Depth Means

**Technical depth** means how much technical or analytical detail you include in your message.

You need to think about how comfortable your audience is with data and analytics.

---

## Questions to Ask

Before presenting, ask:

- Is my audience data-savvy?
- Will they understand the analytical methods?
- Do they need to know how I validated the requirements?
- Will technical details help or confuse them?

---

## If the Audience Is Not Data-Savvy

If the audience is not comfortable with analytics, do not spend too much time explaining technical analysis steps.

Instead, focus on:

- insights
- project goals
- business impact
- recommendations

---

## If the Audience Is Data-Savvy

If the audience understands analytics, you may include more detail about:

- methods
- validation steps
- assumptions
- calculations
- data sources
- limitations

### Important Lesson

Adjust the technical detail based on your audience.

---

# 4. Determining Overall Depth

## What Overall Depth Means

**Overall depth** means how much extra context you include beyond the main insight.

This may include:

- background information
- why the analysis was done
- how the results were developed
- what problem started the analysis
- what stakeholders need to know before deciding

---

## Simple Question to Ask

After sharing the insight, ask yourself:

**What else does this stakeholder need to know?**

That answer tells you the level of overall depth needed.

---

## Why Overall Depth Matters

The amount of detail needed depends on the importance of the business decision.

Bigger decisions usually require more explanation and more context.

### Important Lesson

Different audiences need different levels of detail.

---

# Delivering Your Message

After crafting your message, you need to deliver it effectively.

Important delivery tips include:

1. Connect with your audience.
2. Stick to the facts.
3. Use easy-to-understand language.

---

# 1. Connect With Your Audience

## Know Your Audience

Before presenting, understand who your audience is.

Ask:

- Who are they?
- What do they value?
- What do they care about?
- What decisions do they need to make?
- What background knowledge do they already have?

---

## Build Connection

You can connect with your audience by:

- researching what matters to them
- highlighting shared goals
- finding common ground
- using examples they care about
- focusing on topics relevant to their work

### Important Lesson

When the audience feels the message is relevant to them, they are more likely to listen and act.

---

# 2. Stick to the Facts

## Avoid Assumptions and Judgments

When communicating data, only say what the data supports.

Do not add assumptions unless you clearly explain they are assumptions.

---

## Good Example

“There were fewer users on the weekend.”

This is fact-based.

## Bad Example

“People don’t like coming to the site on the weekend.”

This is an assumption.

The data may show fewer weekend users, but it does not prove why they came less often.

---

## Important Lesson

Data communication should be accurate, objective, and free from unsupported judgments.

---

# 3. Use Easy-to-Understand Language

## Keep Language Clear and Concise

Use simple wording that stakeholders can understand quickly.

Avoid unnecessary complexity.

---

## Example

Better:

“There were approximately twice as many clicks on the second campaign.”

More technical:

“There were 103% more clicks on the second campaign.”

Both may be accurate, but the first is easier to understand.

---

## Be Careful With Rounding or Simplifying

Do not exaggerate.

For example:

- if the increase was 103%, saying “twice as many” is reasonable
- if the increase was 70%, saying “twice as many” is inaccurate

When unsure, use the actual values.

### Important Lesson

Simple language is good, but accuracy must always come first.

---

# Why Communication Is Essential for Business Analysts

Business Analysts often need to share findings with stakeholders.

Strong communication helps stakeholders:

- understand the analysis
- remember the findings
- trust the recommendations
- make informed decisions
- take action

---

# Big Lesson

The goal is not just to present information.

The goal is to communicate insights clearly enough that stakeholders understand what matters and what action should happen next.

# Key Takeaways

- Storytelling makes information more memorable.
- People often remember stories better than statistics.
- Data is important, but stories help explain why the data matters.
- Business Analysts must translate detailed analysis into clear stakeholder-friendly messages.
- Insights are actionable trends and patterns found through analysis.
- Insights should go beyond reporting statistics.
- Choose the communication medium based on audience, context, and level of detail needed.
- Common mediums include presentations, slide decks, emails, spreadsheets, and data visualizations.
- Technical depth depends on how comfortable the audience is with analytics.
- Overall depth depends on how much context stakeholders need.
- Connect with your audience by understanding what they value.
- Stick to facts and avoid unsupported assumptions.
- Use clear, simple language.
- Do not exaggerate data.
- Strong communication is essential for stakeholder decision-making.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- A good insight should be summarized clearly before presentation.
- Stakeholders usually do not have the same deep context as the analyst.
- The analyst’s job is to make the findings understandable and useful.
- Storytelling can be applied to any topic, not only data.
- Even a simple or boring topic can be improved through storytelling.
- The best communication combines facts, insights, context, and a clear message.
- Always match your message to the audience.

---

# Final Summary

This lesson explains how Business Analysts should communicate solutions effectively. Storytelling is powerful because people remember stories more easily than statistics. While data is necessary to support and validate findings, storytelling helps make insights memorable and meaningful. To craft a strong message, analysts should identify the main insight, choose the right communication medium, decide the correct level of technical detail, and determine how much background context the audience needs. When delivering the message, analysts should connect with their audience, stick to facts, avoid assumptions, and use simple language without exaggerating. The main lesson is that strong communication turns analysis into clear insights that stakeholders can understand, remember, and act on.
