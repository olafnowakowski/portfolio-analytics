# Common BRD Mistakes to Avoid

## Main Idea

A **Business Requirements Document (BRD)** can include all the right sections and still be ineffective if the requirements are unclear, outdated, too restrictive, or not connected to the customer’s real problem.

A good BRD should be:

- specific
- clear
- measurable
- customer-focused
- flexible when requirements change
- focused on outcomes
- free from speculation

---

# Why BRD Mistakes Matter

A BRD guides the project team and stakeholders.

If the BRD is poorly written, the project may suffer from:

- confusion
- unclear expectations
- unnecessary work
- missed requirements
- poor solutions
- scope creep
- delays
- stakeholder dissatisfaction

### Important Lesson

A BRD should help the team understand exactly what needs to happen and how success will be measured.

---

# Common Mistakes When Writing a BRD

The main mistakes discussed are:

1. Lack of specificity when defining requirements
2. Failure to center the solution around the customer’s problem
3. Requirements becoming outdated
4. Limiting the creativity of the solution
5. Making speculative statements

---

# 1. Lack of Specificity When Defining Requirements

## What This Means

Requirements must give the project team clear guidance.

A vague requirement does not explain exactly what needs to happen or how success will be measured.

---

## Why This Is a Problem

If requirements are not specific, the team may:

- misunderstand stakeholder expectations
- do unnecessary extra work
- fail to meet the real requirement
- struggle to know when the work is complete
- create a solution that does not solve the problem

---

## Vague Requirement Example

**Improve the user experience of our e-commerce website.**

This is technically a requirement, but it is not specific enough.

It does not explain:

- what part of the user experience should improve
- how the team should measure improvement
- what success looks like
- where the team should focus

---

## Specific Requirement Example

**Reduce the average page load time for the e-commerce website to less than 2 seconds.**

This is better because it is:

- clear
- measurable
- specific
- actionable

The team now knows exactly what to improve and how to know when the requirement has been met.

---

## Important Lesson

A requirement should be specific enough that the team knows what work to do and how to measure completion.

---

# 2. Failure to Center the Solution Around the Customer’s Problem

## What This Means

A BRD must stay focused on the original customer problem and customer requirements.

Before building the solution, the analyst must gather requirements carefully.

---

## Why This Is a Problem

Once the team starts building a solution, they may become focused on features instead of the original need.

For example, engineers or designers may try to “improve” the solution by adding extra features.

This can cause the team to move away from:

- customer requirements
- project goals
- project scope
- the original business need

---

## How to Avoid This Mistake

Continually return to:

- the original requirements
- the project goals
- the project scope
- the customer’s problem

### Important Lesson

The solution should solve the customer’s problem, not simply become more complex or impressive.

---

# 3. Requirements Becoming Outdated

## What This Means

A BRD takes time to create, but business needs can change during the project.

Stakeholders may update or change their requirements as the project develops.

---

## Why This Is a Problem

If the BRD is not updated, the team may work from old information.

This can lead to:

- outdated solutions
- misalignment with stakeholders
- wasted work
- missed expectations
- project delays

---

## How to Avoid This Mistake

The analyst should continue collecting and reviewing requirements as the project progresses.

Stakeholders should have ways to:

- edit requirements
- clarify requirements
- update requirements
- change requirements when necessary

---

## Important Lesson

A BRD should not be treated as completely frozen if stakeholder needs change.

The purpose of the BRD is to address stakeholder requirements, so if those requirements change, the project may need to adjust.

---

# 4. Limiting the Creativity of the Solution

## What This Means

The BRD should define the problem, requirements, and desired outcomes.

It should not over-design the solution too early.

---

## Why This Is a Problem

If the BRD explains exactly how the problem must be solved, it may limit creativity.

Stakeholders and project teams may feel restricted and may not explore better solutions.

---

## Better Approach

Instead of writing detailed instructions for one specific solution, focus on:

- the problem
- the required outcomes
- the business goals
- the requirements any solution must meet

---

## Example

Instead of saying:

**The team must build a mobile app with a blue dashboard and three buttons.**

A better requirement might say:

**Customers must be able to check their order status from a mobile device.**

This leaves room for the team to find the best solution.

---

## Important Lesson

A BRD should guide the solution, not block better ideas.

---

# 5. Making Speculative Statements

## What This Means

Avoid statements based on hope, guesses, or imagination.

Examples of speculative phrases include:

- We hope that...
- We imagine this solution will...
- This might possibly...
- We believe users may...

---

## Why This Is a Problem

Speculative statements can create unclear or unmeasurable goals.

They may also lead the project outside its original scope.

---

## Better Approach

Use clear, affirmative language.

State what will happen and how success will be measured.

---

## Weak Example

**We hope that customers will enjoy the new checkout process.**

## Better Example

**The checkout process will allow customers to complete a purchase in fewer than three steps.**

---

## Important Lesson

A BRD should be written with clear, measurable, affirmative statements.

---

# Summary Table of BRD Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Is a Problem | How to Avoid It |
|---|---|---|
| Lack of specificity | The team does not know exactly what to do or how to measure success | Write clear, measurable requirements |
| Ignoring the customer’s problem | The solution may drift away from the real need | Keep returning to requirements, goals, and scope |
| Outdated requirements | The team may work from old information | Keep collecting and updating requirements as needed |
| Limiting creativity | The team may miss better solutions | Focus on outcomes, not overly detailed solution design |
| Speculative statements | Goals become vague or unmeasurable | Use clear and affirmative language |

---

# Big Lesson

A BRD should clearly define what the project needs to achieve without being vague, outdated, speculative, or overly restrictive.

The document should guide the project team while still allowing enough flexibility to create the best solution.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Requirements must be specific and measurable.
- Vague requirements make it difficult for the project team to know what to do.
- A BRD should stay centered on the customer’s original problem.
- Teams should avoid adding unnecessary improvements that move away from the project scope.
- Requirements can change, so the BRD may need to be updated during the project.
- A BRD should not limit creativity by designing the solution too early.
- The BRD should focus on desired outcomes and requirements.
- Avoid speculative statements such as “We hope that...” or “We imagine...”
- Use clear, affirmative language.
- A good BRD helps stakeholders understand the project and keeps the team aligned.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- A BRD should support action, not confusion.
- Specific requirements help prevent scope creep.
- Customer requirements should remain central throughout the project.
- The analyst should regularly check whether the project still matches the original goals.
- If stakeholder needs change, the analyst should document and communicate those changes.
- Requirements should explain what success looks like.
- Strong BRDs balance structure with flexibility.

---

# Final Summary

This lesson explains common mistakes to avoid when writing a Business Requirements Document. A BRD can be ineffective if requirements are vague, if the solution drifts away from the customer’s problem, if requirements become outdated, if the document limits creativity, or if it includes speculative statements. Strong requirements should be specific, measurable, customer-focused, and written in clear affirmative language. The BRD should focus on the problem, the desired outcome, and the requirements any solution must meet, while still allowing the project team enough flexibility to create the best solution. The main lesson is that an effective BRD keeps the project focused, clear, current, and aligned with stakeholder needs.
