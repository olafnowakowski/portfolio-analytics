# BRD Writing Best Practices

## Main Idea

A **Business Requirements Document (BRD)** should be clear, concise, and actionable.

Even if different companies use different BRD templates, there are common best practices that help make the document easier to understand and more useful for stakeholders.

---

# Why BRD Writing Quality Matters

A BRD is used by stakeholders, analysts, project managers, and teams to understand the project requirements.

If the BRD is unclear, it can cause:

- confusion
- misunderstandings
- delays
- poor decisions
- misaligned expectations
- project scope problems

### Important Lesson

A BRD should help people understand the project, not make the project harder to follow.

---

# Best Practices for Writing a BRD

## 1. List Requirements in Full Sentences

Requirements should be written as complete sentences.

Avoid using short, incomplete phrases because they can be unclear.

### Weak Example

- Faster checkout

### Better Example

- The checkout process must allow customers to complete a purchase in fewer than three steps.

### Why This Matters

Full sentences make requirements easier to understand and reduce confusion.

---

# 2. Avoid Jargon and Buzzwords

A BRD should use clear and simple language.

Avoid jargon, buzzwords, or trendy business words that may confuse readers or weaken the meaning.

## Examples of Jargon and Buzzwords to Avoid

- bandwidth
- deep dive
- leverage
- innovative
- ecosystem
- scalable
- value-add
- optimize
- game-changer
- disruptor
- user-friendly
- re-imagine

### Why This Matters

Stakeholders may come from different backgrounds. Simple language helps everyone understand the same message.

### Important Lesson

Use accessible language that a non-technical reader can understand.

---

# 3. List One Requirement at a Time

Each requirement should be listed separately.

Do not combine multiple requirements into one statement.

### Why This Matters

When several requirements are grouped together, it becomes harder to:

- track them
- measure them
- assign ownership
- test them
- connect them to outcomes
- confirm whether they were completed

---

## Warning Signs

If a requirement uses words like:

- and
- or
- in addition
- also

then it may contain more than one requirement and should probably be split.

### Weak Example

- The system must process orders quickly and send confirmation emails.

### Better Example

- The system must process customer orders within two minutes.
- The system must send customers a confirmation email after an order is completed.

---

# 4. Focus on the Outcome of the Solution

The BRD should focus on what the solution needs to achieve, not every detail of how the solution must be designed.

A proposed solution can be included, but the requirements should not become too detailed or restrictive.

---

## Why This Matters

If the BRD tries to design the full solution too early, it can limit creativity.

The team may miss better options because the document already forces one specific approach.

### Better Focus

Instead of describing exactly how the solution should be built, define:

- what outcome is needed
- what requirement must be met
- what success should look like

---

# 5. Use Affirming Language

Use positive and clear wording.

Write what the system or solution **will do**, rather than focusing only on what it will not do.

### Better Wording

- The system will notify customers when their order ships.

### Weaker Wording

- The system will not leave customers uninformed about shipping.

---

## Why This Matters

Affirming language keeps the BRD focused on desired outcomes.

It makes the document clearer and easier to act on.

---

# 6. Include Visuals

A BRD that is only text-heavy can be difficult to read.

Visuals help readers mentally organize the project information.

Useful visuals may include:

- process maps
- flowcharts
- swimlane diagrams
- business domain models
- tables
- charts
- diagrams

---

### Important Lesson

Visuals can make complex requirements easier to understand than long text blocks.

---

# 7. Align Requirements and Outcomes

Requirements and outcomes must match each other.

Each requirement should connect clearly to a desired outcome or success indicator.

---

## Why This Matters

If requirements and outcomes are not aligned, the project may become confusing or contradictory.

The team needs to understand:

- which requirement supports which outcome
- how success will be measured
- whether the requirement helps meet the project goal

---

## What to Check

When reviewing a BRD, ask:

- Does each requirement support a project outcome?
- Are any requirements contradictory?
- Are the success indicators clear?
- Can each outcome be measured?
- Do the requirements match the business goals?

---

# Big Lesson

A strong BRD should be easy to read, specific, measurable, and focused on business outcomes.

The goal is to create documentation that helps the project team and stakeholders understand what needs to happen and why.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Write requirements in complete sentences.
- Avoid jargon and buzzwords.
- Use simple, accessible language.
- List one requirement at a time.
- Split requirements when they contain multiple ideas.
- Focus on the outcome, not overly detailed solution design.
- Use affirming language that explains what will happen.
- Include visuals to make complex information easier to understand.
- Align requirements with outcomes and success indicators.
- Make sure requirements are not contradictory.
- A clear BRD helps stakeholders understand, evaluate, and act on the project requirements.

---

# Extra Important Notes

- A BRD should be written for the audience, not only for the analyst.
- Clear writing reduces the risk of misinterpretation.
- Each requirement should be easy to trace to a business goal or outcome.
- Avoid vague words because they make requirements harder to test.
- Visuals are helpful when explaining processes, roles, systems, or workflows.
- Good BRD writing supports better decision-making and project alignment.

---

# Final Summary

This lesson explains best practices for writing an effective Business Requirements Document. A strong BRD should use full sentences, avoid jargon, list one requirement at a time, focus on outcomes, use affirming language, include visuals, and align requirements with measurable outcomes. These practices make the document clearer, easier to understand, and more actionable for stakeholders. The main lesson is that a BRD should communicate requirements in a simple, organized, and outcome-focused way so the project team can stay aligned and complete the right work.
