# The Story of a Process: Process Maps and Flowchart Symbols

## Main Idea

A **process map** tells a story.

It does not only show random shapes and arrows. It explains how a process works from beginning to end.

In this lesson, the example process map shows the **internal sales process** for an e-learning company called **Zephyr Learning**.

---

# What the Process Map Shows

The Zephyr Learning process map has two major areas:

1. **External**
   - Activities that happen outside the internal company tool.

2. **Internal**
   - Activities that happen inside the company’s internal system or process.

This separation helps show where information comes from and how it moves through the company.

---

# Zephyr Learning Sales Process Story

## 1. A Potential Customer Starts the Process

The process begins when a potential customer, also called a **lead**, fills out an online form.

This is the starting point of the process.

The lead provides contact information through the website.

---

## 2. The System Checks the Customer Database

After the form is submitted, the internal system checks whether the customer already exists in the database.

This is a decision point.

The question is:

**Does the customer already exist in the database?**

---

## 3. If the Customer Already Exists

If the answer is **yes**, the system updates the existing customer ID with the new contact information.

This prevents duplicate customer records.

---

## 4. If the Customer Does Not Exist

If the answer is **no**, a new customer ID is created.

Then the new lead is assigned to a Zephyr Learning sales representative.

---

## 5. Sales Rep Validates Customer Information

The sales representative checks and validates the customer’s information.

This may include confirming:

- contact details
- customer needs
- interest in tutoring services
- any missing or incorrect information

---

## 6. Sales Rep Contacts the Lead

The sales representative reaches out to the lead to determine whether they are interested in purchasing tutoring services.

The sales rep may collect or confirm additional information during the conversation.

---

## 7. Customer Purchase Decision

The next decision is:

**Did the customer make a purchase?**

There are two possible outcomes.

---

## 8. If the Customer Makes a Purchase

If the customer completes a purchase, the lead is labeled as:

**Converted Sale**

This means the potential customer became an actual customer.

---

## 9. If the Customer Does Not Make a Purchase

If the customer does not purchase or is not expected to purchase in the future, the lead is labeled as:

**Non-Converted Sale**

This ends the process.

---

# Why This Process Map Is Useful

This process map helps explain:

- where the process starts
- how customer information enters the company
- how the system handles existing and new customers
- when sales representatives get involved
- where decisions are made
- how leads are classified
- where the process ends

### Important Lesson

A process map makes the sales process easier to understand than a long written explanation.

# Basic Flowchart Symbols

Flowcharts use shapes to communicate different types of process steps.

Common flowchart symbols include:

- ovals
- rectangles
- diamonds
- arrows
- parallelograms
- other nonstandard shapes

Each shape has a specific meaning.

---

# 1. Oval: Start or End

## Meaning

An **oval** is usually used to show the beginning or end of a process.

## Example from Zephyr Learning

The process starts when:

**Potential customer fills out online form**

The process ends when the lead is labeled as either:

- **Converted Sale**
- **Non-Converted Sale**

---

# 2. Diamond: Decision Point

## Meaning

A **diamond** represents a decision that must be made during the process.

The answer usually creates different paths.

Common decision answers include:

- yes
- no

## Example from Zephyr Learning

Decision points include:

**Does customer already exist in database?**

and

**Did customer make purchase?**

Each answer leads to a different next step.

---

# 3. Rectangle: Process Step

## Meaning

A **rectangle** represents an action or process step.

It describes something that happens in the process.

## Example from Zephyr Learning

Examples of process steps include:

- Customer ID updated
- Customer ID assigned new sales rep
- Sales rep validates customer information

---

# 4. Arrows: Process Flow

## Meaning

**Arrows** show the direction of the process.

They tell the reader which step happens next.

Without arrows, it would be difficult to understand the sequence of events.

---

# What Process Flowchart Symbols Communicate

Flowchart symbols can show:

- actions
- activity steps
- decision points
- inputs and outputs
- people involved
- process direction
- process start and end points

### Important Lesson

Flowchart symbols make the process easier to read and understand.

---

# Why a Process Map Tells a Story

A process map tells the story of how work happens.

In the Zephyr Learning example, the story is:

1. A lead enters information online.
2. The company checks whether the customer already exists.
3. The customer record is updated or created.
4. A sales rep is assigned.
5. The sales rep validates the information.
6. The sales rep contacts the lead.
7. The lead either becomes a converted sale or a non-converted sale.

This story helps stakeholders understand the current process.

---

# Big Lesson

A process map is more than a diagram.

It is a visual story of how a process works.

The shapes, arrows, and categories help explain:

- what happens
- when it happens
- who or what is involved
- where decisions occur
- how the process ends
