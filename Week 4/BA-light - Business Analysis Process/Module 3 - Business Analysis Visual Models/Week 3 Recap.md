# Key Takeaways: Business Visual Models

## Main Topic

This lesson summarizes the importance of **business visual models** in business analysis.

Visual models help Business Analysts understand complex systems, business processes, and relationships between different parts of a company.

They make analysis easier to understand for both the analyst and stakeholders.

---

# What Are Business Visual Models?

**Business visual models** are diagrams or visual tools used to organize and explain business information.

They help show:

- how business processes work
- how different parts of the business connect
- where problems may exist
- where improvements may be possible
- how stakeholders and processes relate to each other

---

# Why Visual Models Are Important

Business analysis can get messy, especially when companies have systems and processes that grew over time without much formal analysis.

In these situations, it can be difficult to understand:

- how processes connect
- who is responsible for what
- where delays happen
- where gaps exist
- how one business area affects another

Visual models help make these relationships clear.

### Important Lesson

Visual modeling is valuable because it turns complicated business information into something easier to see, understand, and discuss.

---

# Process Mapping and Process Flow Diagrams

## What They Are

**Process mapping** is the act of creating a visual model of a business process.

A **process flow diagram** is the finished flowchart that shows a specific business process being analyzed.

---

## Why They Are Used

Process maps and process flow diagrams help Business Analysts get a detailed look at the inner workings of a business.

They can help identify:

- process steps
- decision points
- delays
- repeated work
- bottlenecks
- unclear responsibilities
- areas that need deeper analysis

---

## How They Help Stakeholders

Process maps can also help stakeholders understand the bigger context of the analysis.

They can lead to useful questions such as:

- Why does this step happen here?
- Who owns this part of the process?
- Is this handoff necessary?
- Where are delays happening?
- What part of the process should we improve first?

---

# Business Domain Models

## What They Are

A **business domain model** is a high-level visual model that shows different parts of the business and how they connect.

It gives a **bird’s-eye view** of the business.

---

## Why They Are Used

Business domain models help analysts and stakeholders understand:

- major business elements
- relationships between those elements
- how different business areas connect
- which areas may be relevant to the analysis

---

## Difference From Process Maps

A process map shows a specific process step by step.

A business domain model shows high-level relationships between parts of the business.

| Model | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Process Map** | Shows how a specific process works step by step |
| **Business Domain Model** | Shows how different parts of the business connect at a high level |

Visual modeling software is used to create diagrams, process maps, flowcharts, and other business visual models.

These tools help Business Analysts create models more easily and share them with teams and stakeholders.

# Advantages of Visual Modeling Tools

Visual modeling software is useful because it allows analysts to:

- create visual models simply
- drag and drop shapes onto a canvas
- connect shapes easily
- edit models as the analysis changes
- collaborate with team members
- allow multiple people to work on the same model
- collect feedback directly through comments
- share models instantly with stakeholders

---

# Why Choosing the Right Model Matters

Choosing the right visual model can make the analysis more:

- streamlined
- understandable
- accessible
- useful for stakeholders

Most stakeholders may not fully understand how their role connects to the larger web of business processes.

A visual model can quickly show those connections.

### Important Lesson

The best visual model is the one that helps stakeholders understand the process or relationship clearly.

---

# Are Visual Models Used in Every Analysis?

Visual models are not used in every analysis.

However, they are often much better than long blocks of text.

Long text can be difficult for stakeholders to read, understand, and remember.

Visual models provide a clearer and more engaging way to communicate business information.

# Key Terms

## Process Flow Diagram

A **process flow diagram** is a flowchart that details a specific business process being considered for analysis.

It shows the steps, decisions, and flow of the process.

---

## Business Domain Model

A **business domain model** is a visual model that shows different parts of the business and maps their connections and associations.

It gives a high-level, bird’s-eye view of the business.

---

## Process Flowchart

A **process flowchart** is a visual representation of the sequence of steps and decisions needed to perform a process.

It is one of the most common types of process maps.

---

## Swimlane Diagram

A **swimlane diagram**, also called a **cross-functional map**, works similarly to a flowchart.

The main difference is that the steps are divided by the people, teams, or departments responsible for them.

### Why It Is Useful

It clearly shows who is responsible for each step in the process.

---

## Value Stream Map

A **value stream map** is often used in **lean management**.

Lean management focuses on continuous improvement by making small process changes to improve efficiency.

A value stream map analyzes the processes needed to create a product or provide a service.

### Why It Is Useful

It helps identify waste, delays, and inefficiencies in a process.

---

## SIPOC Diagram

A **SIPOC diagram** is a simplified, high-level process map.

SIPOC stands for:

- **Suppliers**
- **Inputs**
- **Processes**
- **Outputs**
- **Customers**

### Why It Is Useful

It focuses on the essential parts of a process.

---

## Classes

In a business domain model, **classes** are the main parts of a business process or business system.

Examples include:

- customer
- order
- product

Classes are usually shown as boxes in a domain model.

---

## Associations

**Associations** describe how each class connects to another class in a business domain model.

Example:

- a customer places an order
- an order contains a product
- the order connects the customer and product
